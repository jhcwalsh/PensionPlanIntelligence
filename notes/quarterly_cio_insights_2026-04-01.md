# Insights: Q2 2026 in Review
*Synthesized from 3 approved monthly briefings (2026-04-01 – 2026-06-30)*
*Generated: July 02, 2026*

---

The quarter was defined by sustained private markets deployment at scale, a sharpening debate over actuarial return assumptions, and a wave of structural portfolio and governance overhauls. Strong fiscal-year returns at most plans masked persistent benchmark gaps in private equity and real estate, while manager roster changes revealed a clear pattern of exiting smart beta and active small-cap mandates. Consultant ownership structures and AI-related governance frameworks emerged as new board-level screens.

## 1. Private Markets Deployment Sustained at Scale, With Private Credit a Notable Exception

Private market commitment activity was the dominant operational theme across all three months of Q2, spanning buyout, growth equity, venture capital, real assets, and infrastructure — though private credit deployment showed signs of constraint by quarter-end.

In April, **KPPA** (~$20B) recommended $90 million to **Capitol Meridian Partners Fund II**, a North American mid-buyout vehicle focused on aerospace, defense, and government-tech sectors, oversubscribed against a $1.2B target and ~$1.7B hard cap (doc_id=3195). **WSIB** (~$189.7B) approved commitments to **Charterhouse Capital Partners XII**, **Spark Capital IX**, **Spark Capital Growth VI**, and **Monarch Capital Partners VII** (doc_id=3206). **NJDPB** (~$95B) built on a $1B January deployment across five vehicles including $100M to **Gridiron Capital Fund VI** and $150M to **Wind Point Partners XI-A** (doc_id=3179).

May broadened the deployment base. **LACERA** (~$75B) committed $100 million to **Dragoneer Select Opportunities Fund, L.P.**, $125 million to **IDR Core Property Trust**, and $200 million to **Principal Enhanced Property Fund, L.P.** (doc_id=4079), alongside a $190 million commitment to **Bregal Sagemount V, L.P.** (doc_id=3474). **LACERS** (~$25B) considered commitments across eleven funds totaling over $335 million (doc_id=4210). **KPPA** approved an additional $100 million to **ITE Rail Fund, L.P.** following full deployment of an initial $100 million commitment by October 2025 (doc_id=4207).

June sustained the pace. **LACERA** approved $200 million to **Net Lease Real Estate Fund II, L.P.**, $200 million to **Court Square Capital V, L.P.**, and approximately $400 million (€341 million) to **Triton Fund 6 SCSp** (doc_id=4504). **CalPERS** (~$502B) maintained private equity pacing at approximately $15.8 billion per year for four consecutive years, with at least 40% of commitments directed to co-investments and separately managed accounts, generating estimated fee savings of approximately $400 million per $1 billion co-invested (doc_id=4789). **Metro Nashville ERS** (~$2B) approved $20 million to **LGT Crown Europe Small Buyouts VII**, $35 million to **Savano Capital Partners IV**, and $40 million to **O'Brien Staley Partners Value Add Fund V** (doc_id=4856).

The exception was private credit deal flow. **IPERS** (~$41B) reported private credit 2026 commitments at $0 of a $450 million plan, with new commitments on hold due to the slow M&A market (doc_id=4588). **MainePERS** (~$18B) noted that Alternative Credit stands significantly below its 15% policy target at 7.9% of fund (doc_id=4006).

## 2. Actuarial Assumption Debate Sharpens: The 7.00% vs. 6.75% Fault Line

The investment return assumption debate intensified through the quarter, evolving from isolated reviews in May to a defining governance fault line by June, with plans reaching opposing conclusions from similar actuarial evidence.

In May, **TRS Texas** (~$200B) unanimously adopted the 2026 Actuarial Experience Study confirming a 7% investment return assumption (doc_id=4151). **ERSRI** (~$10B) considered its 2026 Actuarial Experience Study, with **GRS** recommending no change to the 7.0% assumption; the Teachers funded ratio stands at 67.9% and State Employees at 63.9% (doc_id=4229). **LAFPP** (~$29B) was scheduled to consider reducing its assumption from 7.00% to 6.75%, with a 55% confidence level cited in the actuarial recommendation (doc_id=4134). **MainePERS** adopted actuarial assumptions recommended by **Cheiron** from the PLD Consolidated Plan experience study (doc_id=4008).

By June, the divergence crystallized. **LAFPP** voted 7-0 to maintain its 7.00% assumption despite **Segal**'s recommendation to reduce it to 6.75% (doc_id=4719). **IPERS** also retained its 7.00% return assumption following its actuary **Cavanaugh Macdonald**'s recommendation, noting that **Wilshire**'s 10-year nominal return expectation of 6.2% is below the current assumption but that retention was recommended given longer-horizon estimates and credibility weighting (doc_id=4588). Meanwhile, **LACERS** (~$25B) was scheduled to consider reducing its assumption from 7.00% to 6.75%, a change recommended by **Segal**; adoption would increase the employer contribution rate by approximately 3.70% (~$113.1 million annually on ~$3.1 billion covered payroll) and raise total UAAL by approximately $997.1 million, with the retirement plan funded ratio projected to decline from 105.77% to 100.97% (doc_id=4717).

**VRS** (~$110B) stress testing showed a five-year average return of 1.95% under a Tech Bubble scenario and 0.86% under a Secular Stagflation scenario, against the 6.75% assumed rate of return (doc_id=4553). **DPFP** (~$3B) remains severely underfunded at a 39.08% funded ratio on a market value basis as of January 1, 2026, with projected full funding not until 2053 (doc_id=4060).

*Sources: [TRS Texas — Board Pack — May 01, 2026](?doc=4151), [ERSRI — Board Pack — May 20, 2026](?doc=4229), [LAFPP — Agenda — May 21, 2026](?doc=4134), [MainePERS — Agenda — May 14, 2026](?doc=4008), [LAFPP — Minutes — May 21, 2026](?doc=4719), [IPERS — Board Pack — June 18, 2026](?doc=4588), [LACERS — Agenda — June 23, 2026](?doc=4717), [VRS — Board Pack — June 17, 2026](?doc=4553), [DPFP — Board Pack — May 08, 2026](?doc=4060)*

**Practical implication:** With **LAFPP**, **IPERS**, and **LACERS** all receiving the same directional actuarial advice yet reaching different conclusions, boards should document the specific rationale — horizon weighting, credibility adjustments, or sponsor capacity — that drives assumption retention or reduction, as divergent outcomes from identical consultant recommendations will attract scrutiny.

## 3. Performance Divergence: Strong Headline Returns Mask Private Equity and Real Estate Gaps

Across all three months, performance reports showed robust absolute returns at most plans but persistent benchmark gaps in private equity and real estate.

**TRS Texas** (reported at $235.2 billion as of December 31, 2025) returned 15.9% against a 14.1% benchmark for calendar year 2025, with three-year returns of 11.1% (benchmark: 9.0%) and five-year returns of 7.8% (benchmark: 6.1%) (doc_id=3148). Commodities returned 102.6% and Absolute Return 18.4% versus an 8.7% benchmark for FY2025 (doc_id=3148). Real Estate was the laggard at -3.5% versus a 3.2% benchmark (doc_id=3148). These figures were reiterated in May reporting (doc_id=3998).

**PERS-OR** (~$110B) reported a 1-year return of 8.40% versus a policy benchmark of 13.10% for the period ending March 2026, with Private Equity returning 5.41% against a 24.43% benchmark (doc_id=4081). **IPERS** reported Private Equity returning 10.9% versus a 22.2% benchmark for the one-year period ending March 31, 2026 (doc_id=4588). **VRS** reported a fiscal year-to-date return of 6.8% versus a custom benchmark of 7.8% as of March 31, 2026; the board attributed underperformance primarily to Private Equity selection effects, while Credit Strategies returned 6.9% vs. 2.9% (doc_id=4591).

**LACERA** reported a 1-year net return of 11.8% against a 13.4% policy benchmark and a FYTD return of 6.5% versus a 9.0% benchmark (doc_id=3474). **NYCERS** reported a trailing one-year return of 12.18% versus 13.71%, with Convertible Bonds returning 7.31% versus 25.76% over one year (doc_id=4644). On the positive side, **NYC Teachers' Retirement System** reported a preliminary fiscal year-to-date return of 13.3% as of June 18, 2026, with emerging markets equities returning 49.0% FYTD (doc_id=4825). **MainePERS** reported a preliminary fund value of $22.9 billion as of May 31, 2026 — described as a new monthly high — with a one-year return of 13.2% (doc_id=4466).

The evolution through the quarter was notable: April data showed 2025 real estate as the consistent drag, while May and June data revealed private equity benchmark gaps widening at multiple plans, with PERS-OR and IPERS showing the starkest divergences.

**Practical implication:** The wide spread between private equity benchmark returns and actual returns at PERS-OR and IPERS warrants peer review of valuation lag and benchmark construction methodology before drawing conclusions about manager underperformance.

## 4. Governance Modernization: Consultant Transitions, AI Frameworks, and Policy Overhauls

Governance reform was a cross-cutting theme throughout Q2, with consultant transitions, AI risk frameworks, and structural policy overhauls occurring simultaneously at multiple plans.

Consultant scrutiny was most acute. In April, **TRS Texas** placed the selection of a new Board Investment Consultant — currently **Meketa Investment Group** — on its agenda (doc_id=3145). **NJDPB** began selecting a new Real Estate Investment Consultant (doc_id=3177). In May, **CTPF** (~$18B) voted 9-1 to extend **Callan**'s investment consulting contract for one year and 1-9 against proceeding with **Cerity Partners**, citing concerns about private equity ownership and conflicts of interest; a new RFP was approved 10-1 (doc_id=3973). **SMCERA** (~$5B) engaged **Meketa Investment Group** as new investment consultant with FY2026-27 fees budgeted at $675,000 (doc_id=4157). In June, **SIB-ND** (~$8B) awarded the Performance and Benchmark Consultant contract to **Callan**, replacing **Verus**, which had held a benchmark contract at $133,000/year (doc_id=4459).

On AI and technology governance, **TRS Texas** added a new AI objective (G4.O7) with five strategies to its FY2027–31 Strategic Plan and a new AI risk category to the Enterprise Risk Management Stoplight Report (doc_id=3150). **TRS-OK** (~$19B) held an AI technology portfolio exposure assessment in April (doc_id=3196).

Structural policy changes accelerated in June. **CalPERS** advanced its Total Portfolio Approach set to launch July 1, 2026, replacing the Strategic Asset Allocation with a reference portfolio framework and setting an aggregate active risk limit of 400 basis points and an aggregate leverage limit of 20% (doc_id=4789). **VRS** brought its FY2027 DB Plan Strategic Asset Allocation to the board alongside proposed benchmark changes for Credit Strategies, Diversifying Strategies, Private Equity, and Private Investment Partnerships (doc_id=4591). **SIB-ND** reported internal equity AUM grew from a $60 million pilot to $2.2 billion and internal fixed income AUM grew to approximately $2.1 billion, with the combined internal program generating an estimated $16.3 million in FY2026 savings (doc_id=4459). **WSIB** proposed a core-satellite private credit framework across direct lending (40%), opportunistic credit (40%), distressed debt (10%), and levered strategies (10%) (doc_id=4590).

## 5. Manager Roster Changes: Smart Beta Exits and International Equity Restructuring

Manager hiring and termination activity across Q2 revealed a pattern of exiting smart beta and active small-cap mandates while restructuring international equity allocations.

In April, **WSIB** hired **Life Cycle Investment Partners** for a new Global Select Equity mandate (doc_id=3206). **KPPA** reviewed a **Franklin Templeton** All Country World ex-U.S. public equity mandate and a **ClearBridge Investments** ACWI ex-US International Growth strategy (doc_id=3195). In May, **CCPF** (~$12B) approved **Driehaus Capital Management LLC** and **Causeway Capital Management LLC** for international small cap equity mandates of up to $225 million each, by a 7-2 vote (doc_id=3706). **TRS-OK** placed potential terminations of **Frontier Capital**, **Geneva Capital**, and **Northern Trust** index accounts on its agenda as part of a domestic equity portfolio structure review (doc_id=4172).

June brought the most decisive restructuring. **LAFPP** approved a comprehensive U.S. Equity Structure Implementation Plan terminating **Northern Trust Investments, Inc.** (STOXX USA 900 Min Variance Smart Beta), **AllianceBernstein L.P.** (U.S. Systematic Value Smart Beta), **Channing Capital Management, LLC**, **Westwood Management Corp**, and **Palisade Capital Management, LP**, while extending contracts with **Boston Partners Global Investors, Inc.**, **Denali Advisors, LLC**, **Phocas Financial Corporation**, **Pacific Investment Management Company, LLC**, and **Frontier Capital Management Company, LLC** through December 31, 2029 (doc_id=4719). **DPERS** (~$12B) terminated its **T. Rowe Price** Global Growth account, directing $150 million to a **BlackRock** Russell 3000 Index Fund and $50 million to a **BlackRock** MSCI ACWI ex US Index Fund (doc_id=4663). **BRS** (~$7B) selected **MFS** for the international equity growth mandate, replacing **Walter Scott**, and **Todd** for the value mandate (doc_id=4816).

*Sources: [WSIB — Board Pack — April 16, 2026](?doc=3206), [KPPA — Board Pack — April 28, 2026](?doc=3195), [CCPF — Minutes — April 02, 2026](?doc=3706), [TRS-OK — Agenda — May 20, 2026](?doc=4172), [LAFPP — Minutes — May 21, 2026](?doc=4719), [DPERS — Minutes — April 24, 2026](?doc=4663), [BRS — Minutes — May 27, 2026](?doc=4816)*

**Practical implication:** The exits from smart beta and active small-cap mandates at LAFPP and the active-to-passive reallocation at DPERS point to cost and performance scrutiny of factor-based strategies; plans retaining similar mandates should ensure upcoming contract reviews include explicit performance attribution relative to passive alternatives.

## 6. CIO Transitions and Legislative Disclosure Risk

*Emerging signal — concentrated in May only but with potential Q3 implications.*

**LACERS** is managing a CIO transition following CIO **Rod June**'s announced retirement effective July 2026, with board recruitment pending Mayoral and City Council approval of a civil service exemption re-authorization (doc_id=3971). **ERS-HI** (~$23B) selected a candidate for an interim CIO position effective June 1, 2026, following the departure of CIO **Kristin Varela**, and approved issuing an RFQ for an executive search firm (doc_id=3613).

On the legislative front, **OCERS** (~$25B) adopted an Oppose position on **SB 1319 (Durazo)**, a California bill that would expand CPRA disclosure requirements for public pension funds' alternative investments (doc_id=3972). **CalPERS** also had SB 1319 as an action item on its May 18, 2026 board agenda (doc_id=3953).

*Sources: [LACERS — Agenda — May 12, 2026](?doc=3971), [ERS-HI — Minutes — April 24, 2026](?doc=3613), [OCERS — Agenda — May 18, 2026](?doc=3972), [CalPERS — Board Pack — May 08, 2026](?doc=3953)*

**Practical implication:** California-domiciled plans with significant alternative investment portfolios should monitor SB 1319's legislative progress closely, as expanded CPRA disclosure obligations could affect manager negotiating dynamics and fund access terms.
