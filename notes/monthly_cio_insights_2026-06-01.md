## 2. Actuarial Assumption Divergence: The 7.00% vs. 6.75% Fault Line

The investment return assumption debate was the sharpest governance theme of the month, with plans reaching opposing conclusions from similar actuarial evidence.

**LACERS** (~$25B) was scheduled at its June 23 meeting to consider reducing its assumption from **7.00% to 6.75%**, a change recommended by **Segal** based on a July 2022–June 2025 experience study; adoption would increase the employer contribution rate by approximately **3.70%** (~**$113.1 million** annually on ~**$3.1 billion** covered payroll) and raise total UAAL by approximately **$997.1 million**, with the retirement plan funded ratio projected to decline from **105.77% to 100.97%** (doc_id=4717). **LAFPP** (~$29B) took the opposite path at its May 21 meeting, voting **7-0** to maintain its **7.00%** assumption despite **Segal**'s recommendation to reduce it to **6.75%** (doc_id=4719). **IPERS** (~$41B) also retained its **7.00%** return assumption following its actuary **Cavanaugh Macdonald**'s recommendation, noting that **Wilshire**'s 10-year nominal return expectation of **6.2%** is below the current assumption but that retention was recommended given longer-horizon estimates and credibility weighting (doc_id=4588).

For context on realized returns, **OCERS** (~$25B) reported a smoothed investment return of **8.88%** exceeding its **7.00%** assumption, with its funded ratio improving from **83.83% to 86.55%** on a valuation basis and from **84.18% to 92.71%** on a market value basis; the unfunded actuarial accrued liability decreased from **$4,569.6 million** to **$3,986.4 million** (doc_id=4557). **VRS** (~$110B) stress testing showed a five-year average return of **1.95%** under a Tech Bubble scenario and **0.86%** under a Secular Stagflation scenario, against the **6.75%** assumed rate of return (doc_id=4553).

*Sources: [LACERS — Agenda — June 23, 2026](?doc=4717), [LAFPP — Minutes — May 21, 2026](?doc=4719), [IPERS — Board Pack — June 18, 2026](?doc=4588), [OCERS — Agenda — June 15, 2026](?doc=4557), [VRS — Board Pack — June 17, 2026](?doc=4553)*

**Practical implication:** With **LACERS**, **LAFPP**, and **IPERS** all receiving the same directional actuarial advice yet reaching different conclusions, boards should document the specific rationale — horizon weighting, credibility adjustments, or sponsor capacity — that drives assumption retention or reduction, as divergent outcomes from identical consultant recommendations will attract scrutiny.

## 3. Benchmark and Policy Overhauls: Internal Management, SAA, and Private Credit Frameworks

Multiple plans undertook structural investment policy changes in June, spanning benchmark adoption, internal program expansion, and portfolio framework redesign.

**SIB-ND** (~$8B) ratified updated Cash Overlay Portfolio guidelines expanding permitted instruments to broader fixed income and equity markets including forwards, swaps, and ETFs (doc_id=4459). Internal equity AUM grew from a **$60 million** pilot to **$2.2 billion** and internal fixed income AUM grew to approximately **$2.1 billion**, with the combined internal program managing approximately **$4.2 billion** out of **$28.8 billion** total AUM and generating an estimated **$16.3 million** in FY2026 savings (doc_id=4459). The committee adopted the **Morningstar LSTA U.S. Leveraged Loan Index plus 100 basis points** as the benchmark for the pension funds' Private Credit allocation by a **5-0** vote, and awarded the Performance and Benchmark Consultant contract to **Callan** effective June 5, 2026, replacing **Verus**, which had held a benchmark contract at **$133,000/year** (doc_id=4459).

**VRS** (~$110B) brought its FY2027 DB Plan Strategic Asset Allocation to the June 17 board alongside proposed benchmark changes for Credit Strategies, Diversifying Strategies, Private Equity, and Private Investment Partnerships effective no sooner than July 1, 2026, with adjusted incentive hurdles validated by independent consultant **Mercer** (doc_id=4591). **VRS** also transitioned its DC International Stock Fund from the MSCI ACWI IMI ex USA Index to an MSCI ACWI IMI ex USA, ex China, ex Hong Kong strategy effective January 30, 2026, to align with its Foreign Adversaries Policy (doc_id=4591).

**WSIB** (~$200B) is reviewing Private Credit Investment Program Policy 2.10.500, proposing a core-satellite framework across direct lending (**40%**), opportunistic credit (**40%**), distressed debt (**10%**), and levered strategies (**10%**), with a proposed benchmark of the S&P UBS Leveraged Loan Index and VettaFI U.S. High Yield Index plus **200 bps** (doc_id=4590). **CalPERS** (~$502B) advanced its Total Portfolio Approach set to launch July 1, 2026, replacing the Strategic Asset Allocation with a reference portfolio framework and setting an aggregate active risk limit of **400 basis points** and an aggregate leverage limit of **20%**, with **Wilshire Advisors** receiving formal oversight of active risk model inputs (doc_id=4789).

*Sources: [SIB-ND — Board Pack — June 12, 2026](?doc=4459), [VRS — Board Pack — June 17, 2026](?doc=4591), [WSIB — Board Pack — June 04, 2026](?doc=4590), [CalPERS — Board Pack — June 15, 2026](?doc=4789)*

**Practical implication:** The simultaneous policy overhauls at **SIB-ND**, **VRS**, **WSIB**, and **CalPERS** suggest a broader reassessment of benchmark adequacy and portfolio construction frameworks; plans that have not reviewed private credit benchmarks or SAA structures since pre-2024 should assess whether current policy documents reflect current market conditions and risk tolerances.

## 4. Manager Restructuring: Terminations, Consolidations, and Smart Beta Exits

June saw meaningful manager roster changes at several plans, with a discernible pattern of exiting smart beta and active small-cap mandates in favor of passive or restructured active strategies.

**LAFPP** (~$29B) approved a comprehensive U.S. Equity Structure Implementation Plan terminating **Northern Trust Investments, Inc.** (STOXX USA 900 Min Variance Smart Beta), **AllianceBernstein L.P.** (U.S. Systematic Value Smart Beta), **AllianceBernstein L.P.**, **Channing Capital Management, LLC**, and **Westwood Management Corp** (Domestic Small Cap Value Equity), and **Palisade Capital Management, LP** (Domestic Small Cap Core Equity), while extending contracts with **Boston Partners Global Investors, Inc.**, **Denali Advisors, LLC**, **Phocas Financial Corporation**, **Pacific Investment Management Company, LLC**, and **Frontier Capital Management Company, LLC** through December 31, 2029 (doc_id=4719).

**DPERS** (~$12B) terminated its **T. Rowe Price** Global Growth account, directing **$150 million** to a **BlackRock** Russell 3000 Index Fund and **$50 million** to a **BlackRock** MSCI ACWI ex US Index Fund, with approximately **60%** of remaining proceeds going to a new **Northern Trust Asset Management** separate account mirroring the Russell 1000 Growth Index (doc_id=4663).

**LAFPP** also approved a three-year contract extension with **Brandes Investment Partners, L.P.** for international equity management from August 1, 2026 through July 31, 2029; **Brandes** manages approximately **$2.4 billion** at a fee of **20 basis points** and is ranked in the first percentile of the eVestment ACWI ex US Large Cap Value Equity universe (doc_id=4852). **BRS** retained **PanAgora** as its core international equity manager — **PanAgora** holds approximately **$384 million** in assets, charges **29 basis points**, and has a relationship with BRS dating to 1994 — and selected **MFS** for the international equity growth mandate, replacing **Walter Scott**, and **Todd** for the value mandate (doc_id=4816).

*Sources: [LAFPP — Minutes — May 21, 2026](?doc=4719), [DPERS — Minutes — April 24, 2026](?doc=4663), [LAFPP — Agenda — July 01, 2026](?doc=4852), [BRS — Minutes — May 27, 2026](?doc=4816)*

**Practical implication:** The exits from smart beta and active small-cap mandates at **LAFPP** and the active-to-passive reallocation at **DPERS** point to cost and performance scrutiny of factor-based and active strategies; plans retaining similar mandates should ensure upcoming contract reviews include explicit performance attribution relative to passive alternatives.
