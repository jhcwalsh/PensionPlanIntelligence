## Climate Strategy & ESG Reporting

**NYC TRS** (part of **NYC Retirement**, **$260B**, NY) presented its FY2025 Annual Climate Report, the most detailed ESG disclosure in this period. Key metrics: Scope 1 and 2 financed emissions intensity fell **49%** since the December 2019 baseline, surpassing the 32% interim target set for June 2025. Climate solution investments reached **$7.34 billion** against a 2035 goal of **$19 billion**. However, the science-based target coverage goal of 70% of financed emissions was not met — only **27%** of financed emissions are covered by validated targets as of June 30, 2025.

The board adopted **NZIF 2.0** (Paris Aligned Investment Initiative) for listed equity and corporate fixed income assessment and expanded the private real estate climate solution definition to include European buildings with top two EPC levels (adding **$115M** in European buildings to the **$245M** in U.S. Energy Star buildings). TRS explicitly decided **not** to set formal Scope 3 interim reduction targets, citing data quality and double-counting concerns. All 42 TRS public markets managers submitted net zero alignment plans; 64% adopted net zero goals with quantified targets.

## Governance & Asset Reclassification

**LACERA** (**$75B**, CA) Joint Organizational Governance Committee recommended reclassifying its headquarters, Gateway Plaza, from an investment asset to a capital asset under Option 4 — treating the title holding company as a blended component unit in Year 1 (effective July 1, 2026) and dissolving it in Year 2. The reclassification will produce an estimated **$30 million** non-cash write-down and an approximately **8 basis point** drag on investment performance, with de minimis funded status impact. The move aligns with board-approved policy to reduce separate account real estate in favor of commingled fund investments. **CliftonLarsonAllen LLP**, LACERA's new external auditor beginning June 30, 2026, reviewed the options and raised no concerns.

**LACERS** adopted a Board Succession Policy establishing the order of succession (Executive Officer → Assistant General Manager → Chief Investment Officer) and authorized the Board President to transmit four Charter reform proposals to the Los Angeles City Council, including requests for exclusive staffing and compensation authority and independent legal counsel authority.
