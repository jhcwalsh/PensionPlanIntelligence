## Private Markets Deployment: PE, Credit, Real Assets, and Secondaries

Private markets commitment activity dominated H1 2026 board agendas, with deployment spanning buyout, secondaries, credit, infrastructure, real estate, and venture strategies. The breadth and scale signal continued institutional conviction in illiquid premiums even as valuation multiples remain elevated and manager selection scrutiny intensifies.

The single largest commitment bloc came from **LACERA ($75B, CA)**, whose CIO approved over **$2.2 billion** in private markets transactions across January–March 2026. These included a **$750 million** multi-asset credit managed account with **Neuberger Berman**, a **$400 million** commitment to **Green Equity Investors X, L.P.** (Leonard Green's North America-focused global buyout fund), **$400 million** into **PGIM Real Estate's PRISA** open-end core real estate fund, a **$412 million** rebalance in **Clarion Lion Properties Fund**, **$350 million** to **AE Industrial Partners IV, L.P.** (industrials/materials buyout), **$150 million** to **Regal Healthcare Capital Partners IV**, and **$100 million** to **Otro Capital Fund I** (sports, media, and entertainment). LACERA's CIO also approved **$41 million** and **$25 million** co-investments alongside **Kingswood Capital Management** and **Primary Venture Partners**, respectively. The pace and size of these CIO-delegated approvals prompted the LACERA Board to formally request a staff review of the private equity investment evaluation and authorization process under current policy.

The **Minnesota State Board of Investment ($161B, MN)** approved eleven private markets commitments totaling up to **$1.975 billion** at its October 2025 meeting (reported at the March 2026 session). Notable allocations included **$300 million** to **Blackstone Supplemental Account – M**, **$250 million** each to **Strategic Partners X** and **Permira IX**, **$250 million** to **ECP VI**, **$200 million** each to **Bridgepoint Europe VIII** and **Wind Point Partners XI**, **$150 million** each to **Advent International GPE XI**, **Nordic Capital XII**, and **TA Realty Value-Add Fund XIV**, and **$125 million** to **Merit Capital Fund VIII**. The breadth of geographies—U.S. mid-market, European buyout, Nordic—reflects a globally diversified deployment mandate.

**IPERS ($41B, IA)** adopted its CY2026 private markets commitment plan targeting maximum commitments of **$750 million** to private real assets, **$700 million** to private equity, and **$575 million** to private credit, for a combined **$2.025 billion**. This followed the adoption of Portfolio C, which reallocated the eliminated 5% Global Smart Beta sleeve into 2% core fixed income and 3% public credit—partly to fund expanded private credit allocations.

Secondaries activity featured prominently. **Dallas Police & Fire Pension System ($3B, TX)** committed **$35 million** to **Blackstone Strategic Partners X** (a $22.5 billion PE secondaries vehicle focused on mature LP portfolios) and up to **$15 million** to **Lexington Continuation Vehicle Investors**, both approved 9-1. **PSERS ($73B, PA)** approved commitments to **LS Power Equity Partners VI**, **DRA Growth and Income Fund XII**, and **Warwick Partners V** at its March Investment Committee meeting. **HPOPS ($8B, TX)** considered commitments to **Ares Pathfinder III**, **Kingswood Capital Opportunities Fund IV**, and **LS Power Equity Partners VI** simultaneously. The recurrence of **LS Power Equity Partners VI** across multiple plans—HPOPS, PSERS—underscores competitive demand for energy transition infrastructure equity.

**WSIB ($200B, WA)** reviewed three commitments at its April Private Markets Committee: **Charterhouse Capital Partners XII** (pan-European mid-market buyout targeting €1.5–2.0bn, with predecessor CCP X generating a 20% net IRR and 2.0x TVPI), dual commitments to **Spark Capital IX** and **Spark Capital Growth VI** (venture and venture growth across **Spark Capital's** $8.7B AUM platform), and **Monarch Capital Partners VII** under its Innovation Portfolio private credit sleeve.

On real estate, **OCERS ($25B, CA)** approved a 2026 Real Estate Investment Plan targeting **$50–100 million** in core and **$150–225 million** in non-core commitments, with sector preferences including data centers, built-to-rent, manufactured housing, and industrial. OCERS also approved a **$350 million** real assets pacing plan and **$400 million** per year in private income strategies. **SERS PA ($35B, PA)** evaluated a commitment to **Francisco Partners Fund VIII** and **Francisco Partners Agility Fund IV** at its March meeting.

**SCERS ($12B, CA)** approved a **$450 million** commitment to **Man Numeric** for a Global Core ex-US equity strategy—the largest single public equity mandate decision in the reviewed dataset.

## Manager Hires and Mandate Changes

Manager hiring activity in H1 2026 spanned absolute return, real estate equity, small cap equity, emerging markets, and infrastructure, with several plans executing multi-manager RFP processes and at least two managing underperforming mandates on watch lists.

**IPERS ($41B, IA)** executed the most comprehensive manager hire process of the period, approving three absolute return/portable alpha managers—**Florin Court Capital** (Florin Court Capital Programme), **Capital Fund Management SA** (CFM ISTEC Program), and **AHL Partners LLP** (AHL Cat Bond Program)—all subject to due diligence and contract negotiations, approved 7-0. Simultaneously, IPERS hired **BentallGreenOak (BGO)** for U.S. Core Plus real estate equity and **CBRE Group, Inc.** for U.S. Core real estate equity, replacing or supplementing existing managers in a real estate equity RFP. These five hires, alongside the Portfolio C restructuring, represent a comprehensive repositioning of the $41B fund's active risk budget.

**LACERS ($25B, CA)** renewed contracts with **Granahan Investment Management** (U.S. Small Cap Growth, ~$160.8M, 70 bps fee) and **Copeland Capital Management** (U.S. Small Cap Core, ~$297.3M, 46 bps fee) for one year each, extending mandates through July 2027 despite persistent underperformance. **Copeland** has been on watch since March 2025 after posting -1.04% versus its 12.81% benchmark for FY2025; **Granahan** returned 2.38% against 13.01% for the same period. Consultant **NEPC** recommended continuation pending improvement, noting style headwinds—dividend-growth strategies in Copeland's case and long-duration/consumer discretionary tilts at Granahan—as mitigating factors. LACERS also renewed a 3-year contract with **Dimensional Fund Advisors LP** for Active Non-U.S. Emerging Markets Value Equities despite DFA appearing on the Ethical Contract Compliance restricted sources list expiring June 30, 2026. The board separately issued an RFP for up to approximately **$800 million** in actively and passively managed Global Listed Infrastructure, with proposals due June 4, 2026—one of the largest active infrastructure mandates publicly marketed in the period.

**LACERA ($75B, CA)** extended its private equity consulting agreement with **StepStone Group LP** for one year (pending April 8 board approval) while simultaneously launching an RFP for a new Real Estate consultant, with StepStone also serving in that current role. The dual consultant review—PE advisory and real estate advisory—is an unusual posture suggesting the board is reassessing external advisory relationships comprehensively.

**PSERS ($73B, PA)** received an update on the **Verus/Cerity merger**, a significant development for a plan using Verus (formerly Verus Investments) as a consultant; the integration status and continuity of services were addressed by the Investment Committee. Additionally, PSERS approved delegated authorization for **LLR Equity Partners** as part of its private markets delegation structure.

**LACERS ($25B, CA)** also advanced discussions on a private credit consulting contract with **Aksia LLC**, whose existing contract expired March 31, 2026. The continuity of Aksia's coverage—which spans manager diligence across private credit—was flagged as a time-sensitive governance matter.

## Portfolio Strategy Shifts: Allocation Reviews, Policy Changes, and Structural Repositioning

Several plans undertook material portfolio strategy changes in H1 2026, spanning asset allocation restructuring, benchmark revisions, private equity incentive fee reforms, and new asset class establishment.

**IPERS ($41B, IA)** adopted Portfolio C via a 7-0 board vote, eliminating the 5% Global Smart Beta allocation and redistributing it as 2% to core fixed income and 3% to public credit. The shift reflects both return expectations and a structural concern flagged by CEO Greg Samorajski: the fund's active risk tracking error of only 23 basis points against a 150-basis-point target—a nearly eightfold gap—was cited as incompatible with the plan's active management mandate. A multi-meeting review of the active risk policy is planned for 2026. Private markets remain below target, with private credit and private real assets both underallocated against policy, driving the **$2.025 billion** CY2026 commitment plan.

**Colorado PERA ($60B, CO)** advanced a Private Equity Incentive Phase-in at both its Investment Committee (March 19) and full Board (March 20), proposing to raise excess return thresholds phased in over five years: from 50 to 70 basis points (1-year), 30 to 40 basis points (3-year), and 25 to 30 basis points (5-year). The change signals PERA's intent to more rigorously align manager compensation with realized outperformance. PERA also established a dedicated Private Credit asset class in January 2025, carved out from the Opportunistic bucket, with a 6% Alternatives target set as of January 1, 2025. The amended CAP Statement of Investment Policy was approved unanimously at the January 2026 meeting.

**TMRS ($40B, TX)** reported FY2025 total fund performance of **13.29%**, exceeding its benchmark by **107 basis points** and adding **$5.6 billion** to the Trust. The board discussed possible amendments to its Investment Policy Statement with current targets of Public Equity 35%, Private Equity 20%, Credit 21%, Real Assets 18%, and Tactical Opportunities 6%. A trustee risk tolerance survey by **NEPC** is planned for 2026 to inform efficient frontier analysis and future asset allocation. Separately, TMRS's actuarial assumed rate of 6.75% is the most conservative among major Texas plans—lower than ERS and TRS (7.0%) and TCDRS (7.25%)—and an assumption review is planned.

**OCERS ($25B, CA)** adopted MSCI private markets benchmarks in 2025 and is exploring AI tools for fee verification and cash flow modeling. The fund's co-investment program was expanded beyond real estate to other private market asset classes. Meketa's 2026 capital market assumptions presented to OCERS project 10-year expected returns of 9.0% for private equity, 7.8% for private debt, 7.5% for infrastructure, and 7.1% for real estate versus 6.3% for U.S. equity—a spread that continues to justify illiquid allocations.

**LACERA ($75B, CA)** increased its Risk Reduction and Mitigation (RRM) allocation from 19% to 24% in the 2024 Strategic Asset Allocation review, with the current **$22.26 billion** RRM portfolio representing 37 months of benefit payment liquidity (excluding hedge funds). The hedge fund benchmark was revised in July 2024 from T-Bills + 250 bps to T-Bills + 200 bps, reflecting lower return expectations. The LACERA Passive Cash Overlay search—with finalists including **Legal & General Asset Management**, **NISA Investment Advisors**, **Parametric Portfolio Associates**, **Russell Investments**, and **State Street Investment Management**—remained in contract development through Q1 2026.

**CalPERS ($502B, CA)** had a Total Fund Policy Review listed as a first reading at its March 16-18 meeting, alongside an Asset Liability Management strategy update for both affiliate funds and the Public Employees' Retirement Fund and a Reference Portfolio Benchmark Recommendation advised by **Wilshire Advisors**. A strategic closed-session discussion of Tesla ownership was also on the agenda, an unusual single-stock governance item for the nation's largest public pension.

## Performance Data: Fund Returns Versus Benchmarks

CY2025 and FY2025 performance data reported across plans in H1 2026 was broadly strong, with total fund returns generally in the high single to low double digits, driven by equity markets and, selectively, by private markets and real assets.

**IPERS ($41B, IA)** led large-fund total return disclosures with a **13.75%** CY2025 return versus a 13.40% policy benchmark, an outperformance of **35 basis points**. All major public equity asset classes outperformed: U.S. Equity returned **17.78%** (benchmark: 17.15%), International Equity **33.21%** (benchmark: 32.39%), and Public Credit **10.82%** (benchmark: 10.22%). The notable exception was LARS (liquid alternatives/risk-seeking), which returned **-6.88%** against a **4.18%** benchmark—a 706-basis-point miss that contributed to the active risk budget review. The fund's 10-year annualized total return stands at **8.98%** versus 8.92%.

**TMRS ($40B, TX)** reported FY2025 total fund performance of **13.29%** versus a benchmark of **12.22%**, outperforming by **107 basis points**—the strongest benchmark-relative result among plans reporting full-year data.

**LACERA ($75B, CA)** posted FY2025 total pension fund performance of **9.7%** against its policy benchmark, with a 5-year return of **9.8%** and 10-year of **7.9%**. The OPEB Trust outperformed the pension fund at **11.1%** (FY2025), **10.5%** (3-year), and **9.0%** (5-year). The RRM portfolio returned **6.8%** for the 1-year period ending February 2026 versus a 6.0% benchmark, with Infrastructure outperforming at **12.3%** (1-year, vs. 11.1% benchmark) and Natural Resources posting a standout **17.8%** (1-year, vs. 4.1% benchmark), driven by gold and electrification-related metals demand. Infrastructure co-investments returned **23.1%** since inception (March 2023) versus a 9.9% benchmark.

**SCERS ($12B, CA)** reported an **11.1%** FY2024-2025 total fund return against the 6.75% actuarial assumption, materially reducing the Net Pension Liability by **$672.7 million** year-over-year to **$1.019 billion**.

**MN SBI ($161B, MN)** reported Combined Funds (as of December 31, 2025) at a 10-year annualized return of **9.9%** versus a 9.7% benchmark, with the fund ranking top quartile or better versus public pension peers across all measured time periods (1, 3, 5, 10, and 20 years) per TUCS.

**OCERS ($25B, CA)** reported a **11.3%** YTD return through mid-November 2025 against a 7.0% benchmark. At the small cap manager level, **LACERS ($25B, CA)** disclosed significant underperformance: **Copeland Capital Management** returned **-1.04%** for FY2025 versus a 12.81% benchmark (a 1,285-basis-point miss), and **Granahan Investment Management** returned **2.38%** versus 13.01% (a 1,063-basis-point miss). Both remain under active monitoring.

**Colorado PERA ($60B, CO)** Q4 2025 CAP fund data showed PERAdvantage International Stock Fund returning **3.6%** versus a **5.1%** benchmark—the only material quarterly miss across its lineup—while the U.S. SMID Cap Stock Fund outperformed at **1.7%** versus 1.1%.

## Governance, ESG, and Leadership Transitions

Governance themes in H1 2026 were unusually active, encompassing leadership turnover, fiduciary counsel restructuring, AI policy adoption, ESG-adjacent legislative activity, and formal board evaluation processes.

**LACERA ($75B, CA)** underwent a concentrated leadership transition: Chief Counsel Vincent Lim retired March 31, 2026 after the Disability Litigation Office he led was merged into the Legal Office; Assistant Executive Officer Laura Guglielmo retired in March 2026; Division Manager Bernie Buenaflor and Assistant Division Manager Leilani Ignacio also departed. **Leisha Collins** was appointed Chief Internal Audit via dual 8-0 votes by both the Board of Retirement (March 4) and Board of Investments (March 11)—an unusual dual-approval requirement reflecting the role's fund-wide oversight mandate. The plan also selected a new three-firm fiduciary counsel panel—**Klausner Kaufman Jensen & Levinson**, **Nossaman LLP**, and **Reed Smith LLP**—for a five-year term, replacing a prior arrangement. Hourly rates range from $500 (Klausner) to $600–$720 (Nossaman) depending on seniority. The LACERA Board adopted an AI Policy Public Summary at its March 4 meeting, requiring human oversight for all AI-assisted final decisions and prohibiting sensitive data in unapproved tools. Microsoft Copilot is deployed organization-wide, with active development of AI-assisted disability retirement document processing and a lacera.gov member chatbot.

**Colorado PERA ($60B, CO)** commissioned a formal 2025 Board Performance Evaluation via **Mosaic Governance Advisors** (14 of 16 trustees participated), the results of which were presented at the March Governance Committee. Priority trustee education topics for 2026 include AI in investments, asset allocation, performance measurement relative to liabilities, technology governance, and cybersecurity risk. A substantive governance debate emerged at the January 2026 board meeting over CEO evaluation criteria: a motion to measure investment performance "relative to liabilities" and using "actuarial value of returns" was defeated 1-13, with only one trustee (Moré) in favor. CEO Andrew Roth's first full-year evaluation is scheduled for June 2026.

**TMRS ($40B, TX)** flagged the impending retirement of Director of Actuarial Services Leslee Hardy (April 30, 2026, after 18 years of service) and the need to hire a Director of Governmental Relations ahead of the 2027 Texas Legislative Session. TMRS achieved GIPS verification for the fourth consecutive year—the first Texas public pension to do so—and received the GFOA Certificate of Excellence in Financial Reporting for the 38th consecutive year.

**IPERS ($41B, IA)** Deputy CEO Rick Hindman retired in January 2026, with direct reports transitioning to CEO Greg Samorajski. The fund's CEM Benchmarking review confirmed IPERS as a low-cost fund for CY2024, with a 5-year net value added of **0.8%**, at the U.S. public median.

On ESG and legislative matters, **Colorado PERA** included a divestment action on its consent agenda for March 20 regarding companies with prohibitions against Israel (Anti-BDS compliance). **LACERA ($75B, CA)** submitted a comment letter to the UK Financial Conduct Authority on proposed sustainability disclosure requirements—an unusually proactive extraterritorial engagement for a U.S. county plan. **SCERS ($12B, CA)** amended its Board Education Policy to incorporate Senate Bill 827, effective January 1, 2026, which added a mandatory two-hour fiscal and financial training requirement for California county retirement board members. California Assembly Bill **1619**—proposing to raise CERL board meeting compensation from $100 to up to $320, the first adjustment in over 40 years—received "Support If Amended" recommendations from both LACERA and SCERS committees. **LACERS ($25B, CA)** adopted a formal Succession Policy formalized by its 2022 Management Audit, specifying a leadership continuity order of Executive Officer → Assistant General Manager → Chief Investment Officer for General Manager vacancies.

**PSERS ($73B, PA)** approved a Public Information Policy and discussed a Cybersecurity Insurance Policy renewal in executive session, reflecting heightened operational risk focus. **OCERS ($25B, CA)** directed immediate recruitment for its vacant Director of Internal Audit position following a resignation that raised concerns about 2026 audit plan completion.
