## 1. Private Markets Deployment Accelerates — With Secondaries as the Entry Point of Choice

The most consistent investment action across this window is new private markets capital deployment, with secondaries funds appearing as a preferred vehicle for plans building or rebuilding allocations. **Dallas Police & Fire Pension System (~$2.2B AUM)** committed **$35 million to Blackstone Strategic Partners X** and up to **$15 million to Lexington Continuation Vehicle Investors**, both private equity secondaries, approved 9-1 at its April board meeting ([source](?doc=1017)). The plan's private equity allocation stands at just 1.8% actual versus a 6% target — a **-$148 million** variance — making secondaries a logical catch-up vehicle given their shorter J-curve and faster capital deployment relative to primaries ([source](?doc=1017)).

**PSERS (~$73B, PA)** simultaneously approved commitments to **LS Power Equity Partners VI, L.P.** and **DRA Growth and Income Fund XII, LLC**, alongside a delegated authorization for **LLR Equity Partners**, at its March 19 Investment Committee meeting ([source](?doc=380)). **HPOPS (~$8B, TX)** considered commitments to **Ares Pathfinder III**, **Kingswood Capital Opportunities Fund IV**, and **LS Power Equity Partners VI** at its April 7 Investment Committee ([source](?doc=568)) — the overlap with PSERS on LS Power is notable.

At the larger end, **LACERA (~$75B, CA)** disclosed CIO-approved commitments totaling **$416 million** in the March–April window: **$350 million to AE Industrial Partners IV, L.P.**, a **$41 million co-investment alongside Kingswood Capital Management**, and a **$25 million co-investment alongside Primary Venture Partners** ([source](?doc=84)). **WSIB (~$200B, WA)** advanced commitments to **Charterhouse Capital Partners XII** (pan-European mid-market buyout, targeting €1.5–2.0bn), **Spark Capital IX** and **Spark Capital Growth VI** (venture), and **Monarch Capital Partners VII** (private credit/innovation) ([source](?doc=682), [source](?doc=1084)). **MN SBI (~$161B, MN)** ratified eleven private markets commitments totaling up to **$1.975 billion** approved at its October 2025 meeting, spanning **Advent International GPE XI**, **Blackstone Supplemental Account – M**, **Strategic Partners X**, **Permira IX**, **TPG Partners X**, and others ([source](?doc=684)).

The ASRS data adds useful market context: the private equity secondaries market was on pace for record transaction volume in 2025, with continuation funds also at record levels and PE distribution rates recovering from a low of 11% of NAV to 13% ([source](?doc=891)). NEPC projects a **3.7% illiquidity premium** for private equity versus U.S. large-cap equity over a 10-year horizon, per TMRS materials ([source](?doc=504)).

*Sources: [DPFP — Agenda — April 02, 2026](?doc=1017), [PSERS — Agenda — March 19, 2026](?doc=380), [HPOPS — Agenda — April 07, 2026](?doc=568), [LACERA — Agenda — April 08, 2026](?doc=84), [WSIB — Board Pack — April 02, 2026](?doc=682), [WSIB — Agenda — April 16, 2026](?doc=1084), [MN SBI — Board Pack — March 26, 2026](?doc=684), [ASRS — Agenda — March 26, 2026](?doc=891), [TMRS — Agenda — March 26, 2026](?doc=504)*

**Bottom line:** Secondary funds are functioning as the primary on-ramp for underfunded private equity programs — plans with large allocation gaps are using them to close the distance quickly while maintaining some liquidity optionality. The concentration of commitments in this single window suggests deployment pressure is real and broad-based.

## 2. Portfolio Restructuring: Active Risk Recalibration and Smart Beta Elimination

A quieter but strategically significant theme is the deliberate restructuring of public market portfolios — not just manager swaps, but architectural changes to how alpha is sourced and risk is budgeted.

**IPERS (~$41B, IA)** adopted **Portfolio C**, eliminating its 5% Global Smart Beta allocation entirely and reallocating 2% to core fixed income and 3% to public credit ([source](?doc=82)). Simultaneously, IPERS hired three absolute return/portable alpha managers — **Florin Court Capital** (Florin Court Capital Programme), **Capital Fund Management SA** (CFM ISTEC Program), and **AHL Partners LLP** (AHL Cat Bond Program) — approved 7-0 ([source](?doc=82)). The motivation is explicit: IPERS CEO Greg Samorajski flagged that the fund's tracking error of **23 basis points** was running far below its **150 bps target** and **300 bps upper limit**, signaling a structural underutilization of active risk budget ([source](?doc=82)).

**SIB-ND (~$27.7B total AUM, ND)** is pursuing a parallel evolution it calls "Advanced Funds Management," explicitly separating alpha from market exposures and building toward a portable alpha and overlay framework as a Phase 3 goal. The fund terminated **William Blair International Leaders** and funded **Principal International** with approximately **$407 million** in early March 2026, and is conducting a Fixed Income 2.0 review following the success of its Equity 2.0 initiative, which returned **18.0% in 2025 versus a 17.1% benchmark** ([source](?doc=1078)).

**CalPERS (~$502B, CA)** is executing the most structurally ambitious version of this shift: a full transition to a **Total Portfolio Approach (TPA)** beginning FY2026-2027, replacing its Strategic Asset Allocation model with a simple **75/25 Global Equity/Global Bond Reference Portfolio** as the performance benchmark. Incentive compensation is being redesigned accordingly, with a five-year phase-in blending TPA and SAA results ([source](?doc=105)). The fund's compensation consultant, **Global Governance Advisors**, noted that the new Reference Portfolio will create more volatility in relative performance reporting compared to the prior 11-benchmark customized structure ([source](?doc=105)).

*Sources: [IPERS — Board Pack — March 27, 2026](?doc=82), [SIB-ND — Board Pack — April 09, 2026](?doc=1078), [CalPERS — Agenda — April 02, 2026](?doc=105)*

**Bottom line:** The convergence of portable alpha hiring, smart beta elimination, and TPA adoption across plans of very different sizes signals a structural shift in how public pension CIOs think about alpha sourcing — away from asset-class silos and toward total-portfolio risk budgeting. Plans still running passive-heavy, low-tracking-error public equity programs should treat IPERS' 23 bps tracking error finding as a diagnostic benchmark.

## 3. Manager Retention Under Pressure: Watch-List Renewals Signal Patience Has Limits

Several plans renewed managers on formal watch status — a pattern that deserves scrutiny because it reveals how boards are balancing performance accountability against transition costs and manager concentration risk.

**LACERS (~$25B, CA)** renewed **Segall Bryant & Hamill** for one year on its U.S. Small Cap Value mandate (~**$172 million**), despite the manager being on watch since August 2024 due to since-inception underperformance of **-1.55% versus the Russell 2000 Value** ([source](?doc=1089)). The renewal was recommended by staff and consultant **NEPC** despite the watch status, with underperformance attributed to the Q4 2020 low-quality stock rally and 2021 meme stock events. SBH charges **57 bps** (~$929,772/year), placing it at the **11th percentile** among peers — meaning 89% of peers charge more ([source](?doc=1089)). Separately, **Informed Momentum Company** (formerly EAM Investors, on watch since June 2024) was also renewed for one year on the U.S. Small Cap Growth mandate (~**$132 million**), despite the watch status — though its fiscal year-to-date return of **+14.5% versus the Russell 2000 Growth** as of February 28, 2026 suggests a meaningful rebound ([source](?doc=1089)). IMC charges **50 bps** (~$609,167/year), at the **4th percentile** among peers ([source](?doc=1089)).

The SBH situation carries an additional governance wrinkle: the firm is indirectly owned by **Mubadala Capital LP**, ultimately owned by the Government of Abu Dhabi following its 2025 acquisition of CI Financial Corp — a foreign sovereign ownership structure that boards are increasingly scrutinizing ([source](?doc=1089)).

**LACERS** also renewed **Loomis Sayles** for three years on its High Yield Fixed Income mandate (~**$416 million**), a manager in compliance with monitoring policy, with a 1-year return of **7.75% versus a 7.18% benchmark** as of February 28, 2026 ([source](?doc=1089)). The contrast between the Loomis renewal (three years, clean record) and the SBH/IMC renewals (one year, watch status) illustrates a deliberate tiering of contract terms by performance standing.

*Sources: [LACERS — Agenda — April 14, 2026](?doc=1089)*

**Practical implication:** One-year renewals for watch-list managers are becoming a standard holding pattern — they preserve optionality while avoiding forced transitions in illiquid or thinly traded asset classes. But the SBH since-inception underperformance record, now spanning multiple market cycles, tests the limits of that patience. Boards should establish explicit watch-list exit triggers rather than allowing rolling one-year renewals to become de facto permanent retention.

## 4. Macro Headwinds Are Registering in YTD Returns — But 2025 Full-Year Results Remain Strong

The Q1 2026 performance picture is materially weaker than the 2025 full-year results that most plans are still celebrating. **DPFP** reported an estimated YTD return of **-2.41%** through March 31, 2026, with the public portfolio down **-1.7%** ([source](?doc=1017)). **SIB-ND** reported the Russell 3000 down **-4.0%** and the S&P 500 down **-4.3%** YTD through March 31, 2026, while MSCI World ex-US was down **-0.9%** ([source](?doc=1078)). **ASRS** noted a geopolitical shock in early March 2026 — an Iran attack that sent the S&P 500 down 2%, MSCI EAFE and EM down nearly 7%, and WTI oil above **$90/barrel**, up approximately 35% for the week, with the VIX spiking to 29 ([source](?doc=891)).

Against this backdrop, the 2025 full-year results were strong across the board: **IPERS** returned **13.75% versus a 13.40% benchmark** for CY2025 ([source](?doc=82)); **TMRS** returned **13.29% versus a 12.22% benchmark** for FY2025, adding **$5.6 billion** to the Trust ([source](?doc=504)); **ASRS** returned **12.79% versus a 12.82% benchmark** for the year ending December 31, 2025 ([source](?doc=891)); and **SCERS** reported an **11.1% return** for FY2024-2025, well above its 6.75% actuarial assumption, driving a **$672.7 million reduction** in Net Pension Liability ([source](?doc=258)).

The one notable underperformer is **PERS-OR (~$110B, OR)**, which returned **10.67% versus a 14.58% policy benchmark** for the one year ending February 2026 — a gap of nearly 400 basis points — and has underperformed its policy benchmark across all measured time periods from 1 through 10 years ([source](?doc=673)). Private equity, at **8.24% versus a 23.23% benchmark** for the one year ending February 2026, is the primary drag ([source](?doc=673)). OPERF's private equity allocation of **24.5%** is above its 20% policy target, compounding the performance impact ([source](?doc=673)).

The macro context from multiple plans' materials is consistent: the Fed held the federal funds rate at **3.50%–3.75%** at its March 2026 meeting (second consecutive pause), February 2026 CPI was **2.4% year-over-year**, and core PCE was sticky at approximately **3.1%** ([source](?doc=84), [source](?doc=1078)).

*Sources: [DPFP — Agenda — April 02, 2026](?doc=1017), [SIB-ND — Board Pack — April 09, 2026](?doc=1078), [ASRS — Agenda — March 26, 2026](?doc=891), [IPERS — Board Pack — March 27, 2026](?doc=82), [TMRS — Agenda — March 26, 2026](?doc=504), [SCERS — Board Pack — March 18, 2026](?doc=258), [PERS-OR — Board Pack — April 03, 2026](?doc=673), [LACERA — Agenda — April 08, 2026](?doc=84)*

**Bottom line:** The 2025 funded-status improvements that boards are ratifying this spring were earned in a favorable market environment that has already reversed in Q1 2026. Plans with large private equity overweights relative to target — particularly those using lagged valuations — face a delayed recognition of mark-to-market pressure. PERS-OR's private equity benchmark gap is an early warning of what overweight-to-target positions can cost in a distribution-constrained vintage cycle.

## 5. Fee Transparency and Cost Efficiency Are Becoming Formal Governance Priorities

Two plans in this window moved to institutionalize investment fee validation as a standalone governance function — a signal that cost oversight is graduating from a periodic audit item to a permanent program.

**KPPA (~$20B, KY)** conducted a competitive RFP for Investment Cost Efficiency Services, selecting **Albourne America LLC** to perform Validation of Fees and Expenses services at **$2,000 per fund for since-inception validation**, with optional ongoing quarterly (**$1,300/fund**) or annual (**$800/fund**) monitoring ([source](?doc=1020)). The RFP drew six respondents; finalist **bFinance** proposed a contingent fee model charging **35% of identified savings over two years**, while **XTP Implementation Services** proposed a success-based model with no fixed fee ([source](?doc=1020)). Staff noted a potential conflict of interest with XTP, as it is a sister company of **Wilshire**, CERS's existing general consultant, both owned by Monica Holdings LP ([source](?doc=1020)).

**PERA Colorado (~$60B, CO)** disclosed that its internal management costs approximately **6.6 basis points** versus an estimated **$111 million** cost to externally manage the same assets — assets currently managed internally for approximately **$27 million** ([source](?doc=407)). All CAP investment management fees were confirmed to fall well below the median of Callan peer groups ([source](?doc=407)). PERA also completed an Investments Cash Overlay audit in February 2026 with a medium risk rating, and conducted wire transfer monitoring for both halves of 2025 ([source](?doc=408)).

The LACERS data adds a useful fee benchmarking reference point: SBH's **57 bps** fee on a small-cap value mandate places it at the 11th percentile among peers, and IMC's **50 bps** on small-cap growth places it at the 4th percentile — meaning the market for active small-cap mandates is substantially more expensive than what LACERS is paying ([source](?doc=1089)).

*Sources: [KPPA — Board Pack — March 25, 2026](?doc=1020), [PERA Colorado — Board Pack — March 19, 2026](?doc=407), [PERA Colorado — Board Pack — March 19, 2026](?doc=408), [LACERS — Agenda — April 14, 2026](?doc=1089)*

**Practical implication:** The move toward dedicated fee validation programs — separate from general investment consulting — reflects growing board awareness that alternative investment fee structures are opaque enough to warrant specialist oversight. Plans without a systematic fee validation process, particularly those with large private markets allocations, should treat KPPA's RFP as a governance template worth replicating.
