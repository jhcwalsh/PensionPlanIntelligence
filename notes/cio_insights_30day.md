## 1. Private Markets Commitment Pace Remains Robust Despite Equity Headwinds

The most consistent signal across this window is continued, deliberate private markets deployment even as public equity markets turned negative. **DCRB ($13.9B, DC)** approved a $750 million allocation to an unnamed public equity fund and a $100 million commitment to a private credit fund, while also closing a €100 million re-up to **CapVest Equity Partners VI** — all against a backdrop of a 1.0% calendar year-to-date total fund return as of March 17, 2026 ([source](?doc=1929)). **WSIB ($200B, WA)** approved commitments to **Charterhouse Capital Partners XII** (pan-European mid-market buyout, targeting €1.5–2.0bn), **Spark Capital IX** and **Spark Capital Growth VI** (venture and venture growth), and **Monarch Capital Partners VII** (private credit/innovation) at its April 16 meeting ([source](?doc=1084)). **MN SBI ($161B, MN)** ratified eleven private markets commitments totaling up to $1.975 billion at its October 2025 meeting, including up to $300 million to **Blackstone Supplemental Account – M**, up to $250 million to **Strategic Partners X**, and up to $250 million to **ECP VI** ([source](?doc=684)). **MPSERS ($122.4B, MI)** committed $1.56 billion across eight fund partnerships in Q4 2025, headlined by a $500 million commitment to **Apollo Asset-Backed Finance Fund** and $300 million to **Alpina SMRS Hybrid** managed by **LGT Capital Partners** ([source](?doc=1517)). **IPERS ($41B, IA)** set a CY2026 private markets commitment plan with estimated maximum commitments of $750 million to private real assets, $700 million to private equity, and $575 million to private credit ([source](?doc=82)). **DPFP ($3B, TX)** committed $35 million to **Blackstone Strategic Partners X** and up to $15 million to **Lexington Continuation Vehicle Investors** — both private equity secondaries — approved 9-1 ([source](?doc=1017)). **ERSRI ($10B, RI)** committed up to $50 million to **L Squared Capital Partners V** (lower middle market buyout) and had **Energy Capital Partners VI** pending at its March 25 SIC meeting ([source](?doc=1945)).

The secondaries theme is particularly notable: DPFP, MN SBI (Strategic Partners X), and WSIB (Charterhouse) all made commitments to buyout or secondaries vehicles in the same window. ASRS noted that the private equity secondaries market was on pace for record transaction volume in 2025, with continuation funds also at record levels and PE distribution rates recovering from a low of 11% of NAV to 13% ([source](?doc=891)). This is consistent with plans using secondaries as a liquidity-efficient deployment mechanism while primary fundraising remains selective.

*Sources: [DCRB — Minutes — March 19, 2026](?doc=1929), [WSIB — Agenda — April 16, 2026](?doc=1084), [MN SBI — Board Pack — March 26, 2026](?doc=684), [MPSERS — Board Pack — March 25, 2026](?doc=1517), [IPERS — Board Pack — March 27, 2026](?doc=82), [DPFP — Agenda — April 02, 2026](?doc=1017), [ERSRI — Board Pack — April 08, 2026](?doc=1945), [ASRS — Agenda — March 26, 2026](?doc=891)*

**Bottom line:** The breadth of private markets deployment across plans of all sizes — from DPFP at $3 billion to MN SBI at $161 billion — signals that commitment pacing plans are proceeding on schedule regardless of near-term public market volatility; secondaries and private credit are the preferred deployment channels.

## 2. Investment Consultant Consolidation: Verus/Cerity Transition Ripples Across Multiple Plans

A structural shift in the investment consulting market is creating visible governance friction across plans. **OPERS-OK ($10B, OK)** approved the transition of investment consulting services from **Verus Advisory** to **Cerity Partners LLC** at its April 16 meeting ([source](?doc=2067)). **PSERS ($73B, PA)** included a "Verus/Cerity Merger Update" as a formal agenda item at its March 19 Investment Committee meeting ([source](?doc=380)). **CTPF ($18B, IL)** conducted due diligence on **Cerity LLC** as a potential investment consultant at its April 9 meeting, alongside a watch list board action and private credit market update ([source](?doc=2048)). The merger is generating consent-to-assignment decisions, due diligence reviews, and in some cases competitive re-evaluations — all simultaneously.

This is not a trivial administrative matter. Consultant relationships are long-term and deeply embedded in manager selection, asset allocation, and risk monitoring processes. Plans that relied on Verus for private markets diligence (a core Verus competency) will need to assess whether Cerity's capabilities are equivalent, particularly as private markets deployment accelerates. OPERS-OK's straightforward consent-to-assignment contrasts with CTPF's competitive evaluation, suggesting plans are taking different views on whether the merger warrants a full re-procurement.

*Sources: [OPERS-OK — Agenda — April 16, 2026](?doc=2067), [PSERS — Agenda — March 19, 2026](?doc=380), [CTPF — Agenda — April 09, 2026](?doc=2048)*

**Practical implication:** Plans with Verus relationships should confirm whether their contracts require affirmative consent to assignment and, if so, treat this as an opportunity to benchmark Cerity's capabilities against the market — particularly for private markets advisory, where the competitive landscape now includes **Albourne** (retained by LACERS, KPPA, and DPFP), **StepStone** (LACERA), and **Meketa** (ERSRI, DPFP, OCERS).

## 3. Fee Scrutiny and Cost Efficiency Are Becoming Formal Programs, Not Ad Hoc Reviews

Several plans are institutionalizing fee validation as a standalone governance function. **KPPA ($20B, KY)** ran a formal RFP for Investment Cost Efficiency Services, evaluating **Albourne America**, **bFinance**, and **XTP Implementation Services**, ultimately recommending Albourne at $2,000 per fund for since-inception validation with optional ongoing monitoring at $800–$1,300 per fund annually ([source](?doc=1020)). The RFP was originally directed by the CERS Board in June 2025, reflecting a deliberate governance decision rather than a reactive one. **PERA Colorado ($60B, CO)** disclosed that internal management costs approximately 6.6 basis points versus an estimated $111 million cost to externally manage the same assets — currently managed internally for approximately $27 million — and confirmed all CAP investment management fees fall well below the median of Callan peer groups ([source](?doc=407)). **IPERS ($41B, IA)** reported FY2025 preliminary investment management expenses of $62.08 million, or 0.137% of average fund value, a decrease of 33.1% from FY2024, and was ranked a low-cost fund by CEM Benchmarking with a five-year net value added of 0.8% ([source](?doc=82)). **LACERS ($25B, CA)** disclosed specific fee rates for managers under renewal: **Segall Bryant & Hamill** at 57 basis points (~$929,772/year, 11th percentile among peers) and **Informed Momentum Company** at 50 basis points (~$609,167/year, 4th percentile) ([source](?doc=1089)).

The LACERS data is particularly instructive: both managers are on watch for performance, yet both are being renewed in part because their fees are at the low end of the peer distribution. This suggests fee competitiveness is functioning as a partial offset to performance concerns in renewal decisions — a dynamic worth monitoring.

*Sources: [KPPA — Board Pack — March 25, 2026](?doc=1020), [PERA Colorado — Board Pack — March 19, 2026](?doc=407), [IPERS — Board Pack — March 27, 2026](?doc=82), [LACERS — Agenda — April 14, 2026](?doc=1089)*

**Practical implication:** The formalization of fee validation as a board-level program — not just staff-level monitoring — raises the bar for manager fee justification across the industry. Managers in the upper quartile of fee distribution should expect more systematic scrutiny, particularly in alternatives where fee complexity (management fees, carried interest, monitoring fees) creates the largest validation gap.

## 4. Public Equity Underperformance and Benchmark Drag Are Concentrating in Specific Structures

The Q1 2026 public equity drawdown is creating differentiated outcomes that reveal structural vulnerabilities. The **S&P 500** was down approximately 4.3% year-to-date as of March 31, 2026, while **MSCI World ex-US** was down only 0.9% over the same period ([source](?doc=1078)). **DPFP** reported a total fund return of -2.41% year-to-date through March 31, 2026, with the public portfolio down -1.7% ([source](?doc=1017)). **MSRS ($25B, MN)** DC plan data showed U.S. large cap equity down approximately 4% year-to-date through Q1 2026, while international equity was modestly positive ([source](?doc=1618), [source](?doc=1619)).

The more significant signal is the persistent benchmark drag at **PERS-OR ($110B, OR)**: the total fund returned 10.67% for the trailing one year ending February 2026 against a policy benchmark of 14.58% — a gap of nearly 400 basis points — and has underperformed its policy benchmark across all measured time periods from one to ten years ([source](?doc=673)). Private equity, at 8.24% versus a 23.23% benchmark for the one-year period, is the primary driver of this gap. With private equity at 24.5% of the portfolio — above its 20% policy target — the lagged valuation effect of private equity is creating a structural benchmark drag that is difficult to close in the near term.

**MPSERS** presents the inverse: private equity and venture capital returned 7.8% versus a 21.1% benchmark for the one-year period ending December 31, 2025, also a significant underperformance, yet the total fund still returned 13.7% because domestic equity (20.2% vs. 17.0% benchmark) and international equity (34.4% vs. 32.4% benchmark) more than compensated ([source](?doc=1516)). The MPSERS equity beta of approximately 0.34 — well below the peer median of approximately 0.55 — reflects a deliberately lower equity risk posture that paradoxically delivered strong absolute returns in 2025's "everything rally."

*Sources: [SIB-ND — Board Pack — April 09, 2026](?doc=1078), [DPFP — Agenda — April 02, 2026](?doc=1017), [MSRS — Performance — March 31, 2026](?doc=1618), [MSRS — Performance — March 31, 2026](?doc=1619), [PERS-OR — Board Pack — April 03, 2026](?doc=673), [MPSERS — Board Pack — March 25, 2026](?doc=1516)*

**Practical implication:** Plans with large private equity overweights relative to policy targets face a compounding problem in 2026: lagged valuations suppress reported returns while public equity benchmarks reset lower, potentially narrowing the gap on paper but not reflecting true economic performance. Boards should ensure their performance attribution frameworks distinguish between lagged valuation effects and genuine alpha generation.

## 5. Total Portfolio Approach Adoption Is Reshaping Incentive Structures and Benchmark Architecture

**CalPERS ($502B, CA)** is the most advanced example of a structural shift that will have industry-wide implications. The fund is transitioning to a Total Portfolio Approach beginning FY2026-2027, replacing its Strategic Asset Allocation model and measuring performance against a simple 75/25 global equity/global bond Reference Portfolio rather than a customized multi-asset-class benchmark ([source](?doc=105)). Global Governance Advisors recommended a five-year phase-in of TPA performance into incentive calculations, blending TPA and SAA results during the transition, and proposed adding a 15% Total Fund performance weighting for the General Counsel, CFO, and COO to reduce organizational silos ([source](?doc=103)). The FY2026-2027 budget includes $43.5 million for annual incentive awards and $13.6 million for long-term incentive awards ([source](?doc=104)).

**SIB-ND ($8B, ND)** is pursuing a parallel evolution it terms "Advanced Funds Management," explicitly separating alpha from market exposures and outlining a three-phase roadmap toward total portfolio optimization and capital efficiency, with overlay and portable alpha frameworks as Phase 3 goals ([source](?doc=1078)). **IPERS ($41B, IA)** hired three absolute return/portable alpha managers — **Florin Court Capital**, **Capital Fund Management SA** (CFM ISTEC Program), and **AHL Partners LLP** (AHL Cat Bond Program) — and simultaneously eliminated its 5% Global Smart Beta allocation, reallocating to core fixed income and public credit under Portfolio C ([source](?doc=82)). CEO Greg Samorajski flagged concern over IPERS' very low active risk, with tracking error of 23 basis points against a target of 150 basis points, signaling that the portable alpha buildout is intended to increase active risk in a controlled manner.

The common thread is a move away from static asset class buckets toward dynamic risk allocation — a structural shift that changes how managers are selected, how performance is attributed, and how incentive compensation is designed.

*Sources: [CalPERS — Agenda — April 02, 2026](?doc=105), [CalPERS — Agenda — April 02, 2026](?doc=103), [CalPERS — Agenda — April 13, 2026](?doc=104), [SIB-ND — Board Pack — April 09, 2026](?doc=1078), [IPERS — Board Pack — March 27, 2026](?doc=82)*

**Practical implication:** The TPA/portable alpha transition at large plans creates new demand for liquid, benchmark-agnostic strategies — trend-following, cat bonds, and systematic macro — while reducing the relevance of traditional asset-class-specific mandates. Managers positioned as "core" within a single asset class bucket face structural headwinds as allocators increasingly evaluate contributions to total portfolio risk-adjusted return rather than asset-class-relative performance.
