## 1. Private Markets Deployment Accelerates Despite Structural Underweights

The most consistent signal across this window is active private markets commitment activity, even as several plans acknowledge meaningful gaps between actual and target allocations. **Dallas Police & Fire Pension System (~$3B, TX)** authorized **$35 million** to **Blackstone Strategic Partners X** and up to **$15 million** to **Lexington Continuation Vehicle Investors** — both private equity secondaries — by a 9-1 vote, while simultaneously disclosing that private equity sits at 1.8% actual versus a 6% target, and total private markets at 11.2% versus an 18% target, a shortfall of approximately **$148 million** ([source](?doc=1017)). The board has 4–6 additional private markets commitments planned for 2026 under a formal pacing plan ([source](?doc=1017)).

**Washington State Investment Board (~$200B, WA)** approved commitments to **Charterhouse Capital Partners XII** (pan-European mid-market buyout, targeting €1.5–2.0bn), **Spark Capital IX** and **Spark Capital Growth VI** (venture and venture growth), and **Monarch Capital Partners VII** (private credit/innovation) at its April 2 and April 16 meetings ([source](?doc=682), [source](?doc=1084)). Charterhouse's predecessor funds posted net IRRs of 20% (CCP X) and 15% (CCP XI) as of December 31, 2025 ([source](?doc=682)).

**LACERA (~$75B, CA)** had its CIO approve **$350 million** to **AE Industrial Partners IV**, a **$41 million** co-investment alongside **Kingswood Capital Management**, and a **$25 million** co-investment alongside **Primary Venture Partners** — all in the March 11, 2026 cycle ([source](?doc=84)). **Minnesota State Board of Investment (~$161B, MN)** ratified eleven private markets commitments totaling up to **$1.975 billion** at its October 2025 meeting, including up to **$300 million** to **Blackstone Supplemental Account – M**, **$250 million** to **Strategic Partners X**, and **$250 million** to **Permira IX** ([source](?doc=684)). **IPERS (~$41B, IA)** established a 2026 private markets commitment plan with estimated maximum commitments of **$750 million** to private real assets, **$700 million** to private equity, and **$575 million** to private credit ([source](?doc=82)).

The secondaries theme is notable: DPFP, MN SBI (Strategic Partners X), and LACERA (Kingswood co-investment) all touched the secondaries market in this window, consistent with ASRS's observation that the PE secondaries market is on pace for record transaction volume in 2025 and continuation funds are also at record levels ([source](?doc=891)).

*Sources: [DPFP — Agenda — April 02, 2026](?doc=1017), [WSIB — Board Pack — April 02, 2026](?doc=682), [WSIB — Agenda — April 16, 2026](?doc=1084), [LACERA — Agenda — April 08, 2026](?doc=84), [MN SBI — Board Pack — March 26, 2026](?doc=684), [IPERS — Board Pack — March 27, 2026](?doc=82), [ASRS — Agenda — March 26, 2026](?doc=891)*

**Bottom line:** Secondaries and co-investments are the preferred deployment vehicles for plans closing allocation gaps quickly — expect continued elevated commitment volumes through 2026 as pacing plans execute against persistent underweights.

## 2. Manager Watch Lists and Conditional Renewals Signal Tighter Active Manager Accountability

A recurring pattern this period is the renewal of underperforming managers on explicit watch status — a practice that reflects governance discipline but also reveals the operational friction of terminating active mandates mid-cycle. **LACERS (~$25B, CA)** renewed **Segall Bryant & Hamill** for one year despite since-inception underperformance of 14.34% versus a 15.89% benchmark (since inception September 2020), with the manager on watch since August 2024 ([source](?doc=1089)). The underperformance was attributed to the Q4 2020 low-quality stock rally and 2021 meme stock activity. Separately, **Informed Momentum Company** (formerly EAM Investors, rebranded February 2025) was also renewed for one year despite being on watch since June 2024, though its fiscal year-to-date return of 32.3% versus a 17.8% benchmark as of February 28, 2026 suggests a meaningful strategy rebound ([source](?doc=1089)).

Fee transparency is also sharpening. SBH charges 57 bps (~$929,772/year, 11th percentile among peers), while IMC charges 50 bps (~$609,167/year, 4th percentile) — both disclosed explicitly against peer universes ([source](?doc=1089)). **Kentucky Public Pensions Authority (~$20B, KY)** went further, running a formal RFP for Investment Cost Efficiency Services and unanimously recommending **Albourne America LLC** to perform fee validation across its public and private market portfolios at **$2,000 per fund** for since-inception validation ([source](?doc=1020)). Albourne's service has analyzed over 7,000 investments and validated over **$200 billion** in client assets since 2014 ([source](?doc=1020)).

**North Dakota State Investment Board (~$8B, ND)** terminated **William Blair International Leaders** in early March 2026 and funded **Principal International** with approximately **$407 million** as a replacement ([source](?doc=1078)) — a cleaner termination-and-replace action compared to LACERS' conditional renewals.

*Sources: [LACERS — Agenda — April 14, 2026](?doc=1089), [KPPA — Board Pack — March 25, 2026](?doc=1020), [SIB-ND — Board Pack — April 09, 2026](?doc=1078)*

**Bottom line:** Boards are maintaining active mandates under formal watch rather than terminating — but the parallel rise of fee validation mandates (KPPA) and explicit peer-percentile fee disclosures (LACERS) signals that the tolerance window for underperformance is narrowing alongside cost scrutiny.

## 3. Portfolio Architecture Evolution: From Asset Class Silos to Total Portfolio and Risk-Factor Frameworks

Several large plans are actively restructuring how they think about portfolio construction, moving away from static asset class buckets toward integrated risk and factor frameworks. **SIB-ND (~$8B, ND)** is explicitly evolving toward "Advanced Funds Management," separating alpha from market exposures, with a three-phase roadmap culminating in overlay and portable alpha frameworks ([source](?doc=1078)). The Equity 2.0 initiative delivered 18.0% in 2025 versus a 17.1% benchmark, and a Fixed Income 2.0 review is now underway ([source](?doc=1078)). The board also approved exploration of AI tools to strengthen investment analysis ([source](?doc=1078)).

**IPERS (~$41B, IA)** adopted Portfolio C, eliminating its 5% Global Smart Beta allocation and reallocating 2% to core fixed income and 3% to public credit ([source](?doc=82)). CEO Greg Samorajski flagged concern over a tracking error of just 23 basis points against a 150 bps target and 300 bps upper limit — a signal that the fund is running far too passive relative to its active risk budget ([source](?doc=82)). Three new absolute return/portable alpha managers were hired: **Florin Court Capital**, **Capital Fund Management SA** (CFM ISTEC Program), and **AHL Partners LLP** (AHL Cat Bond Program) ([source](?doc=82)).

**CalPERS (~$502B, CA)** is transitioning to a Total Portfolio Approach beginning FY2026-2027, replacing its Strategic Asset Allocation model with a simple 75/25 Global Equity/Global Bond Reference Portfolio as the performance benchmark ([source](?doc=105)). Incentive compensation is being restructured accordingly, with a five-year phase-in blending TPA and SAA results, and a new Collaboration metric introduced for investment team members ([source](?doc=105)). The FY2026-27 budget allocates **$43.5 million** for annual incentive awards and **$13.6 million** for long-term incentive awards ([source](?doc=104)).

*Sources: [SIB-ND — Board Pack — April 09, 2026](?doc=1078), [IPERS — Board Pack — March 27, 2026](?doc=82), [CalPERS — Agenda — April 02, 2026](?doc=105), [CalPERS — Agenda — April 13, 2026](?doc=104)*

**Bottom line:** The shift from asset class benchmarking to total portfolio and risk-factor frameworks is no longer a leading-edge practice — it is becoming a governance expectation at large plans, with compensation structures now being redesigned to follow.

## 4. Fixed Income and Risk Mitigation Portfolios Are Earning Their Keep — But Long-Duration Remains a Drag

The macro backdrop — Fed funds rate held at 3.50%–3.75% at the March 2026 meeting, February 2026 CPI at 2.4%, and core PCE sticky at approximately 3.1% — is creating a differentiated environment within fixed income allocations ([source](?doc=84), [source](?doc=1078)). **LACERA (~$75B, CA)** reported its Risk Reduction and Mitigation portfolio (24% total fund target) returned 6.8% versus a 6.0% benchmark for the one year ending February 2026, with Investment Grade Bonds returning 6.4% versus a 6.3% benchmark ([source](?doc=85)). The RRM portfolio provides 37 months of benefit payments in liquidity, excluding hedge funds ([source](?doc=85)).

However, Long-Term Government Bonds — with an effective duration of 13.9 years — posted an inception-to-date return of -6.4% annualized since November 2021, a direct consequence of the 2022 rate spike ([source](?doc=85)). This is a live illustration of the liability-hedging trade-off: duration extension protects against falling rates but imposes severe mark-to-market losses in rising rate environments.

**LACERS (~$25B, CA)** renewed **Loomis Sayles** for three years on its **$416 million** High Yield Fixed Income portfolio, which returned 7.75% versus a 7.18% benchmark for the one year ending February 28, 2026 ([source](?doc=1089)). High yield is performing; long duration is not. **DPFP (~$3B, TX)** disclosed Fixed Income & Cash significantly overweight at 19.4% actual versus a 13% target — a **$140 million** excess — while private equity sits severely underweight ([source](?doc=1017)). The overweight likely reflects deployment timing rather than strategic intent, but it is a drag on return potential in the current environment.

*Sources: [LACERA — Agenda — April 08, 2026](?doc=85), [LACERS — Agenda — April 14, 2026](?doc=1089), [DPFP — Agenda — April 02, 2026](?doc=1017)*

**Bottom line:** Short-to-intermediate credit is delivering; long-duration government bonds remain structurally challenged at current yield levels. Plans with large fixed income overweights relative to target — particularly those building toward private markets targets — should treat the overweight as a deployment queue, not a strategic position.

## 5. Geopolitical and Macro Volatility Is Reshaping Near-Term Risk Posture Without Altering Strategic Direction

Multiple plans flagged geopolitical and macro risks explicitly in this window, but the pattern of decisions shows boards are absorbing volatility rather than repositioning. **ASRS (~$55B, AZ)** noted that an Iran attack in early March 2026 caused the S&P 500 to fall 2%, MSCI EAFE and EM to fall nearly 7%, WTI oil to rise above $90/barrel (up approximately 35% for the week), and the VIX to spike to 29 ([source](?doc=891)). Despite this, ASRS's 20-year expected return declined from 8.4% to 7.4% based on updated capital markets assumptions — a structural concern that predates the geopolitical event ([source](?doc=891)).

**LACERA (~$75B, CA)** cited ongoing U.S. tariff and trade policy uncertainty and geopolitical tensions as market themes, while noting the Fed held rates at 3.50%–3.75% for a second consecutive pause ([source](?doc=84)). **SIB-ND (~$8B, ND)** reported U.S. equities down approximately 4% YTD as of March 31, 2026, with the S&P 500 down 4.3% and MSCI World ex-US down 0.9% ([source](?doc=1078)). **DPFP (~$3B, TX)** reported a total fund YTD return of -2.41% through March 31, 2026, with the public portfolio down 1.7% ([source](?doc=1017)). **TMRS (~$40B, TX)** noted that NEPC projects a 3.7% illiquidity premium for private equity versus U.S. large-cap equity over a 10-year forward horizon, with U.S. large-cap equity projected at 4.5% and private equity at 8.2% ([source](?doc=504)).

Notably, none of the plans in this window made defensive tactical shifts in response to the volatility — commitment activity continued, manager searches proceeded, and asset allocation reviews remained on their scheduled timelines.

*Sources: [ASRS — Agenda — March 26, 2026](?doc=891), [LACERA — Agenda — April 08, 2026](?doc=84), [SIB-ND — Board Pack — April 09, 2026](?doc=1078), [DPFP — Agenda — April 02, 2026](?doc=1017), [TMRS — Agenda — March 26, 2026](?doc=504)*

**Bottom line:** Boards are treating Q1 2026 equity weakness and geopolitical shocks as noise within a long-horizon framework — the absence of defensive repositioning across this peer group is itself a signal that strategic conviction in private markets and diversified allocations remains intact.
