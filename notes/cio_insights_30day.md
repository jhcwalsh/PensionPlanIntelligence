## 1. Private Markets Deployment Accelerates Despite Underweight Positions

The most consistent investment action across this window is active private markets commitment pacing, with multiple plans deploying capital into private equity, private credit, and real assets simultaneously. **WSIB ($200B, WA)** approved commitments to **Charterhouse Capital Partners XII** (pan-European mid-market buyout targeting €1.5–2.0bn), **Spark Capital IX** and **Spark Capital Growth VI** (venture and venture growth, from a firm with **$8.7B** committed AUM), and **Monarch Capital Partners VII** (private credit/innovation) at its April 16 meeting ([source](?doc=1084)). **LACERA ($75B, CA)** disclosed CIO-approved commitments of **$350M** to **AE Industrial Partners IV**, **$41M** co-investment alongside **Kingswood Capital Management**, and **$25M** alongside **Primary Venture Partners** ([source](?doc=84)). **MN SBI ($161B, MN)** ratified eleven private markets commitments totaling up to **$1.975 billion** approved in October 2025, spanning **Advent International GPE XI** ($150M), **Blackstone Supplemental Account – M** ($300M), **Strategic Partners X** ($250M), **Permira IX** ($250M), **TPG Partners X** ($150M), and others ([source](?doc=684)). **SJCERA ($2B, CA)** committed **$50M** to **Focus Senior Housing Fund III** and **$50M** to **Pemberton Strategic Credit Fund IV** ([source](?doc=2547)). **ERSRI ($10B, RI)** approved up to **$50M** to **L Squared Capital Partners V** for its pooled trust ([source](?doc=1945)). **DPFP ($3B, TX)** committed **$35M** to **Blackstone Strategic Partners X** and up to **$15M** to **Lexington Continuation Vehicle Investors**, both private equity secondaries, approved 9-1 ([source](?doc=1017)).

The signal beneath the activity: several plans are committing from a position of structural underweight. DPFP disclosed private equity at **1.8% actual vs. 6% target** and total private markets at **11.2% actual vs. 18% target**, a **-$148M** variance ([source](?doc=1017)). **IPERS ($41B, IA)** noted private credit and private real assets both below target allocations as of December 2025, and approved a **CY2026 commitment plan** of up to **$750M** in private real assets, **$700M** in private equity, and **$575M** in private credit ([source](?doc=82)). The secondaries-heavy tilt at DPFP and LACERA is consistent with a broader market observation from **ASRS ($55B, AZ)**, whose consultant NEPC noted the private equity secondaries market was on pace for record transaction volume in 2025, with continuation funds also at record levels and PE distribution rates recovering from a low of **11% of NAV to 13%** ([source](?doc=891)).

*Sources: [WSIB — Agenda — April 16, 2026](?doc=1084), [WSIB — Board Pack — April 02, 2026](?doc=682), [LACERA — Agenda — April 08, 2026](?doc=84), [MN SBI — Board Pack — March 26, 2026](?doc=684), [SJCERA — Agenda — April 10, 2026](?doc=2547), [ERSRI — Board Pack — April 08, 2026](?doc=1945), [DPFP — Agenda — April 02, 2026](?doc=1017), [IPERS — Board Pack — March 27, 2026](?doc=82), [ASRS — Agenda — March 26, 2026](?doc=891)*

**Bottom line:** The pace and breadth of private markets commitments — spanning buyout, secondaries, venture, senior housing, and private credit — reflects genuine structural underweight positions being worked down, not opportunistic timing. Plans with mature pacing programs (LACERA, MN SBI, WSIB) are deploying at scale; smaller plans (DPFP, SJCERA) are building programs from near-zero. The secondaries concentration is a rational response to the liquidity and vintage-year diversification advantages in the current environment.

## 2. Investment Consultant Transitions Signal Governance Reassessment

Two plans in this window replaced their primary investment consultant, a low-frequency event that typically signals dissatisfaction with strategic alignment or a desire for fresh perspective ahead of an asset allocation review. **OPERS-OK ($10B, OK)** approved the transition of investment consulting services from **Verus Advisory** to **Cerity Partners LLC** at its April 16 meeting ([source](?doc=2067)). **CTPF ($18B, IL)** conducted due diligence on **Cerity LLC** as a potential replacement investment consultant at its April 9 Investment Committee meeting, with a watch list board action also on the agenda ([source](?doc=2048)). The appearance of Cerity in both searches — as the incoming consultant at OPERS-OK and as a finalist at CTPF — is notable and suggests the firm is actively competing for public pension mandates.

Beyond these transitions, **NVPERS ($55B, NV)** placed an Investment Services Agreement with **Callan** on its April 16 board agenda for possible approval ([source](?doc=1533)), and **SIB-ND ($8B, ND)** held a competitive evaluation of performance measurement consultant proposals in executive session on April 14 ([source](?doc=1077)). The pattern across four plans in a single 30-day window suggests a broader reassessment cycle is underway, likely triggered by the combination of a volatile Q1 2026 market environment and upcoming asset allocation studies at several funds.

*Sources: [OPERS-OK — Agenda — April 16, 2026](?doc=2067), [CTPF — Agenda — April 09, 2026](?doc=2048), [NVPERS — Agenda — April 16, 2026](?doc=1533), [SIB-ND — Board Pack — April 14, 2026](?doc=1077)*

**Bottom line:** Consultant transitions cluster around inflection points — asset allocation reviews, leadership changes, or performance disappointments. Managers and consultants competing for public pension mandates should note that Cerity Partners is emerging as a credible challenger in the mid-size plan segment. Plans approaching their own consultant review cycles should treat this window as a signal that the competitive landscape is shifting.

## 3. Q1 2026 Market Stress Is Visible in the Data — But Plans Are Not Repositioning

The macro backdrop embedded in board materials is unambiguous: U.S. equities were under meaningful pressure in Q1 2026. **SIB-ND** reported the Russell 3000 down **-4.0%** and the S&P 500 down **-4.3%** year-to-date through March 31, 2026 ([source](?doc=1078)). **DPFP** reported a total fund return of **-2.41%** YTD through March 31, 2026, with the public portfolio down **-1.7%** ([source](?doc=1017)). **MSRS ($25B, MN)** DC plan data showed U.S. large cap equity down **-3.96% to -4.34%** YTD through Q1 2026 ([source](?doc=1618)). **NYC TRS ($260B, NY)** noted U.S. GDP slowed to **0.5% annualized** in Q4 2025 from **4.4%** the prior quarter, with CPI rising to **3.3%** in March 2026 ([source](?doc=1956)). **ASRS** flagged a geopolitical shock — an Iran attack causing the S&P 500 to fall **2%** and MSCI EAFE and EM to fall **nearly 7%** in early March 2026, with WTI oil spiking above **$90/barrel** ([source](?doc=891)). **FPPA ($6B, CO)** reported a U.S. equity MTD return of **-0.9%** in February 2026 against gold up **21.5%** YTD ([source](?doc=2232)).

Critically, none of the plans in this dataset made defensive repositioning moves in response to this volatility. **LACERA** increased its Risk Reduction and Mitigation portfolio target from 19% to 24% in its 2024 Strategic Asset Allocation — a prior-cycle decision — and that portfolio returned **6.8% vs. a 6.0% benchmark** over the one year ending February 2026 ([source](?doc=85)). **IPERS** flagged a tracking error of only **23 basis points** against a target of **150 bps**, raising concern about insufficient active risk rather than too much ([source](?doc=82)). Plans are staying the course.

*Sources: [SIB-ND — Board Pack — April 09, 2026](?doc=1078), [DPFP — Agenda — April 02, 2026](?doc=1017), [MSRS — Performance — March 31, 2026](?doc=1618), [NYC Retirement — Agenda — April 16, 2026](?doc=1956), [ASRS — Agenda — March 26, 2026](?doc=891), [FPPA — Board Pack — April 09, 2026](?doc=2232), [LACERA — Agenda — April 08, 2026](?doc=85), [IPERS — Board Pack — March 27, 2026](?doc=82)*

**Bottom line:** The Q1 2026 drawdown in U.S. equities is real and visible in board materials, but it has not triggered tactical repositioning at any plan in this dataset. ## 4. Climate Commitments Persist, But Execution Gaps Are Widening

**NYC TRS** provided the most detailed climate data in this window. The fund reported a **49% reduction** in Scope 1 and 2 financed emissions intensity since its 2019 baseline, surpassing its **32%** interim target ([source](?doc=1956)). However, the science-based target coverage goal of **70%** of financed emissions by FY2025 was not met — only **27%** of financed emissions were covered by validated targets as of June 30, 2025 ([source](?doc=1956)). TRS holds **$7.34 billion** in climate solution investments against a **$19 billion** 2035 goal, with **Nvidia, Microsoft, and Amazon** comprising approximately **30%** of total climate solutions in FY2025 ([source](?doc=1956)). TRS explicitly decided not to set Scope 3 portfolio-level reduction targets, citing data quality and double-counting concerns ([source](?doc=1956)).

**WSIB** included energy transition investing as a formal agenda topic at its April 16 meeting alongside its private equity commitments ([source](?doc=1084)). **NYCERS ($88B, NY)** renewed its membership in the **Ceres Investor Network on Climate Risk** for **$13,995** annually ([source](?doc=167)). **LACERA** submitted a comment letter to the UK Financial Conduct Authority regarding proposed sustainability reporting requirements ([source](?doc=84)).

The pattern across these plans is consistent: headline emissions metrics are improving, but the harder targets — Scope 3, science-based coverage, and absolute dollar deployment toward climate solutions — are lagging. Federal policy headwinds were explicitly cited by NYC TRS as an "ongoing challenge to pension fund climate action efforts" ([source](?doc=1956)).

*Sources: [NYC Retirement — Agenda — April 16, 2026](?doc=1956), [WSIB — Agenda — April 16, 2026](?doc=1084), [NYCERS — Agenda — April 09, 2026](?doc=167), [LACERA — Agenda — April 08, 2026](?doc=84)*

**Bottom line:** The gap between headline emissions reductions and harder implementation targets is the defining tension in public pension climate programs right now. Plans that have set ambitious 2035 goals — like NYC TRS's $19 billion climate solutions target — face a compounding challenge: the easy reductions are largely done, the remaining targets require active portfolio construction choices, and the political environment is making those choices harder to defend publicly.

## 5. Watch-List Manager Renewals Reveal the Limits of Termination as a Tool

A recurring pattern in this window is the renewal of managers on formal watch status — a decision that reflects the practical difficulty of terminating and replacing underperforming active managers in illiquid or specialized mandates. **LACERS ($25B, CA)** renewed **Segall Bryant & Hamill** for one year despite watch status since August 2024 and since-inception underperformance of **14.34% vs. 15.89%** benchmark ([source](?doc=1089)). The firm's indirect ownership by **Mubadala Capital LP** following the 2025 acquisition of CI Financial Corp was noted as a governance consideration. LACERS also renewed **Informed Momentum Company** (formerly EAM Investors) for one year despite watch status since June 2024, noting the co-founder's retirement and firm rebrand — though the manager posted strong fiscal year-to-date outperformance of **+14.5% vs. Russell 2000 Growth** as of February 28, 2026 ([source](?doc=1089)). **LAFPP ($29B, CA)** renewed **MacKay Shields LLC** for three years despite watch status as of December 31, 2025 due to below-median 3-year peer performance, citing a management fee of approximately **33 bps** ($2.5 million) at the **23rd percentile** among peers and the concurrence of consultant **RVK** ([source](?doc=2069)).

The common thread: in each case, staff and consultants recommended renewal on the basis of fee competitiveness, recent performance improvement, or the cost and disruption of replacement. This is rational but creates a structural problem — watch lists lose credibility if they rarely result in termination. The LACERS data is particularly instructive: SBH's since-inception underperformance is attributed to specific market episodes (Q4 2020 low-quality rally, 2021 meme stocks), a narrative that can be applied indefinitely to defer accountability.

*Sources: [LACERS — Agenda — April 14, 2026](?doc=1089), [LAFPP — Agenda — April 02, 2026](?doc=2069)*

**Bottom line:** Watch lists are most effective when boards establish explicit time limits and outcome criteria at the point of placement — not at the point of renewal. Plans that allow watch status to persist across multiple renewal cycles without a defined exit trigger are effectively converting a monitoring tool into a retention mechanism. Boards should require staff to present a termination scenario alongside every watch-list renewal recommendation.
