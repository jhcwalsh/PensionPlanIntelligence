# Insights: 2026 Year in Review
*Synthesized from 3 approved monthly briefings (2026-01-01 – 2026-12-31)*
*Generated: July 03, 2026*

---

The first half of 2026 was defined by sustained private-markets deployment across plans of every size, a widening debate over actuarial return assumptions, and a wave of governance modernization spanning consultant reviews, manager restructuring, and policy overhauls. Strong fiscal-year 2025 returns — often exceeding benchmarks at the total-fund level — coexisted with persistent real estate weakness and notable private equity benchmark gaps, creating tension between backward-looking performance narratives and forward-looking assumption-setting. Plans simultaneously executed structural portfolio shifts, exited smart beta and active small-cap mandates, and confronted leadership transitions and legislative disclosure risk.

## 1. Private Markets Deployment Accelerates Across Strategies and Plan Sizes

Private markets commitment activity was the single most persistent operational theme across all three months, spanning buyout, growth equity, venture capital, private credit, real assets, and infrastructure.

In April, **KPPA** (~$20B) recommended $90 million to **Capitol Meridian Partners Fund II**, a North American mid-buyout vehicle focused on aerospace, defense, and government-tech, oversubscribed against a $1.2B target and ~$1.7B hard cap (doc_id=3195). **WSIB** (~$189.7B) approved commitments to **Charterhouse Capital Partners XII**, **Spark Capital IX**, **Spark Capital Growth VI**, and **Monarch Capital Partners VII** (doc_id=3206). **NJDPB** (~$95B) built on a $1B January deployment across five vehicles including $100M to **Gridiron Capital Fund VI**, $150M to **Wind Point Partners XI-A**, $350M to a **Golub Capital** separate account plus $250M in co-investments, and $150M to **Ardian Secondaries Fund IX Infrastructure B** (doc_id=3179).

May broadened the deployment base. **LACERA** (~$75B) approved $100 million to **Dragoneer Select Opportunities Fund, L.P.**, $125 million as a secondary investment in **IDR Core Property Trust**, and $200 million to **Principal Enhanced Property Fund, L.P.** (doc_id=4079). **LACERA** also confirmed a $190 million commitment to **Bregal Sagemount V, L.P.** (doc_id=3474). **LACERS** (~$25B) considered commitments across eleven funds totaling over $335 million, including up to $80 million to **Hayfin Tactical Solutions Fund (US Parallel) LP**, up to $50 million to **Francisco Partners VIII, L.P.**, and up to $40 million to **New Enterprise Associates 19, L.P.** (doc_id=4210). **KPPA** approved an additional $100 million to **ITE Rail Fund, L.P.** following full deployment of an initial $100 million commitment by October 2025 (doc_id=4207).

June sustained the pace. **LACERA** approved $200 million to **Net Lease Real Estate Fund II, L.P.**, $200 million to **Court Square Capital V, L.P.**, and approximately $400 million (€341 million) to **Triton Fund 6 SCSp** (doc_id=4504). **PSERS** (~$73B) approved investments in **TPG Peppertree Fund XI-A, L.P.** and **PAI Mid-Market Fund II SCSp** (doc_id=4610). **CalPERS** (~$502B) maintained private equity pacing at approximately $15.8 billion per year for four consecutive years, with at least 40% of commitments directed to co-investments and separately managed accounts, generating estimated fee savings of approximately $400 million per $1 billion co-invested (doc_id=4789). **Metro Nashville ERS** (~$2B) approved $20 million to **LGT Crown Europe Small Buyouts VII**, $35 million to **Savano Capital Partners IV**, and $40 million to **O'Brien Staley Partners Value Add Fund V** (doc_id=4856).

A notable counterpoint emerged in private credit: **IPERS** (~$41B) reported private credit 2026 commitments at $0 of a $450 million plan, with new commitments on hold due to the slow M&A market (doc_id=4588). **MainePERS** (~$18B) noted that **Alternative Credit** stands significantly below its 15% policy target at 7.9% of fund, while **Private Equity** is above its 10% strategic target at 15.1% (doc_id=4006).

## 2. Strong 2025 Returns Mask Persistent Asset-Class Divergence — Real Estate and Private Equity Lag

Headline total-fund returns for calendar or fiscal year 2025 were robust across reporting plans, but asset-class dispersion was wide and consistent: real estate dragged, private equity lagged benchmarks at several plans, and alternatives such as commodities and emerging markets equities outperformed.

**TRS Texas** (~$200B), reported at $235.2 billion as of December 31, 2025, returned 15.9% against a 14.1% benchmark for calendar year 2025, ranking top quartile among peers (doc_id=3148). Three-year and five-year returns were 11.1% (benchmark: 9.0%) and 7.8% (benchmark: 6.1%) respectively (doc_id=3148). Commodities returned 102.6%, Absolute Return 18.4% vs. 8.7% benchmark, and Stable Value Hedge Funds 13.3% vs. 7.1% (doc_id=3148). Real Estate was the sole laggard at -3.5% vs. a 3.2% benchmark (doc_id=3148). **WSIB**'s Commingled Trust Fund returned 11.47% for full-year 2025, with all 11 fixed income portfolios outperforming their benchmarks (doc_id=3207). **CTPF** (~$18B) returned 12.05% gross in FY2025, led by international equity at 16.93% and domestic equity at 15.86% (doc_id=3024). **DPFP** (~$3B) posted a 15.49% market value return for FY2025 versus a 6.50% assumed rate (doc_id=4062). **IPERS** reported a CY2025 total fund return of 13.75% versus a 13.4% benchmark (doc_id=4588).

The private equity benchmark gap became a recurring concern from May onward. **PERS-OR** (~$110B) reported a 1-year return of 8.40% versus a policy benchmark of 13.10% for the period ending March 2026, with Private Equity returning 5.41% against a 24.43% benchmark (doc_id=4081). **IPERS** Private Equity returned 10.9% versus a 22.2% benchmark for the one-year period ending March 31, 2026 (doc_id=4588). **VRS** (~$110B) attributed fiscal year-to-date underperformance primarily to Private Equity selection effects, while Public Equity returned 8.9% vs. 7.3% and Credit Strategies returned 6.9% vs. 2.9% (doc_id=4591). **LACERA** reported a 1-year net return of 11.8% against a 13.4% policy benchmark as of March 2026 (doc_id=3474).

Emerging markets equities were a standout in the opposite direction. The **NYC Teachers' Retirement System** reported Emerging Markets Equities at 43.0% FYTD as of May 11, 2026 (doc_id=4057), rising to 49.0% FYTD as of June 18, 2026 (doc_id=4825). **NYCERS** reported Emerging Markets Equity at 30.5% versus 29.55% over one year as of March 31, 2026 (doc_id=4644).

**Practical implication:** The wide spread between private equity benchmark returns and actual returns at PERS-OR, IPERS, and VRS warrants peer review of valuation lag and benchmark construction methodology; plans still overweight core real estate should review benchmark-relative drag before the next allocation cycle.

## 3. Actuarial Assumption Divergence: The 7.00% vs. 6.75% Fault Line

The investment return assumption debate sharpened across May and June, with plans receiving similar actuarial advice yet reaching opposing conclusions.

In May, **LAFPP** (~$29B) was scheduled to consider reducing its assumption from 7.00% to 6.75%, with a 55% confidence level cited in the actuarial recommendation (doc_id=4134). **ERSRI** (~$10B) considered its 2026 Actuarial Experience Study, with **GRS** recommending no change to the 7.0% investment return assumption; the Teachers funded ratio stands at 67.9% and State Employees at 63.9% (doc_id=4229). **TRS Texas** unanimously adopted the 2026 Actuarial Experience Study confirming a 7% investment return assumption (doc_id=4151). **MainePERS** adopted actuarial assumptions recommended by **Cheiron** from the PLD Consolidated Plan experience study (doc_id=4008).

By June, the fault line crystallized. **LAFPP** voted 7-0 to maintain its 7.00% assumption despite **Segal**'s recommendation to reduce it to 6.75% (doc_id=4719). **LACERS** (~$25B) was scheduled to consider reducing its assumption from 7.00% to 6.75%, a change recommended by **Segal** based on a July 2022–June 2025 experience study; adoption would increase the employer contribution rate by approximately 3.70% (~$113.1 million annually on ~$3.1 billion covered payroll) and raise total UAAL by approximately $997.1 million, with the retirement plan funded ratio projected to decline from 105.77% to 100.97% (doc_id=4717). **IPERS** retained its 7.00% return assumption following its actuary **Cavanaugh Macdonald**'s recommendation, noting that **Wilshire**'s 10-year nominal return expectation of 6.2% is below the current assumption (doc_id=4588).

Funded status pressures provided context. **DPFP** (~$3B) remains severely underfunded at a 39.08% funded ratio on a market value basis as of January 1, 2026, with a total actuarial accrued liability of $5.76 billion against market value assets of $2.25 billion and projected full funding not until 2053 (doc_id=4060). **CTPF**'s 47.9% funded ratio and $15.0B net pension liability against a $28.6B total pension liability were disclosed in its FY2025 CAFR (doc_id=3024). In contrast, **OCERS** (~$25B) reported a smoothed investment return of 8.88% exceeding its 7.00% assumption, with its funded ratio improving from 83.83% to 86.55% on a valuation basis and from 84.18% to 92.71% on a market value basis (doc_id=4557). **VRS** stress testing showed a five-year average return of 1.95% under a Tech Bubble scenario and 0.86% under a Secular Stagflation scenario, against the 6.75% assumed rate of return (doc_id=4553).

*Sources: [LAFPP — Agenda — May 21, 2026](?doc=4134), [ERSRI — Board Pack — May 20, 2026](?doc=4229), [TRS Texas — Board Pack — May 01, 2026](?doc=4151), [MainePERS — Agenda — May 14, 2026](?doc=4008), [LAFPP — Minutes — May 21, 2026](?doc=4719), [LACERS — Agenda — June 23, 2026](?doc=4717), [IPERS — Board Pack — June 18, 2026](?doc=4588), [DPFP — Board Pack — May 08, 2026](?doc=4060), [CTPF — CAFR — December 31, 2026](?doc=3024), [OCERS — Agenda — June 15, 2026](?doc=4557), [VRS — Board Pack — June 17, 2026](?doc=4553)*

**Practical implication:** With LACERS, LAFPP, and IPERS all receiving the same directional actuarial advice yet reaching different conclusions, boards should document the specific rationale — horizon weighting, credibility adjustments, or sponsor capacity — that drives assumption retention or reduction, as divergent outcomes from identical consultant recommendations will attract scrutiny.

## 4. Governance Modernization: Consultant Transitions, AI Frameworks, and Proxy Reform

Governance reform was a cross-cutting theme across all three months, encompassing consultant reviews, AI risk integration, proxy policy updates, and legislative disclosure risk.

In April, **TRS Texas** approved a revised Proxy Voting Policy requiring written reports to the Board whenever TRS votes differently from its proxy advisor or against company management (doc_id=3150). The FY2027–31 Strategic Plan adds a new AI objective (G4.O7) with five strategies and a Pension Data Warehouse Project, and a new AI risk category was added to the Enterprise Risk Management Stoplight Report (doc_id=3151). TRS Texas placed the selection of a new **Board Investment Consultant** — currently **Meketa Investment Group** — on the April 30 agenda (doc_id=3145). **TRS-OK** (~$19B) held an April 22 Investment Committee meeting covering an AI technology portfolio exposure assessment and final selection of investment consultants (doc_id=3196). **NJDPB** began selecting a new Real Estate Investment Consultant (doc_id=3177).

In May, consultant governance intensified. **CTPF** voted 9-1 to extend **Callan**'s investment consulting contract for one year and 1-9 against proceeding with **Cerity Partners** as the new investment consultant, citing concerns about private equity ownership and conflicts of interest; a new RFP was approved 10-1 (doc_id=3973). **LACERA** extended **StepStone Group LP** as its private equity investment consulting services agreement for one year by a 6-0 vote (doc_id=3474). **SMCERA** (~$5B) engaged **Meketa Investment Group** as new investment consultant following a competitive RFP process in December 2025, with FY2026-27 fees budgeted at $675,000 (doc_id=4157).

In June, **SIB-ND** (~$8B) awarded the Performance and Benchmark Consultant contract to **Callan** effective June 5, 2026, replacing **Verus**, which had held a benchmark contract at $133,000/year (doc_id=4459). On the legislative front, **OCERS** (~$25B) adopted an Oppose position on **SB 1319 (Durazo)**, a California bill that would expand CPRA disclosure requirements for public pension funds' alternative investments (doc_id=3972). **CalPERS** also had SB 1319 as an action item on its May 18, 2026 board agenda (doc_id=3953).

## 5. Manager Restructuring: Smart Beta Exits, International Equity Searches, and MWBE Mandates

Manager roster changes across the three months revealed a pattern of exiting smart beta and active small-cap mandates while expanding international equity and MWBE-aligned exposures.

In April, **WSIB** hired **Life Cycle Investment Partners** for a new Global Select Equity mandate (doc_id=3206). **KPPA** reviewed a **Franklin Templeton** All Country World ex-U.S. public equity mandate and a **ClearBridge Investments** ACWI ex-US International Growth strategy (doc_id=3195). **CTPF** hired two MWBE private credit managers and increased allocations to one MWBE non-core real estate manager and two MWBE private equity managers during FY2025 (doc_id=3024).

In May, **CCPF** (~$12B) approved **Driehaus Capital Management LLC** and **Causeway Capital Management LLC** for international small cap equity mandates of up to $225 million each, by a 7-2 vote (doc_id=3706). **CTPF** voted 8-0-1 to approve RFPs for International Small Cap Growth and Domestic Small Cap Value managers after first-look presentations from **Conestoga Capital Advisors**, **Phocas Financial Corporation**, and **William Blair** (doc_id=3974). **TRS-OK** placed potential terminations of **Frontier Capital**, **Geneva Capital**, and **Northern Trust** index accounts on its agenda as part of a domestic equity portfolio structure review (doc_id=4172). **LACERS** approved a 3-year contract renewal with **Loomis, Sayles & Company L.P.** for active High Yield Fixed Income on a $416 million allocation by a 6-0 vote, and a 1-year renewal with **Informed Momentum Company LLC** for active U.S. Small Cap Growth Equities ($132 million) despite the manager being on watch status for underperformance, also by a 6-0 vote (doc_id=4210).

In June, **LAFPP** approved a comprehensive U.S. Equity Structure Implementation Plan terminating **Northern Trust Investments, Inc.** (STOXX USA 900 Min Variance Smart Beta), **AllianceBernstein L.P.** (U.S. Systematic Value Smart Beta), **AllianceBernstein L.P.**, **Channing Capital Management, LLC**, and **Westwood Management Corp** (Domestic Small Cap Value Equity), and **Palisade Capital Management, LP** (Domestic Small Cap Core Equity), while extending contracts with **Boston Partners Global Investors, Inc.**, **Denali Advisors, LLC**, **Phocas Financial Corporation**, **Pacific Investment Management Company, LLC**, and **Frontier Capital Management Company, LLC** through December 31, 2029 (doc_id=4719). **DPERS** (~$12B) terminated its **T. ## 6. Benchmark and Policy Framework Overhauls

Multiple plans undertook structural investment policy changes, spanning benchmark adoption, internal management expansion, and portfolio construction redesign.

In April, **TRS Texas** moved to amend its IPS to raise the Public Markets Total Manager Organization Market Value Limit from 3% to 5% and the IIC Approval Authority per Manager Organization from 6% to 8%, driven by two unnamed managers breaching existing thresholds through strong performance rather than active allocation decisions (doc_id=3150). The Audit Committee separately flagged two passive IPS violations in Q1 2026 arising from the same dynamic — single manager threshold breaches from market appreciation (doc_id=3151). **CTPF** completed a $2.7B rebalancing from international equity to fixed income and private credit during FY2025, consistent with its policy target of 29.5% fixed income and 8% private equity (doc_id=3024).

In June, **SIB-ND** ratified updated Cash Overlay Portfolio guidelines expanding permitted instruments to broader fixed income and equity markets including forwards, swaps, and ETFs (doc_id=4459). Internal equity AUM grew from a $60 million pilot to $2.2 billion and internal fixed income AUM grew to approximately $2.1 billion, with the combined internal program managing approximately $4.2 billion out of $28.8 billion total AUM and generating an estimated $16.3 million in FY2026 savings (doc_id=4459). SIB-ND adopted the **Morningstar LSTA U.S. Leveraged Loan Index plus 100 basis points** as the benchmark for the pension funds' Private Credit allocation by a 5-0 vote (doc_id=4459). **CalPERS** advanced its Total Portfolio Approach set to launch July 1, 2026, replacing the Strategic Asset Allocation with a reference portfolio framework and setting an aggregate active risk limit of 400 basis points and an aggregate leverage limit of 20% (doc_id=4789). **WSIB** proposed a core-satellite private credit framework across direct lending (40%), opportunistic credit (40%), distressed debt (10%), and levered strategies (10%), with a proposed benchmark of the S&P UBS Leveraged Loan Index and VettaFI U.S. High Yield Index plus 200 bps (doc_id=4590). **VRS** brought its FY2027 DB Plan Strategic Asset Allocation to the June 17 board alongside proposed benchmark changes for Credit Strategies, Diversifying Strategies, Private Equity, and Private Investment Partnerships, with adjusted incentive hurdles validated by independent consultant **Mercer** (doc_id=4591).

*Sources: [TRS Texas — Board Pack — April 30, 2026 (3)](?doc=3150), [TRS Texas — Board Pack — April 30, 2026 (4)](?doc=3151), [CTPF — CAFR — December 31, 2026](?doc=3024), [SIB-ND — Board Pack — June 12, 2026](?doc=4459), [CalPERS — Board Pack — June 15, 2026](?doc=4789), [WSIB — Board Pack — June 04, 2026](?doc=4590), [VRS — Board Pack — June 17, 2026](?doc=4591)*

**Practical implication:** IPS concentration limits written for a lower-return environment are increasingly being breached passively by outperforming managers; plans that have not reviewed private credit benchmarks or SAA structures since pre-2024 should assess whether current policy documents reflect current market conditions and risk tolerances.

## 7. CIO Transitions and Leadership Continuity Risk

*Emerging signal — supported by May only, though two distinct plans are affected.*

Two plans disclosed CIO departures in May. **LACERS** is managing a CIO transition following CIO **Rod June**'s announced retirement effective July 2026, with board recruitment pending Mayoral and City Council approval of a civil service exemption re-authorization (doc_id=3971). **ERS-HI** (~$23B) held a special meeting on April 24, 2026, selecting a candidate for an interim CIO position effective June 1, 2026, following the departure of CIO **Kristin Varela**, and approved issuing an RFQ for an executive search firm to recruit a permanent CIO (doc_id=3613).

*Sources: [LACERS — Agenda — May 12, 2026](?doc=3971), [ERS-HI — Minutes — April 24, 2026](?doc=3613)*

**Practical implication:** Concurrent CIO vacancies at two mid-sized plans during a period of active private markets deployment and policy overhaul heighten execution risk; boards should ensure interim governance protocols preserve investment decision-making continuity during search processes.
