## 1. Private Equity Deployment Accelerates Across Mid-Market and Specialist Strategies

April's deal flow confirmed that large and mid-sized plans are actively closing under-allocation gaps in private equity, with a pronounced tilt toward specialist and lower-middle-market mandates.

**KPPA** (~**$20B**, KY) led the week's most notable commitment: a recommended **$90 million** to **Capitol Meridian Partners Fund II**, a North American mid-buyout vehicle focused on aerospace, defense, and government-tech sectors co-founded by former Carlyle partners Brooke Coburn and Adam Palmer. The fund is oversubscribed against a **$1.2B** target and **~$1.7B** hard cap, meaning KPPA's CERS may receive less than requested. Capitol Meridian Fund I (2022 vintage) has returned **15.5%** since inception as of September 2025. The commitment is part of a pacing plan targeting **$600M** in annual PE commitments through 2026 to close an under-allocation gap of **$526M–$964M** against an 8% PE target. (?doc=3195)

**WSIB** (~**$189.7B**, WA) approved commitments to **Charterhouse Capital Partners XII**, **Spark Capital IX**, **Spark Capital Growth VI**, and an Innovation Portfolio commitment to **Monarch Capital Partners VII** at its April 16 meeting. (?doc=3206) Earlier in the year, WSIB committed **$100M** to **Public Pension Capital, LLC** — the fund's first investment with that manager — targeting lower middle market North American service businesses at a **1.28%** management fee and maximum **10%** carry, approved on a 13-1 vote with Treasurer Pellicciotti opposed. (?doc=3207)

**NJDPB** (~**$95B**, NJ) is considering commitments to **1315 Capital Fund IV** and **1315 Capital Emerging Growth and Buyout Fund II** at its April 29 meeting, building on a **$1B** January deployment across five vehicles including **$100M** to **Gridiron Capital Fund VI**, **$150M** to **Wind Point Partners XI-A**, **$350M** to a **Golub Capital** separate account plus **$250M** in co-investments for senior secured private credit, and **$150M** to **Ardian Secondaries Fund IX Infrastructure B**. (?doc=3177, ?doc=3179)

**CTPF** (~**$18B**, IL) disclosed in its FY2025 CAFR that it hired two MWBE private credit managers following a completed asset class structure review, increased allocations to one MWBE non-core real estate manager and two MWBE private equity managers, and conducted a search for a replacement Core Plus fixed income manager. (?doc=3024)

**Practical implication:** Plans with documented under-allocation gaps are executing at pace; managers in aerospace/defense mid-buyout and lower-middle-market services are attracting first-time mandates, and oversubscription dynamics at funds like Capitol Meridian suggest secondary-market or co-investment access may be the only entry point for late-moving allocators.

## 2. Strong 2025 Returns Mask Divergent Asset-Class Outcomes; Real Estate Remains the Outlier

Across the plans reporting full-year 2025 performance, headline returns were robust, but the dispersion at the asset-class level was wide — and real estate was a consistent drag.

**TRS Texas** (~**$200B**, TX) — reported at **$235.2B** — returned **15.9%** against a **14.1%** benchmark for calendar year 2025, ranking top quartile among peers. Three-year and five-year returns were **11.1%** (benchmark: **9.0%**) and **7.8%** (benchmark: **6.1%**) respectively. Standout contributors included Commodities (**102.6%**), Risk Parity (**22.4%** vs. **19.7%** benchmark), Absolute Return (**18.4%** vs. **8.7%**), and Stable Value Hedge Funds (**13.3%** vs. **7.1%**). Real Estate was the sole laggard at **-3.5%** vs. a **3.2%** benchmark. The fund also generated **$541M** in non-investment alpha through investment operations in 2025. (?doc=3148)

**CTPF** returned **12.05%** gross in FY2025, led by international equity (**16.93%**) and domestic equity (**15.86%**), with infrastructure contributing **11.23%**. The 5-year return of **9.66%** exceeded the **9.24%** benchmark. The fund's **47.9%** funded ratio and **$15.0B** net pension liability against a **$28.6B** total pension liability underscore the urgency of sustained return generation. (?doc=3024)

**WSIB**'s Commingled Trust Fund returned **11.47%** for full-year 2025, though Q4 2025 came in at **1.98%** vs. a **2.63%** benchmark. All 11 fixed income portfolios outperformed their benchmarks in 2025, with the Intermediate Bond Fund leading at **8.8%** vs. **7.88%**. (?doc=3207)

The **ITE Rail Fund**, held across KPPA's CERS portfolios, delivered **9.3%** (1-year), **9.6%** (3-year), **9.9%** (5-year), and **10.6%** since inception (October 2014) as of September 2025 — a consistent real-asset performer that prompted a recommended additional **$100M** commitment. (?doc=3195)

**Practical implication:** The broad outperformance of alternatives (commodities, absolute return, infrastructure, rail) relative to traditional real estate in 2025 reinforces the case for diversified real-asset exposure; plans still overweight core real estate should review benchmark-relative drag before the next allocation cycle.

## 3. Portfolio Rebalancing and Structural Shifts Reflect Liability-Driven Urgency

Two plans executed or completed material structural rebalancing during the period, driven by liability profiles and policy target gaps rather than tactical market views.

**CTPF** completed a **$2.7B** rebalancing from international equity to fixed income and private credit during FY2025, consistent with its policy target of **29.5%** fixed income and **8%** private equity. The move reflects the fund's deeply underfunded position — **47.9%** funded ratio — and the need to reduce volatility while maintaining return potential through private credit. (?doc=3024)

**TRS Texas** is amending its IPS to raise the Public Markets Total Manager Organization Market Value Limit from **3% to 5%** and the IIC Approval Authority per Manager Organization from **6% to 8%**, driven by two unnamed managers breaching existing thresholds through strong performance rather than active allocation decisions. The Governance Committee unanimously recommended the amendment, with full Board adoption scheduled for the April 30–May 1 meeting. (?doc=3150, ?doc=3147) The Audit Committee separately flagged two passive IPS violations in Q1 2026 arising from the same dynamic — single manager threshold breaches from market appreciation. (?doc=3151)

**MBOI** (~**$20B**, MT) held a Loan Committee special meeting April 30 to vote on three INTERCAP loans totaling **$7.749M**: **$3.5M** to Corvallis K-12 School District (facility improvements, 15-year term), **$2.332M** to Kalispell School District #5 (boiler replacement, 15-year term), and **$1.917M** to the City of Kalispell (fire pumper trucks, 10-year term). (?doc=3201)

**Practical implication:** IPS concentration limits written for a lower-return environment are increasingly being breached passively by outperforming managers; plans should audit manager-level thresholds now and consider proactive amendments before compliance violations accumulate.

## 4. Governance Modernization: AI Integration, Consultant Transitions, and Proxy Reform

Governance reform was a cross-cutting theme in April, with multiple plans simultaneously reviewing investment consultant relationships, updating proxy policies, and formalizing AI risk frameworks.

**TRS Texas** led the most comprehensive governance overhaul. The Governance Committee approved a revised Proxy Voting Policy requiring written reports to the Board whenever TRS votes differently from its proxy advisor or against company management. The Securities Lending Policy was streamlined to assign borrower credit review solely to the agent **State Street**, where a custodian controls review identified key-person risk in custom services documentation, with an SLA enhancement targeted for December 2027. The FY2027–31 Strategic Plan adds a new AI objective (G4.O7) with five strategies and a Pension Data Warehouse Project, and a new AI risk category was added to the Enterprise Risk Management Stoplight Report. (?doc=3150, ?doc=3151) TRS also placed the selection of a new **Board Investment Consultant** — currently **Meketa Investment Group** — on the April 30 agenda, with deliberation potentially moving to executive session. (?doc=3145)

**TRS-OK** (~**$19B**, OK) held an April 22 Investment Committee meeting covering a Global Equity Structure Review, an AI technology portfolio exposure assessment, and final selection of investment consultants. **Northern Trust** custody contract renewals for securities lending, valuation, and transition management services were also on the agenda. (?doc=3196)

**NJDPB** is selecting a new Real Estate Investment Consultant at its April 29 meeting and reviewing the FY2025 financial statements, with an asset allocation plan review alongside **RVK Inc.** also scheduled. (?doc=3177)

The simultaneous consultant reviews at **TRS Texas**, **TRS-OK**, and **NJDPB** in a single month is notable. Combined with TRS Texas's AI risk framework additions and proxy reform, the pattern suggests boards are recalibrating oversight infrastructure after a period of strong returns exposed gaps in policy design.

**Practical implication:** Consultants up for renewal at multiple large plans simultaneously creates a competitive selection environment; managers and service providers should expect heightened scrutiny of fee structures, reporting capabilities, and AI-related due diligence frameworks as boards modernize their oversight toolkits.

## 5. Manager Hiring Activity Signals Continued Diversification Into Non-Core Mandates

New manager hiring across April reinforced a preference for non-traditional equity exposures, specialist fixed income, and MWBE-aligned mandates.

**WSIB** hired **Life Cycle Investment Partners** for a new Global Select Equity mandate, approved at the April 16 board meeting. (?doc=3206) **KPPA**'s CERS committee reviewed a **Franklin Templeton** All Country World ex-U.S. public equity mandate and a **ClearBridge Investments** ACWI ex-US International Growth strategy as part of ongoing public equity due diligence. (?doc=3195) **CTPF** hired two MWBE private credit managers and increased allocations to one MWBE non-core real estate manager and two MWBE private equity managers during FY2025. (?doc=3024)

The breadth of non-U.S. equity mandates under review — **Franklin Templeton** ACWI ex-U.S., **ClearBridge** ACWI ex-US International Growth, and **Life Cycle Investment Partners** Global Select Equity — across two separate plans in the same month suggests a shared conviction that international equity, which led CTPF's asset-class returns at **16.93%** in FY2025, warrants structural re-examination after years of U.S. equity dominance.

**Practical implication:** Plans reviewing international equity structures should benchmark manager candidates against CTPF's FY2025 international equity return of **16.93%** as a recent peer reference point, and ensure MWBE manager pipelines are embedded in search processes given the regulatory and governance scrutiny visible across Illinois and Washington mandates.
