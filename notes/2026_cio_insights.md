# CIO Insights: 2026 Institutional Trends

*Synthesized from board and investment committee activity across 76 U.S. public pension plans (~$4.2 trillion AUM)*

*Generated: April 17, 2026*

---

## 1. Private Markets Deployment Accelerates Despite Valuation Concerns

The dominant capital deployment story across plans in early 2026 is the sustained — and in several cases intensifying — pace of private markets commitments, even as boards acknowledge elevated purchase price multiples and muted exit activity. **MN SBI ($161B)** approved eleven private markets commitments totaling up to $1.975 billion in a single meeting, spanning buyout (Advent International GPE XI, Permira IX, TPG Partners X), secondaries (Strategic Partners X), and real estate (TA Realty Value-Add Fund XIV) ([source](?doc=684)). **CalPERS ($502B)** deployed $13.8 billion in new PE commitments in the first half of FY2026 alone, with its PE portfolio NAV reaching $110.8 billion — 18.5% of the total fund versus a 17% target ([source](?doc=1011)). **MPSERS ($80B)** committed $1.56 billion across eight fund partnerships in Q4 2025, including a $500 million commitment to Apollo Asset-Backed Finance Fund ([source](?doc=1517)). **ATRS ($20B)** authorized approximately $1.245 billion in new commitments at its February meeting, including $600 million to Axiom Investors and $400 million to Sands Capital Alternatives ([source](?doc=1271)).

Pacing plans confirm this is deliberate, not opportunistic. **KPPA ($20B)** adopted a PE pacing strategy of $600 million annually in 2025–2026, stepping to $500 million in 2027 and $400 million thereafter, targeting an 8% PE allocation from a current underweight ([source](?doc=1022)). **DPFP ($3B)** anticipates 4–6 additional private markets commitments throughout 2026 to close a meaningful gap: private market assets at 11.2% versus an 18% target, a -$148 million variance ([source](?doc=1017)). **IPERS ($41B)** approved a CY2026 private markets commitment plan of up to $2.025 billion across private real assets ($750M), private equity ($700M), and private credit ($575M) ([source](?doc=82)).

Yet boards are not blind to risk. CalPERS noted global median PE buyout purchase price multiples rose to 14.5x EBITDA in 2025 from 13.3x in 2024, with equity contributions now representing 66–69% of deal financing ([source](?doc=1011)). **WSIB ($200B)** committed to Public Pension Capital, LLC partly because of its capped carry at 10% versus the market standard 20%, estimated to generate 2.89% higher annual net returns on identical cash flows ([source](?doc=683)).

*Sources: [CalPERS — Agenda — March 16, 2026](?doc=1011), [MN SBI — Board Pack — March 26, 2026](?doc=684), [MPSERS — Board Pack — March 25, 2026](?doc=1517), [ATRS — Board Pack — February 02, 2026](?doc=1271), [KPPA — Board Pack — February 25, 2026](?doc=1022), [DPFP — Agenda — April 02, 2026](?doc=1017), [IPERS — Board Pack — March 27, 2026](?doc=82), [WSIB — Board Pack — February 05, 2026](?doc=683)*

**Practical implication:** The institutional bid for private assets remains structurally strong, but the fee-consciousness visible at WSIB, CalPERS (41% of capital deployed through no- or low-fee vehicles), and KPPA (hiring Albourne specifically for fee validation at $2,000 per fund) signals that the next phase of private markets growth will be defined by cost discipline, not just access.

## 2. Private Credit Emerges as a Distinct Strategic Allocation

Private credit is transitioning from an opportunistic sleeve to a formal, standalone asset class across multiple plans. **ATRS ($20B)** added a 5% target allocation to private credit in December 2025, updating its Statement of Investment Policy and Real Asset Investment Policies accordingly, with a private credit implementation discussion on the February 2026 agenda ([source](?doc=1271)). **OPERS ($97B)** noted its Private Credit sub-asset class is expected to take more than three years to reach its long-term target of 4% for the DB Fund ([source](?doc=20)). **WSIB ($200B)** presented a private credit implementation timeline update and held an education session on private credit benchmarking at its April 2026 meeting ([source](?doc=1084)). **DCRB ($13B)** approved a $100 million commitment to an unnamed private credit fund ("Fund AP") at its March meeting ([source](?doc=1929)). **MainePERS ($18B)** committed up to $375 million combined to Blackstone Senior Direct Lending Fund Series II and its co-investment vehicle, while its Alternative Credit asset class sits at 7.8% versus a 10% transitional target ([source](?doc=1869)).

The buildout is accompanied by dedicated consulting infrastructure. **LACERS ($25B)** is actively discussing renewal of its private credit consulting contract with **Aksia LLC** and received a Private Credit Program 2026 Strategic Plan ([source](?doc=927)). **DPFP ($3B)** expanded **Albourne's** advisory scope to cover Private Equity, Private Credit, and Real Assets ([source](?doc=1017)).

*Sources: [ATRS — Board Pack — February 02, 2026](?doc=1271), [OPERS — Agenda — January 21, 2026](?doc=20), [WSIB — Agenda — April 16, 2026](?doc=1084), [DCRB — Minutes — March 19, 2026](?doc=1929), [MainePERS — Agenda — February 17, 2026](?doc=1869), [LACERS — Agenda — February 10, 2026](?doc=927), [DPFP — Agenda — April 02, 2026](?doc=1017)*

**Bottom line:** Private credit is no longer a niche allocation — it is becoming a core portfolio building block. Plans that have not yet formalized a target allocation and dedicated advisory relationship are falling behind the institutional consensus.

## 3. Risk Mitigation and Portfolio Construction Sophistication

Several plans are moving beyond simple asset allocation toward more sophisticated risk management frameworks. **OPERS ($97B)** is increasing its Risk Mitigation Strategies allocation from 3.5% to 10% of the DB Fund, split across Long Treasuries (3.4%), Trend Following (3.3%), and Alternative Risk Premia (3.3%), while reducing Core Fixed Income from 8.6% to 5% ([source](?doc=20)). This required a temporary shift to monthly benchmark resets through June 2026 to facilitate implementation. **SIB-ND ($8B)** outlined a three-phase "Advanced Funds Management" roadmap culminating in alpha-beta separation, portable alpha frameworks, and dynamic beta management, with AI integration as a Phase 3 goal ([source](?doc=1079)). **IPERS ($41B)** hired three absolute return/portable alpha managers — **Florin Court Capital**, **Capital Fund Management SA**, and **AHL Partners LLP** — while its CEO flagged concern that tracking error of 23 basis points was far below the 150 bps target, signaling a desire for more active risk-taking ([source](?doc=82)). **LACERA ($75B)** increased its Risk Reduction and Mitigation target from 19% to 24% of the total fund, with the RRM portfolio delivering a 1-year return of 6.8% versus a 6.0% benchmark ([source](?doc=85)). **ERSRI ($10B)** is exploring a Long Volatility strategy within its Crisis Protection Class as a structural hedge ([source](?doc=1945)).

*Sources: [OPERS — Agenda — January 21, 2026](?doc=20), [SIB-ND — Board Pack — March 13, 2026](?doc=1079), [IPERS — Board Pack — March 27, 2026](?doc=82), [LACERA — Agenda — April 08, 2026](?doc=85), [ERSRI — Board Pack — April 08, 2026](?doc=1945)*

**Practical implication:** The institutional shift toward explicit risk mitigation allocations — trend following, alternative risk premia, long volatility — represents a structural change in how pension CIOs think about portfolio resilience. Plans building these sleeves now are positioning for a regime where equity-bond correlation remains unstable.

## 4. Fee Transparency and Cost Efficiency Gain Institutional Momentum

Fee management is evolving from a negotiation tactic to a formal governance function. **KPPA ($20B)** conducted a dedicated RFP for Investment Cost Efficiency Services, evaluating **Albourne America LLC**, **bFinance**, and **XTP Implementation Services**, ultimately recommending Albourne at $2,000 per fund for since-inception fee validation ([source](?doc=1020)). **CalPERS ($502B)** reported that 41% of PE capital deployed in the first half of FY2026 was through no- or low-fee vehicles including co-investments and Customized Investment Accounts ([source](?doc=1011)). **WSIB ($200B)** highlighted Public Pension Capital's maximum carry of 10% versus the market standard 20%, estimating 2.89% higher annual net returns ([source](?doc=683)). **PERA Colorado ($60B)** noted internal management costs of approximately 6.6 basis points versus an estimated $111 million cost to externally manage the same assets ([source](?doc=407)). **SIB-ND ($8B)** projected management fee savings of over $6 million annually from transitioning its private markets program from fund-of-funds to direct GP relationships ([source](?doc=1080)). **IPERS ($41B)** reported FY2025 investment management expenses of $62.08 million (0.137% of average fund value), a decrease of 33.1% from FY2024 ([source](?doc=82)).

*Sources: [KPPA — Board Pack — March 25, 2026](?doc=1020), [CalPERS — Agenda — March 16, 2026](?doc=1011), [WSIB — Board Pack — February 05, 2026](?doc=683), [PERA Colorado — Board Pack — March 19, 2026](?doc=407), [SIB-ND — Board Pack — February 13, 2026](?doc=1080), [IPERS — Board Pack — March 27, 2026](?doc=82)*

**Bottom line:** Fee validation is becoming a standalone governance function, not an afterthought. The combination of third-party fee audits, co-investment programs, and direct GP relationships is compressing the net fee burden across the institutional landscape.

## 5. Investment Consultant Musical Chairs and the Verus-Cerity Transition

A notable wave of investment consultant transitions is underway. **OPERS-OK ($10B)** is transitioning consulting services from **Verus Advisory** to **Cerity Partners** ([source](?doc=2067)). **SCERS ($12B)** noted that its consultant Verus was acquired by Cerity Partners and is launching an RFP for a General Investment Consultant ([source](?doc=261)). **PSERS ($73B)** discussed the Verus/Cerity merger at its March Investment Committee meeting ([source](?doc=380)). **CTPF ($18B)** replaced its 20-year consultant **Callan LLC** with **Verus** for a five-year contract beginning FY2027, approved 6–4 ([source](?doc=2047)). **TRS Texas ($200B)** formed a special committee to recommend a Board investment consultant at its April 2026 meeting ([source](?doc=1993)). **PERA Colorado ($60B)** hired **Aon** as its Board-appointed investment consultant for the Combined Investment Fund ([source](?doc=409)).

*Sources: [OPERS-OK — Agenda — April 16, 2026](?doc=2067), [SCERS — Minutes — February 18, 2026](?doc=261), [PSERS — Agenda — March 19, 2026](?doc=380), [CTPF — Minutes — March 05, 2026](?doc=2047), [TRS Texas — Board Pack — February 12, 2026](?doc=1993), [PERA Colorado — Board Pack — January 23, 2026](?doc=409)*

**Practical implication:** The Verus-Cerity consolidation is forcing plans to reassess consultant relationships, creating a window for competing firms. Plans that have not reviewed their consulting arrangements in the past three years should treat this market disruption as a catalyst.

## 6. Climate and ESG: Divergent Trajectories

Climate-related investment activity shows a widening gap between leaders and plans retreating from ESG framing. **NYC Retirement ($260B)** reported a 49% reduction in Scope 1 and 2 financed emissions intensity since its 2019 baseline, surpassing its 32% interim target, and holds $7.34 billion in climate solution investments against a $19 billion 2035 goal ([source](?doc=1956)). All 42 TRS public markets managers submitted net zero alignment plans. However, TRS decided not to set Scope 3 portfolio-level reduction targets, citing data quality and double-counting concerns ([source](?doc=1956)).

In contrast, **ATRS ($20B)** terminated its **BlackRock MSCI ACWI IMI Index Fund** and redeployed assets to an Ex-China Ex-Hong Kong strategy, maintaining a standing "Holdings in China and Hong Kong Report" as a board agenda item ([source](?doc=1271)). **PERA Colorado ($60B)** included "Divestment from Companies with Prohibitions Against Israel" as a consent agenda item ([source](?doc=404)). **WSIB ($200B)** held a dedicated discussion on energy transition investing alongside asset stewardship and corporate governance ([source](?doc=1084)).

*Sources: [NYC Retirement — Agenda — April 16, 2026](?doc=1956), [ATRS — Board Pack — February 02, 2026](?doc=1271), [PERA Colorado — Board Pack — March 20, 2026](?doc=404), [WSIB — Agenda — April 16, 2026](?doc=1084)*

**Bottom line:** The institutional ESG landscape is bifurcating. Plans with established climate frameworks are deepening implementation, while geopolitical divestment mandates (China, Israel) are creating a parallel set of portfolio constraints that have nothing to do with climate but everything to do with political risk management.

## 7. Macroeconomic Positioning: Caution Amid Slowing Growth

Board materials across plans paint a consistent macroeconomic picture: slowing U.S. growth, sticky inflation, and elevated uncertainty from tariff policy. **NYC Retirement** noted U.S. GDP slowed sharply to 0.5% annualized in Q4 2025 from 4.4% the prior quarter, with CPI rising to 3.3% in March 2026 ([source](?doc=1956)). **SIB-ND** reported the S&P 500 down -4.3% YTD through March 31, 2026 ([source](?doc=1078)). **DPFP ($3B)** reported a total fund estimated YTD return of -2.41% through March 31, 2026 ([source](?doc=1017)). **MPSERS** sold $2.7 billion in risk assets, primarily domestic equity, and increased Long-Term Fixed Income by $1.2 billion ([source](?doc=1517)). **NHRS ($9B)** reduced global equity exposure to 45% to moderate risk ([source](?doc=1710)).

Gold's surge — $4,731/oz as of April 13, 2026, up 47.3% year-over-year per NYC Retirement data ([source](?doc=1956)) — and strong non-U.S. equity performance (MSCI EAFE +31.22% in 2025 per ERSRI data ([source](?doc=1949))) are reshaping relative value assessments. **KPPA** maintained an overweight to non-U.S. equities based on attractive valuations versus U.S. large caps, noting a negative U.S. equity risk premium of approximately -85 basis points ([source](?doc=1022)).

*Sources: [NYC Retirement — Agenda — April 16, 2026](?doc=1956), [SIB-ND — Board Pack — April 09, 2026](?doc=1078), [DPFP — Agenda — April 02, 2026](?doc=1017), [MPSERS — Board Pack — March 25, 2026](?doc=1517), [NHRS — Minutes — February 10, 2026](?doc=1710), [KPPA — Board Pack — February 25, 2026](?doc=1022), [ERSRI — Board Pack — February 18, 2026](?doc=1949)*

**Practical implication:** The Q1 2026 equity drawdown and persistent inflation are testing the conviction behind recent allocation increases to risk assets. Plans that built risk mitigation sleeves in 2025 are better positioned; those still underweight defensive strategies face a narrowing window to act before the next volatility event.
