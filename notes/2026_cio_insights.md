# CIO Insights: 2026 Institutional Trends
*Synthesized from board and investment committee activity across 73 U.S. public pension plans (~$4.0 trillion AUM)*
*Generated: April 17, 2026*

---

## 1. Private Markets Deployment Accelerates Despite Persistent Underallocation

The dominant capital deployment story across plans in this cycle is the aggressive pacing of private markets commitments — even as most plans remain meaningfully below their private equity and private credit targets. This gap between ambition and execution is structural, not tactical, and it is widening the dispersion between plans that can deploy efficiently and those that cannot.

**IPERS** ($41B) approved a CY2026 private markets commitment plan totaling an estimated maximum of $2.025 billion — $750M to private real assets, $700M to private equity, and $575M to private credit ([source](?doc=82)). **Minnesota SBI** ($161B) authorized eleven private markets commitments totaling up to $1.975 billion in a single October 2025 meeting, spanning buyout, secondaries, and real estate ([source](?doc=684)). **CalPERS** ($502B) deployed $13.8 billion in new PE commitments in the first half of FY2026 alone, with 41% flowing through no- or low-fee vehicles including co-investments and Customized Investment Accounts ([source](?doc=1011)). **LACERA** ($75B) approved $416 million in PE commitments in a single March 2026 meeting — $350M to **AE Industrial Partners IV**, $41M alongside **Kingswood Capital Management**, and $25M alongside **Primary Venture Partners** — and had previously approved over $1.8 billion in combined commitments in January 2026 across real estate, credit, and private equity ([source](?doc=87), [source](?doc=86)).

Yet underallocation persists. **DPFP** ($3B) reported private equity at just 1.8% of assets versus a 6% target, and private credit at 0.1% versus 4% ([source](?doc=1017)). **KPPA/CERS** ($20B) showed PE NAV of ~$1.08B against a target of ~$1.6B, prompting Wilshire to recommend $600M annual commitments in 2025-2026, stepping down to $500M in 2027 ([source](?doc=1022)). The pattern is clear: boards are approving aggressive pacing plans, but the deployment pipeline — constrained by deal flow, legal review timelines, and GP capacity — cannot close the gap quickly.

The fee consciousness embedded in these commitments is notable. **CalPERS** is channeling 41% of capital through no- or low-fee structures ([source](?doc=1011)). **WSIB** ($200B) is evaluating **Public Pension Capital, LLC**, whose maximum carry of 10% (versus the market-standard 20%) is estimated to generate 2.89% higher annual net returns on identical cash flows ([source](?doc=683)). **KPPA** recommended hiring **Albourne America LLC** for fee validation services at $2,000 per fund ([source](?doc=1020)), and **CERS** noted that **Oaktree Capital Management** was hired to replace **Shenkman Capital** at a fee more than 10 basis points lower ([source](?doc=1026)).

*Sources: [CalPERS — Agenda — March 16, 2026](?doc=1011), [IPERS — Board Pack — March 27, 2026](?doc=82), [MN SBI — Board Pack — March 26, 2026](?doc=684), [LACERA — Board Pack — March 11, 2026](?doc=87), [LACERA — Agenda — March 11, 2026](?doc=86), [DPFP — Agenda — April 09, 2026](?doc=1017), [KPPA — Board Pack — February 25, 2026](?doc=1022), [WSIB — Board Pack — February 05, 2026](?doc=683), [KPPA — Board Pack — March 25, 2026](?doc=1020), [KPPA — Board Pack — January 12, 2026](?doc=1026)*

**Practical implication:** Plans with streamlined CIO delegation authority (LACERA, CalPERS) are deploying capital at multiples of the pace available to plans requiring full board approval for each commitment. The governance structure is becoming a competitive advantage in private markets access.

## 2. Risk Mitigation Strategies and Portfolio Architecture Overhauls Signal a Regime Shift

Several of the largest plans are undertaking fundamental portfolio restructuring that goes beyond incremental rebalancing — building new risk mitigation sleeves, separating alpha from beta, and rethinking how fixed income and alternatives interact.

**OPERS** ($97B) is increasing its Risk Mitigation Strategies allocation from 3.5% to 10% of the DB Fund, comprising Long Treasuries (3.4%), Trend Following (3.3%), and Alternative Risk Premia (3.3%), while simultaneously reducing Core Fixed Income from 8.6% to 5% ([source](?doc=20)). The board authorized temporary monthly benchmark resets through June 2026 to facilitate this transition ([source](?doc=21)). **SIB-ND** ($8B) presented a three-phase "Advanced Funds Management" roadmap culminating in alpha-beta separation, portable alpha frameworks, and dynamic beta management, with AI integration planned across the investment process ([source](?doc=1079)). **IPERS** ($41B) hired three absolute return managers — **Florin Court Capital**, **Capital Fund Management SA**, and **AHL Partners LLP** — for portable alpha strategies, while simultaneously eliminating its 5% Global Smart Beta allocation and reallocating to core fixed income and public credit ([source](?doc=82)). **LACERA** ($75B) increased its Risk Reduction and Mitigation target from 19% to 24%, with the $22.26 billion RRM portfolio delivering a 1-year return of 6.8% versus a 6.0% benchmark as of February 2026 ([source](?doc=85)).

This is not defensive positioning. These plans are building structural diversification into portfolio architecture — trend following, alternative risk premia, and portable alpha are being treated as permanent allocations, not tactical overlays.

*Sources: [OPERS — Agenda — January 21, 2026](?doc=20), [OPERS — Minutes — January 21, 2026](?doc=21), [SIB-ND — Board Pack — March 13, 2026](?doc=1079), [IPERS — Board Pack — March 27, 2026](?doc=82), [LACERA — Agenda — April 08, 2026](?doc=85)*

**Bottom line:** The institutional consensus is shifting from "60/40 plus alternatives" toward explicitly engineered risk mitigation sleeves. Plans that delay this architectural work may find themselves structurally exposed in the next drawdown.

## 3. Private Credit Emerges as a Distinct Asset Class — But Deployment Lags

Private credit is being carved out of opportunistic buckets and established as a standalone allocation across multiple plans, but actual deployment remains well below targets.

**PERA Colorado** ($60B) established Private Credit as a formal asset class in January 2025, carved from the Opportunistic bucket, with objectives including excess returns over public fixed income and inflation protection ([source](?doc=407)). **OPERS** noted that its Private Credit sub-asset class is expected to take more than three years to reach its long-term target of 4% for the DB Fund ([source](?doc=20)). **WSIB** ($200B) presented a private credit implementation timeline update and held an education session on private credit benchmarking ([source](?doc=682)). **LACERS** ($25B) is actively building its private credit program, with **Aksia LLC** presenting a 2026 strategic plan and the board approving approximately $190M in private credit commitments including **Sound Point Strategic Capital Fund III** ($70M), **BDT & MSD Real Estate Credit Opportunity Fund III** ($100M), and **Square Nine Specialty Credit Fund I** ($20M) ([source](?doc=925)). **DPFP** expanded **Albourne's** advisory scope to cover Private Credit alongside Private Equity and Real Assets ([source](?doc=1017)).

The SIB-ND board pack noted the private credit market has grown to approximately $2 trillion ([source](?doc=1078)), and PERA Colorado's alternatives team described "attractive double-digit yields from senior direct loans" ([source](?doc=409)).

*Sources: [PERA Colorado — Board Pack — March 19, 2026](?doc=407), [OPERS — Agenda — January 21, 2026](?doc=20), [WSIB — Board Pack — April 02, 2026](?doc=682), [LACERS — Agenda — February 24, 2026](?doc=925), [DPFP — Agenda — April 09, 2026](?doc=1017), [SIB-ND — Board Pack — April 09, 2026](?doc=1078), [PERA Colorado — Board Pack — January 23, 2026](?doc=409)*

**Practical implication:** Private credit is transitioning from an opportunistic allocation to a strategic one, but the three-plus-year deployment timeline acknowledged by OPERS suggests that plans entering now should expect a prolonged ramp. Consultant and advisory infrastructure (Aksia, Albourne) is being built in parallel with the allocation itself.

## 4. Real Assets Pivot: Infrastructure and Natural Resources Gain at the Expense of Traditional Real Estate

Plans are recalibrating real assets allocations toward infrastructure and natural resources, driven by energy transition themes, AI-related demand for data centers, and continued caution on office and traditional retail.

**LACERA** ($75B) reported its Real Assets ex-Real Estate portfolio returned 12.1% for the one-year period versus a 7.6% benchmark, with Natural Resources delivering 17.8% versus 4.1% — driven by record gold demand and metals tied to AI, electrification, and industrial automation ([source](?doc=88)). **LACERS** is transitioning from public to private investments in both Infrastructure and Natural Resources, with public market completion portfolios expected to sunset by 2027 ([source](?doc=88)). **NCRS** ($120B) proposed increasing Real Asset Equity from 12.5% to 18% while decreasing Corporate Equity from 48% to 44% ([source](?doc=742)). **OCERS** ($25B) approved a 2026 real estate investment plan favoring sector specialists in data centers, built-to-rent, manufactured housing, and student housing, with **Townsend** increasing long-term value-add real estate return expectations by 200 bps to 8.5% ([source](?doc=941)). **WSIB** discussed energy transition investing as a dedicated agenda topic ([source](?doc=1084)).

**LACERS** ($25B) issued an RFP for Global Listed Infrastructure with an estimated mandate size of up to approximately $800 million ([source](?doc=923)), signaling meaningful new allocation to the space.

*Sources: [LACERA — Agenda — March 11, 2026](?doc=88), [NCRS — Board Pack — January 29, 2026](?doc=742), [OCERS — Agenda — February 25, 2026](?doc=941), [WSIB — Agenda — April 16, 2026](?doc=1084), [LACERS — Agenda — March 10, 2026](?doc=923)*

**Bottom line:** The real assets allocation is being reshaped by structural demand themes — energy transition, AI infrastructure, and housing scarcity — rather than traditional return-seeking. Plans with legacy office exposure (PERA Colorado noted real estate properties exceeding 50% leverage limits due to adverse valuations ([source](?doc=407))) face a more complex transition.

## 5. Internalization, Fee Compression, and Operational Modernization as Alpha Sources

A growing number of plans are treating operational efficiency and fee reduction as explicit sources of return, with internal management, fee validation, and technology modernization receiving board-level attention.

**PERA Colorado** ($60B) reported that internal management generates approximately $111 million in annual savings, with internal costs of 6.6 basis points versus an estimated 27.3 basis points for external management of the same assets ([source](?doc=415)). **SIB-ND** ($8B) is transitioning its private markets program from fund-of-funds to a direct GP model, projecting savings of over $6 million annually in management fees and over $4.6 million in potential carried interest savings ([source](?doc=1080)). **IPERS** ($41B) reported FY2025 investment management expenses of $62.08 million (0.137% of average fund value), a 33.1% decrease from FY2024 ([source](?doc=82)). **OPERS** ($97B) reported CEM Benchmarking 2024 actual cost of 33.4 bps (excluding performance fees), below the peer median of 37.4 bps ([source](?doc=20)). **NM ERB** ($13B) approved fee reduction initiatives for its Alternative Retirement Plan that will save participants over $1 million annually through recordkeeper renegotiations and conversion to lower-cost Collective Investment Trusts ([source](?doc=467)).

Technology modernization is accelerating across plans. **LACERA** ($75B) is deploying Microsoft Copilot across the organization, with AI projects including a chatbot, AI-enabled disability retirement medical report organization, and AI-enhanced Excel productivity for investment analysis ([source](?doc=5)). **SIB-ND** presented an AI integration roadmap evolving from productivity tools toward portfolio monitoring and AI-driven investment recommendations ([source](?doc=1079)). **PERA Colorado** received an educational presentation on AI in investment management from Schroders ([source](?doc=411)).

*Sources: [PERA Colorado — Board Pack — November 20, 2025](?doc=415), [SIB-ND — Board Pack — February 13, 2026](?doc=1080), [IPERS — Board Pack — March 27, 2026](?doc=82), [OPERS — Agenda — January 21, 2026](?doc=20), [NM ERB — Minutes — December 05, 2025](?doc=467), [LACERA — Agenda — April 01, 2026](?doc=5), [SIB-ND — Board Pack — March 13, 2026](?doc=1079), [PERA Colorado — Board Pack — November 21, 2025](?doc=411)*

**Practical implication:** The gap between high-cost and low-cost plans is widening. Plans that combine internal management, direct GP relationships, and AI-enabled operations are compounding a structural advantage that will be difficult for peers to replicate.

## 6. Benchmark Methodology Under Review as Private Markets Distort Total Fund Attribution

Multiple plans are grappling with the fundamental challenge of benchmarking illiquid assets within a total fund framework, and several are making consequential changes.

**KPPA/CERS** and **KRS** both adopted a "neutralized" approach for private equity benchmarking — using actual time-weighted PE performance as the benchmark contribution to total fund roll-up, while evaluating PE success separately via IRR and Public Market Equivalent analysis ([source](?doc=1022), [source](?doc=1026)). This replaced a Russell 3000 + 3% benchmark that was distorting total fund results during periods of public market volatility. **VRS** ($110B) hired **Mercer** to conduct an independent review of benchmarks and hurdle rates for Private Equity, Credit Strategies, and Diversifying Strategies, with results due April 2026 ([source](?doc=633)). **OCERS** ($25B) changed its private markets benchmarks from the Cambridge suite to MSCI indices for Private Equity, Infrastructure, and Natural Resources ([source](?doc=960)). **PERA Colorado** proposed transitioning its private equity incentive fee structure to higher excess return targets — from 50bps to 70bps (1-year), 30bps to 40bps (3-year), and 25bps to 30bps (5-year) — phased in over five years ([source](?doc=407)).

The significance is that benchmark choices directly affect CIO compensation, manager evaluation, and board perception of value-added. **CalPERS** PE returned 17.8% for the one-year period but trailed its Policy Benchmark of 18.8% ([source](?doc=1011)), while **VRS** PE returned just 7.9% against a 16.1% benchmark over one year ([source](?doc=633)) — a gap that may reflect benchmark construction as much as manager performance.

*Sources: [KPPA — Board Pack — February 25, 2026](?doc=1022), [KPPA — Board Pack — January 12, 2026](?doc=1026), [VRS — Board Pack — January 30, 2026](?doc=633), [OCERS — Agenda — November 19, 2025](?doc=960), [PERA Colorado — Board Pack — March 19, 2026](?doc=407), [CalPERS — Agenda — March 16, 2026](?doc=1011)*

**Bottom line:** The industry is moving away from public-equity-plus-premium benchmarks for private markets. Plans that do not revisit their PE benchmarking methodology risk misallocating capital based on distorted attribution signals.

## 7. Q1 2026 Market Stress Tests Governance Resilience

The Q1 2026 equity drawdown — with the Russell 3000 down approximately 4.0% and the S&P 500 down approximately 4.3% year-to-date through March 31, 2026 ([source](?doc=1078)) — arrived against a backdrop of tariff uncertainty, sticky inflation (core PCE at ~3.1%), and a Fed on hold at 3.50%-3.75% ([source](?doc=1078)). **DPFP** reported an estimated total fund YTD return of -2.41% through March 31, 2026 ([source](?doc=1017)).

This stress, while modest by historical standards, is testing the governance frameworks that plans built during the 2023-2025 bull market. **IPERS** CEO flagged that the plan's active risk tracking error of 23 basis points is far below the Board-established target of 150 bps and upper limit of 300 bps, proposing a multi-meeting review to evaluate whether the plan is taking sufficient active risk ([source](?doc=82)). **PERA Colorado** debated whether CEO evaluation criteria should measure investment performance "relative to liabilities" — an amendment that was defeated 1-13 ([source](?doc=404)). **DPFP** saw a contentious 6-5 vote on its City of Dallas settlement and a failed motion to pause COLA payments ([source](?doc=578), [source](?doc=580)).

Plans that invested in governance infrastructure during calmer markets — **PERA Colorado** retained **Mosaic Governance Advisors** for board performance evaluation ([source](?doc=406)), **SCERS** engaged Mosaic for a Strategic Vision Plan ([source](?doc=271)), and **LACERA** adopted a formal Succession Policy ([source](?doc=921)) — appear better positioned to navigate the policy debates that market stress inevitably triggers.

*Sources: [SIB-ND — Board Pack — April 09, 2026](?doc=1078), [DPFP — Agenda — April 09, 2026](?doc=1017), [IPERS — Board Pack — March 27, 2026](?doc=82), [PERA Colorado — Board Pack — March 20, 2026](?doc=404), [PERA Colorado — Board Pack — March 19, 2026](?doc=406), [DPFP — Agenda — March 06, 2026](?doc=572), [DPFP — Agenda — November 07, 2025](?doc=580), [SCERS — Minutes — October 15, 2025](?doc=271), [LACERS — Agenda — March 24, 2026](?doc=921)*

**Practical implication:** The Q1 drawdown is a governance stress test more than a portfolio stress test. Plans with clear delegation frameworks, pre-established rebalancing protocols, and strong board-staff role clarity will navigate the next phase of volatility with fewer forced errors.
