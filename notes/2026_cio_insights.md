# CIO Insights: 2026 Institutional Trends
*Synthesized from board and investment committee activity across 108 U.S. public pension plans (~$4.4 trillion AUM)*
*Generated: April 24, 2026*

---

## 1. Private Markets Deployment Accelerates Despite Elevated Valuations and Liquidity Headwinds

The dominant capital allocation story across the dataset is the sustained — and in many cases intensifying — pace of private markets commitments, even as plans acknowledge stretched valuations and slower distributions. **CalPERS ($502B)** deployed $13.8 billion in new PE commitments in the first half of FY2026 alone, with 41% flowing through no- or low-fee vehicles (co-investments and Customized Investment Accounts) ([source](?doc=1011)). **MN SBI ($161B)** approved eleven private markets commitments totaling up to $1.975 billion in a single meeting, spanning buyout, secondaries, and real estate value-add ([source](?doc=684)). **MPSERS ($80B)** made $1.56 billion in new commitments during Q4 2025 across absolute return, international equity, private equity, real estate, and infrastructure ([source](?doc=1517)).

Smaller plans are also leaning in. **KPPA ($20B)** adopted a PE pacing strategy calling for $600 million in annual commitments in 2025–2026, stepping down to $500 million in 2027 and $400 million thereafter, to close a gap between its current ~$1.08 billion PE NAV and an 8% allocation target on a $20 billion plan ([source](?doc=1022)). **DPFP ($3B)** committed $35 million to **Blackstone Strategic Partners X** and up to $15 million to **Lexington Continuation Vehicle Investors**, both PE secondaries, while noting private market assets at 11.2% versus an 18% target — a -$148 million variance ([source](?doc=1017)). **MainePERS ($18B)** disclosed rolling 12-month private market commitments of $1.57 billion approved and $1.37 billion closed, spanning direct lending, infrastructure, and private equity ([source](?doc=1869)).

The valuation backdrop is not being ignored. CalPERS noted global median PE buyout purchase price multiples rose to 14.5x EBITDA in 2025 from 13.3x in 2024, with equity contributions now representing 66–69% of deal financing ([source](?doc=1011)). **WSIB ($200B)** highlighted **Charterhouse Capital Partners XII** predecessor fund performance — CCP X at 20% net IRR and CCP XI at 15% net IRR — while noting CCP XII's first investment entered at 13.3x EV/EBITDA ([source](?doc=682)). Plans are managing the tension between deployment urgency and valuation discipline by favoring secondaries, co-investments, and lower-fee structures.

*Sources: [CalPERS — Agenda — March 16, 2026](?doc=1011), [MN SBI — Board Pack — March 26, 2026](?doc=684), [MPSERS — Board Pack — March 25, 2026](?doc=1517), [KPPA — Board Pack — February 25, 2026](?doc=1022), [DPFP — Agenda — April 09, 2026](?doc=1017), [MainePERS — Agenda — March 12, 2026](?doc=1869), [WSIB — Board Pack — April 02, 2026](?doc=682)*

**Practical implication:** The industry-wide push into private markets is creating a crowded deployment environment. Plans that can access lower-fee structures (co-investments, CIAs, direct GP relationships) and exercise vintage-year discipline will differentiate themselves; those simply filling allocation gaps at peak multiples face meaningful J-curve and return compression risk.

## 2. Private Credit Emerges as a Distinct Strategic Allocation

Private credit is transitioning from an opportunistic sleeve to a standalone strategic asset class across multiple plans. **PERA Colorado ($60B)** established a Private Credit asset class in January 2025 as a carve-out from its Opportunistic bucket ([source](?doc=407)). **CRS Cincinnati ($2B)** approved an increase of its private credit allocation to a newly approved target per December 2025 asset allocation changes ([source](?doc=2718)). **DPFP ($3B)** engaged **Albourne** for Private Markets Advisory covering Private Equity, Private Credit, and Real Assets since July 2024, and expanded that scope in 2026 ([source](?doc=570)). **LACERS ($25B)** continued discussions on a private credit consulting contract with **Aksia LLC** and notified the board of approximately $430 million in new alternative commitments including private credit and real estate credit strategies ([source](?doc=925)). **DCRB ($13B)** approved a $100 million commitment to an unnamed private credit fund ("Fund AP") ([source](?doc=1929)).

The commitment pipeline is substantial. **MainePERS** committed up to $250 million to **Blackstone Senior Direct Lending Fund Series II** plus $125 million to its co-investment fund, and $50 million to **Redwood Drawdown Fund IV** ([source](?doc=1869)). **SJCERA ($2B)** committed $50 million to **Pemberton Strategic Credit Fund IV** ([source](?doc=2547)). **LACERA ($75B)** invested $750 million with **Neuberger Berman** via a multi-asset credit strategy through a dedicated managed account ([source](?doc=86)). **WSIB** held an education session on private credit benchmarking and strategy, signaling ongoing buildout of its private credit allocation ([source](?doc=681)).

*Sources: [PERA Colorado — Board Pack — March 19, 2026](?doc=407), [CRS Cincinnati — Board Pack — March 05, 2026](?doc=2718), [DPFP — Agenda — April 09, 2026](?doc=570), [LACERS — Agenda — February 24, 2026](?doc=925), [DCRB — Minutes — March 19, 2026](?doc=1929), [MainePERS — Agenda — March 12, 2026](?doc=1869), [SJCERA — Agenda — April 10, 2026](?doc=2547), [LACERA — Agenda — March 11, 2026](?doc=86), [WSIB — Board Pack — February 19, 2026](?doc=681)*

**Bottom line:** Private credit has crossed the threshold from tactical to strategic for a critical mass of plans. The speed of institutionalization — dedicated consultants, standalone IPS sections, pacing plans — suggests this asset class will absorb significant new capital over the next 2–3 years, potentially compressing the very illiquidity premium that justified the allocation.

## 3. Fee Consciousness and Cost Structure Innovation Intensify

Plans are pursuing fee reduction through multiple channels: renegotiation, structural innovation, consultant-led fee audits, and internalization. **CalPERS** directed 41% of its PE capital through no- or low-fee vehicles ([source](?doc=1011)). **WSIB** evaluated **Public Pension Capital, LLC**, which charges a maximum carry of 10% versus the market standard 20%, translating to approximately 2.89% higher net returns per year ([source](?doc=681)). **SIB-ND ($8B)** is transitioning from fund-of-funds to a direct GP program, projecting savings of over $6 million annually in management fees and over $4.6 million in potential carried interest savings ([source](?doc=1080)).

On the audit side, **KPPA** conducted an RFP for Investment Cost Efficiency Services, ultimately recommending **Albourne America LLC** at $2,000 per fund for since-inception fee validation ([source](?doc=1020)). **CCPF ($12B)** negotiated fee reductions on a $350 million Manager of Managers mandate with **Bivium Capital** ([source](?doc=1106)). **IPERS ($41B)** reported FY2025 preliminary investment management expenses of $62.08 million (0.137% of average fund value), a decrease of 33.1% from FY2024 ([source](?doc=82)). **CRS Cincinnati** reported an administrative expense ratio of approximately 15 basis points, below budget and in line with peers ([source](?doc=2716)).

*Sources: [CalPERS — Agenda — March 16, 2026](?doc=1011), [WSIB — Board Pack — February 19, 2026](?doc=681), [SIB-ND — Board Pack — February 13, 2026](?doc=1080), [KPPA — Board Pack — March 25, 2026](?doc=1020), [CCPF — Minutes — March 05, 2026](?doc=1106), [IPERS — Board Pack — March 27, 2026](?doc=82), [CRS Cincinnati — Board Pack — April 02, 2026](?doc=2716)*

**Practical implication:** The fee conversation has evolved from headline management fees to total cost of ownership — including carried interest, fund-of-funds layering, and operational costs. Plans that can internalize, co-invest, or negotiate structural fee innovations (capped carry, budgeted management fees) are creating durable cost advantages that compound meaningfully over time.

## 4. Climate and ESG: Execution Deepens While Political Headwinds Mount

**NYC Retirement ($260B)** reported a 49% reduction in Scope 1 and 2 financed emissions intensity since its 2019 baseline, surpassing its 32% interim target ([source](?doc=1956)). Climate solution investments reached $7.34 billion against a 2035 goal of $19 billion ([source](?doc=1956)). All 42 TRS public markets managers submitted net zero alignment plans; 64% adopted net zero goals with quantified targets ([source](?doc=1956)). However, the science-based target coverage goal of 70% of financed emissions by FY2025 was not met — only 27% was covered by validated targets ([source](?doc=1956)). TRS also decided not to set Scope 3 portfolio-level reduction targets, citing data quality and double-counting concerns ([source](?doc=1956)).

**WSIB** held a dedicated discussion on energy transition investing and climate change considerations alongside its asset stewardship and corporate governance review ([source](?doc=1084)). **PERA Colorado** included divestment from companies with prohibitions against Israel on its consent agenda ([source](?doc=404)), illustrating how politically-driven mandates are layering onto ESG frameworks. NYC TRS explicitly noted that "federal policy and rhetoric" was identified as an ongoing challenge to pension fund climate action efforts ([source](?doc=1956)).

The tension is real: plans are deepening climate execution operationally while navigating an increasingly hostile political environment. **LAFPP ($29B)** reported that **Glass Lewis** voted 2,829 ballots covering 15,066 proposals in 2025, with 22.50% of votes cast against management proposals ([source](?doc=2069)), suggesting active stewardship continues even as headline ESG rhetoric shifts.

*Sources: [NYC Retirement — Agenda — April 16, 2026](?doc=1956), [WSIB — Agenda — April 16, 2026](?doc=1084), [PERA Colorado — Board Pack — March 20, 2026](?doc=404), [LAFPP — Agenda — April 02, 2026](?doc=2069)*

**Bottom line:** The largest plans are executing on climate commitments with increasing sophistication, but the gap between ambition and measurable progress (particularly on Scope 3 and science-based targets) is widening. Plans should expect continued political pressure from both directions and should ground their climate strategies in fiduciary language and measurable financial risk metrics.

## 5. Investment Consultant Transitions Signal Strategic Reassessment

A notable cluster of consultant transitions and RFPs suggests plans are reassessing their advisory relationships. **OPERS-OK ($10B)** transitioned investment consulting services from **Verus Advisory** to **Cerity Partners** ([source](?doc=2067)). **CTPF ($18B)** replaced its 20-year consultant **Callan LLC** with **Verus** for a five-year contract beginning FY2027, approved 6-4 — a contested vote reflecting board-level disagreement ([source](?doc=2047)). **SJCERA ($2B)** announced an Investment Consulting RFP to be released during summer 2026 ([source](?doc=2547)). **SCERS ($12B)** is targeting late March for release of a General Investment Consultant RFP, noting that its current consultant **Verus** was acquired by **Cerity Partners** ([source](?doc=261)). **TRS-OK ($19B)** had Investment Consultant RFP finalists under consideration ([source](?doc=1272)). **SIB-ND** issued an RFP for combined Performance and Benchmark Consulting services ([source](?doc=1079)).

The Verus/Cerity Partners merger is a catalyst: multiple plans referenced the transaction as prompting review. **PSERS ($73B)** discussed the Verus/Cerity merger update at its March Investment Committee meeting ([source](?doc=380)).

*Sources: [OPERS-OK — Agenda — April 16, 2026](?doc=2067), [CTPF — Minutes — March 05, 2026](?doc=2047), [SJCERA — Agenda — April 10, 2026](?doc=2547), [SCERS — Minutes — February 18, 2026](?doc=261), [TRS-OK — Agenda — February 25, 2026](?doc=1272), [SIB-ND — Board Pack — March 13, 2026](?doc=1079), [PSERS — Agenda — March 19, 2026](?doc=380)*

**Practical implication:** Consultant consolidation (Verus/Cerity) is creating a wave of mandatory reassessment. Plans should use this moment to evaluate not just the surviving entity but whether their advisory model — generalist consultant, specialist overlays, or hybrid — still fits their evolving portfolio complexity, particularly as private markets and private credit allocations grow.

## 6. Macroeconomic Caution Amid Strong 2025 Returns

Plans universally reported strong 2025 calendar-year returns — **IPERS** at 13.75% ([source](?doc=82)), **TMRS ($40B)** at 13.29% ([source](?doc=504)), **DCRB** at 14.1% ([source](?doc=1929)), **CRS Cincinnati** at 13.7% ([source](?doc=2718)), **ERSRI** at 12.49% ([source](?doc=1947)), **MPSERS** at 13.7% ([source](?doc=1517)) — but the forward-looking tone is markedly cautious.

**NYC TRS** noted U.S. GDP slowed sharply to 0.5% annualized in Q4 2025 from 4.4% the prior quarter, with CPI rising to 3.3% in March 2026 ([source](?doc=1956)). **SIB-ND** reported the Russell 3000 down approximately -4.0% YTD through March 31, 2026, and the S&P 500 down -4.3% ([source](?doc=1078)). **DPFP** reported an estimated YTD total fund return of -2.41% through March 31, 2026 ([source](?doc=1017)). **KPPA** flagged a negative U.S. equity risk premium of approximately -85 basis points versus a typical 2–3%, concluding that public equities appear overextended with muted forward returns ([source](?doc=1022)). Gold reached $4,731/oz as of April 13, 2026, up 47.3% year-over-year ([source](?doc=1956)).

Several plans are responding tactically. **MPSERS** sold $2.7 billion in risk assets, primarily domestic equity, and increased long-term fixed income by $1.2 billion ([source](?doc=1517)). **EPCP ($2B)** approved a rebalancing plan liquidating $17 million from overweight international equity into underweight fixed income ([source](?doc=2764)). **KPPA** extended fixed income duration from short to neutral and maintained an overweight to non-U.S. equities based on attractive relative valuations ([source](?doc=1022)).

*Sources: [IPERS — Board Pack — March 27, 2026](?doc=82), [TMRS — Agenda — March 26, 2026](?doc=504), [DCRB — Minutes — March 19, 2026](?doc=1929), [CRS Cincinnati — Board Pack — March 05, 2026](?doc=2718), [ERSRI — Board Pack — March 11, 2026](?doc=1947), [MPSERS — Board Pack — March 25, 2026](?doc=1517), [NYC Retirement — Agenda — April 16, 2026](?doc=1956), [SIB-ND — Board Pack — April 09, 2026](?doc=1078), [DPFP — Agenda — April 09, 2026](?doc=1017), [KPPA — Board Pack — February 25, 2026](?doc=1022), [EPCP — Minutes — March 18, 2026](?doc=2764)*

**Bottom line:** The 2025 return tailwind masked a deteriorating macro picture. Plans entering 2026 with overweight equity positions and underweight fixed income face a challenging rebalancing environment. The divergence between strong trailing returns and weak forward indicators demands that boards resist anchoring to recent performance when setting contribution and allocation policy.

## 7. Portfolio Architecture Evolution: Alpha-Beta Separation and Advanced Frameworks

Several plans are moving beyond traditional asset-class silos toward more sophisticated portfolio construction. **SIB-ND** presented a three-phase "Advanced Funds Management" roadmap including alpha-beta separation, new uncorrelated alpha sources (alternative strategies, active extension, sector specialists), dynamic beta management, and an eventual overlay and portable alpha framework ([source](?doc=1079)). The plan's Equity 2.0 initiative delivered 18.0% in 2025 versus a 17.1% benchmark, and a Fixed Income 2.0 review is underway ([source](?doc=1078)).

**IPERS** flagged concern over very low active risk — tracking error of 23 basis points against a target of 150 bps and upper limit of 300 bps — and initiated a multi-meeting review of active risk policy ([source](?doc=82)). The plan also adopted Portfolio C, removing its 5% Global Smart Beta allocation and reallocating 2% to core fixed income and 3% to public credit ([source](?doc=82)). **LAFPP** eliminated Smart Beta strategies entirely as part of its U.S. equity restructuring, terminating contracts with **Northern Trust**, **AllianceBernstein**, and others ([source](?doc=2077)). **CalPERS** is transitioning to a Total Portfolio Approach (TPA), which will fundamentally shift its capital allocation framework across asset classes ([source](?doc=1011)).

*Sources: [SIB-ND — Board Pack — March 13, 2026](?doc=1079), [SIB-ND — Board Pack — April 09, 2026](?doc=1078), [IPERS — Board Pack — March 27, 2026](?doc=82), [LAFPP — Minutes — February 19, 2026](?doc=2077), [CalPERS — Agenda — March 16, 2026](?doc=1011)*

**Practical implication:** The retreat from Smart Beta and the push toward alpha-beta separation represent a meaningful philosophical shift. Plans that can implement portable alpha, dynamic beta overlays, and risk-budgeted frameworks will have structural advantages in a lower-return environment — but the governance and operational complexity is substantial, favoring larger, better-resourced investment offices.

## 8. Cybersecurity and Operational Risk Elevated to Board-Level Priority

Cybersecurity has moved from an IT concern to a standing board-level agenda item. **ERSRI** conducted a cybersecurity program review and considered a cyber-security insurance policy renewal, with the Director of Cybersecurity presenting on completed technology inventory, data mapping, password manager implementation, and penetration testing ([source](?doc=1945)). Post-quantum cryptography requirements are planned for vendors within 2–3 years ([source](?doc=1945)). **CRS Cincinnati** rated cybersecurity risk as "high" on its risk dashboard ([source](?doc=2716)). **PSERS** discussed a Cybersecurity Insurance Policy in executive session ([source](?doc=376)). **PERA Colorado** conducted a cybersecurity briefing in closed executive session ([source](?doc=404)). **SERS PA** received a cybersecurity update in its Audit, Risk and Compliance Committee ([source](?doc=383)).

Multiple plans are simultaneously modernizing pension administration systems — **SJCERA** signed with **Heywood** ([source](?doc=2548)), **DPFP** is evaluating a **PensionGold** upgrade with **LRS** at approximately $425,000 ([source](?doc=1017)), **CCPF** approved a 3.5-year implementation agreement with **Procentia** ([source](?doc=1106)) — creating additional attack surface during transition periods.

*Sources: [ERSRI — Board Pack — April 08, 2026](?doc=1945), [CRS Cincinnati — Board Pack — April 02, 2026](?doc=2716), [PSERS — Agenda — March 19, 2026](?doc=376), [PERA Colorado — Board Pack — March 20, 2026](?doc=404), [SERS PA — Agenda — February 24, 2026](?doc=383), [SJCERA — Agenda — March 13, 2026](?doc=2548), [DPFP — Agenda — April 09, 2026](?doc=1017), [CCPF — Minutes — March 05, 2026](?doc=1106)*

**Practical implication:** The convergence of aging pension administration systems, cloud migration, and escalating cyber threats creates a risk profile that boards must actively govern — not delegate. Plans undertaking simultaneous system modernization and cybersecurity upgrades should ensure dedicated board oversight and adequate insurance coverage during transition windows.
