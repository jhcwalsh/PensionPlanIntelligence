# CIO Insights: 2026 Institutional Trends
*Synthesized from board and investment committee activity across 46 U.S. public pension plans (~$3.4 trillion AUM)*
*Generated: April 15, 2026*

---

## 1. Private Equity Deployment Accelerates — But Fee Discipline Is Reshaping How Capital Flows

The most consistent signal across the dataset is sustained, large-scale private equity commitment activity, with a simultaneous and deliberate shift toward lower-cost structures. **CalPERS ($502B)** committed **$13.8 billion** in new PE investments in the first half of fiscal year 2026, with **41% deployed through no- or low-fee vehicles** including co-investments and Customized Investment Accounts (doc_id=1011). The performance data validates the strategy: CIAs returned **25.1%** over one year versus **-0.5%** for Fund of Funds structures, and the Fund of Funds underperformance is explicitly attributed in part to higher fee loads (doc_id=1011).

**Minnesota SBI ($161B)** approved eleven private markets commitments totaling up to **$1.975 billion** at its October 2025 meeting, spanning managers including **Advent International GPE XI**, **Blackstone Supplemental Account – M**, **Strategic Partners X**, **Permira IX**, and **TPG Partners X** (doc_id=684). **LACERA ($75B)** approved a **$350 million** commitment to **AE Industrial Partners IV**, plus co-investments of **$41 million** alongside **Kingswood Capital Management** and **$25 million** alongside **Primary Venture Partners** (doc_id=84). **WSIB ($200B)** moved on **Charterhouse Capital Partners XII** (pan-European mid-market buyout, targeting €1.5–2.0bn), **Spark Capital IX** and **Spark Capital Growth VI**, and **Monarch Capital Partners VII** (doc_id=682, 1084). **IPERS ($41B)** set a CY2026 private markets commitment plan with estimated maximum commitments of **$750 million** to private real assets, **$700 million** to private equity, and **$575 million** to private credit (doc_id=82). **HPOPS ($8B)** considered commitments to **Ares Pathfinder III**, **Kingswood Capital Opportunities Fund IV**, and **LS Power Equity Partners VI** (doc_id=568), while **PSERS ($73B)** approved commitments to **DRA Growth and Income Fund XII**, **LS Power Equity Partners VI**, and **Warwick Partners V** (doc_id=380).

The global PE market context is relevant: CalPERS data shows the number of funds closed fell **38% year-over-year** in Q3 2025 even as aggregate fundraising held steady, and the global median PE buyout purchase price multiple rose to **14.5x EBITDA** in 2025 from **13.3x** in 2024 (doc_id=1011). Equity contributions now represent **66–69%** of deal financing versus mid-50s% in 2017–2019, compressing return potential from leverage. This context makes the fee-efficiency pivot at CalPERS strategically coherent, not merely administrative.

*Sources: [CalPERS — Agenda — March 16, 2026](?doc=1011), [MN SBI — Board Pack — March 26, 2026](?doc=684), [LACERA — Agenda — April 08, 2026](?doc=84), [WSIB — Board Pack — April 02, 2026](?doc=682), [WSIB — Agenda — April 16, 2026](?doc=1084), [IPERS — Board Pack — March 27, 2026](?doc=82), [HPOPS — Agenda — April 07, 2026](?doc=568), [PSERS — Agenda — March 19, 2026](?doc=380)*

**Bottom line:** The PE commitment wave is real and broad-based, but the structural shift toward co-investments and direct vehicles is equally significant — plans that remain overweight fund-of-funds structures face a compounding performance and cost disadvantage that is now visible in audited return data.

## 2. Portfolio Architecture Is Being Rebuilt: Alpha-Beta Separation and Total Portfolio Approaches Gain Traction

Several plans are undertaking fundamental restructuring of how they think about portfolio construction — moving beyond static asset class buckets toward integrated risk frameworks. **SIB-ND ($8B)** is executing a multi-phase evolution toward "Advanced Funds Management," explicitly separating alpha from market exposures, building overlay and portable alpha capabilities, and integrating AI tools into investment monitoring (doc_id=1078, 1079). The Equity 2.0 initiative delivered **18.0%** in 2025 versus a **17.1%** benchmark, and a Fixed Income 2.0 re-underwriting is now underway with new managers expected by year-end 2026 (doc_id=1078, 1079). The board also funded **Arrowstreet US All Cap** with approximately **$790 million** across three pools as part of this evolution (doc_id=1079).

**CalPERS** is transitioning to a Total Portfolio Approach (TPA), which the board pack describes as a framework that will "fundamentally shift capital allocation across asset classes" (doc_id=1011). **IPERS** flagged a structural concern that cuts against the active management narrative: its tracking error stood at just **23 basis points** against a target of **150 bps** and an upper limit of **300 bps**, prompting CEO Greg Samorajski to propose a multi-meeting review of active risk policy (doc_id=82). IPERS simultaneously hired three absolute return/portable alpha managers — **Florin Court Capital**, **Capital Fund Management SA**, and **AHL Partners LLP** — and eliminated its 5% Global Smart Beta allocation, reallocating **2%** to core fixed income and **3%** to public credit under the newly adopted Portfolio C (doc_id=82).

The common thread is dissatisfaction with traditional asset class silos as an organizing principle. Plans are seeking more precise control over risk exposures and return sources, with AI and data infrastructure cited as enabling tools at SIB-ND.

*Sources: [SIB-ND — Board Pack — April 09, 2026](?doc=1078), [SIB-ND — Board Pack — March 13, 2026](?doc=1079), [CalPERS — Agenda — March 16, 2026](?doc=1011), [IPERS — Board Pack — March 27, 2026](?doc=82)*

**Bottom line:** The shift from asset class allocation to risk factor and alpha-beta frameworks is no longer a theoretical aspiration — it is being operationalized at plans ranging from $8B to $502B, with concrete manager hires, benchmark changes, and governance restructuring to match.

## 3. Private Equity Secondaries as a Tactical Tool for Underfunded Programs

A distinct sub-theme within private markets is the use of secondaries to rapidly build private equity exposure for plans that are materially underweight their targets. **Dallas Police & Fire Pension System ($3B)** approved a **$35 million** commitment to **Blackstone Strategic Partners X** and up to **$15 million** to **Lexington Continuation Vehicle Investors** — both private equity secondaries — at its March 2026 meeting, approved **9-1** (doc_id=1017, 570). The context is stark: DPFP's private equity allocation stands at **1.8% actual versus a 6% target**, and total private markets are **$148 million** underweight at **11.2% actual versus an 18% target** (doc_id=1017). The plan has **4–6 additional private markets commitments** anticipated throughout 2026 per its pacing plan (doc_id=1017).

**MN SBI** committed up to **$250 million** to **Strategic Partners X** as part of its eleven-commitment October 2025 package (doc_id=684), and **PSERS** approved a commitment to **Warwick Partners V** alongside other private market funds (doc_id=380). The secondaries market is being used not merely for portfolio management but as an accelerant for plans with structural allocation gaps.

*Emerging signal — limited data*: The concentration of secondaries activity at underfunded programs suggests secondaries are functioning as a catch-up mechanism, but the dataset does not provide enough detail on pricing or discount levels to assess whether plans are achieving favorable entry points.

*Sources: [DPFP — Agenda — April 02, 2026](?doc=1017), [DPFP — Agenda — April 02, 2026](?doc=570), [MN SBI — Board Pack — March 26, 2026](?doc=684), [PSERS — Agenda — March 19, 2026](?doc=380)*

**Bottom line:** Secondaries are serving a dual purpose — portfolio construction efficiency and allocation gap closure — and plans with large private markets shortfalls are using them as a primary deployment vehicle rather than a niche strategy.

## 4. Fee Validation and Cost Transparency Are Becoming Institutional Infrastructure

The data reveals a meaningful push to institutionalize fee oversight as a standalone function, not merely a line item in investment consulting agreements. **KPPA ($20B)** ran a formal RFP process — issued July 9, 2025, closed August 11, 2025 — for Investment Cost Efficiency Services, with three finalists presenting: **Albourne America LLC**, **bFinance**, and **XTP Implementation Services** (doc_id=1020). Staff unanimously recommended **Albourne**, citing its dedicated Fee and Liquidity team, analysis of over **7,000 investments**, and validation of over **$200 billion** in client assets since 2014, at a fixed fee of **$2,000 per fund** for since-inception validation (doc_id=1020). A potential conflict of interest was flagged: XTP and Wilshire are sister companies both owned by Monica Holdings LP, and Wilshire currently provides general consulting to CERS (doc_id=1020).

**Colorado PERA ($60B)** is pursuing a Private Equity Incentive Phase-in that would increase excess return targets for internal managers — from **50 bps to 70 bps** (1-year), **30 bps to 40 bps** (3-year), and **25 bps to 30 bps** (5-year) — phased in over five years (doc_id=407). Aon analysis cited in the PERA data shows internal management costs approximately **6.6 basis points** versus an estimated **$111 million** cost to externally manage the same assets, currently managed internally for approximately **$27 million** (doc_id=407). **IPERS** reported FY2025 preliminary investment management expenses of **$62.08 million** (0.137% of average fund value), a decrease of **33.1%** from FY2024, and was ranked a low-cost fund by CEM Benchmarking (doc_id=82).

*Sources: [KPPA — Board Pack — March 25, 2026](?doc=1020), [PERA Colorado — Board Pack — March 19, 2026](?doc=407), [IPERS — Board Pack — March 27, 2026](?doc=82)*

**Bottom line:** Fee validation is transitioning from a periodic audit function to a continuous monitoring capability — plans that have not yet formalized this infrastructure are likely leaving recoverable value on the table, particularly in private markets where fee complexity is highest.

## 5. Risk Mitigation Allocations Are Holding — and Outperforming

Contrary to the assumption that strong equity markets would prompt plans to reduce defensive allocations, the data shows meaningful commitment to risk mitigation structures. **LACERA ($75B)** increased its Risk Reduction and Mitigation (RRM) target allocation from **19% to 24%** in its 2024 Strategic Asset Allocation, with a total RRM market value of **$22.26 billion** as of February 28, 2026 (doc_id=85). The RRM portfolio returned **6.8%** over one year versus a **6.0%** benchmark, **5.1%** over three years versus **5.0%**, and **1.5%** over five years versus **1.1%** (doc_id=85). The portfolio provides **37 months** of benefit payments in liquidity, excluding hedge funds (doc_id=85). Components include Investment Grade Bonds (**$11.723B**, 70% passive/30% active, effective duration **5.8 years**, YTW **4.2%**), Long-Term Government Bonds (**$1.716B**, effective duration **13.9 years**), Hedge Funds (**$7.544B**), and Cash (**$1.276B**) (doc_id=85).

**TMRS ($40B)** reported 2025 total fund performance of **13.29%**, exceeding its benchmark by **1.07%** and adding **$5.6 billion** to the Trust, with a current asset allocation that includes **21%** to Credit and **18%** to Real Assets alongside **35%** Public Equity and **20%** Private Equity (doc_id=504). The diversified structure — including a **6%** Tactical Opportunities sleeve — contributed to benchmark-beating results. **IPERS** hired three absolute return managers as a portfolio diversifier, explicitly targeting uncorrelated return streams (doc_id=82).

*Sources: [LACERA — Agenda — April 08, 2026](?doc=85), [TMRS — Agenda — March 26, 2026](?doc=504), [IPERS — Board Pack — March 27, 2026](?doc=82)*

**Bottom line:** The data contradicts the assumption that strong equity performance reduces demand for risk mitigation — plans are expanding defensive and diversifying allocations precisely because they have the funded status headroom to do so, and the performance record of these structures is validating the decision.

## 6. Governance Modernization: Succession, AI Policy, and Operational Infrastructure

Boards are investing materially in operational and governance infrastructure, with AI policy formalization and succession planning emerging as recurring agenda items. **LACERA** approved an AI Policy dated January 22, 2026 at its March 4 board meeting (**7-0**), and the operations briefing details active AI deployments including Microsoft Copilot, a lacera.gov chatbot in development, AI-enabled disability retirement medical report organization, and AI-enhanced Excel productivity for investment analysis (doc_id=1, 5). **SIB-ND** presented a formal AI integration roadmap progressing from current productivity tools toward portfolio monitoring and ultimately AI-driven investment recommendations (doc_id=1079). **LACERS ($25B)** adopted a formal Succession Policy establishing an order of succession — Executive Officer, Assistant General Manager, Chief Investment Officer — and submitted four Charter reform proposals to City Council to strengthen board independence (doc_id=921, 920).

**Colorado PERA** conducted a formal 2025 Board Performance Evaluation through **Mosaic Governance Advisors** with 14 of 16 trustees participating, identifying priority trustee education topics for 2026 including AI in investments, asset allocation, investment performance measurement, technology governance, and cybersecurity risk oversight (doc_id=406). The board also debated — and defeated **1-13** — an amendment to CEO evaluation criteria that would have measured investment performance "relative to liabilities" using "actuarial value of returns" (doc_id=404).

*Sources: [LACERA — Agenda — April 01, 2026](?doc=1), [LACERA — Agenda — April 01, 2026](?doc=5), [SIB-ND — Board Pack — March 13, 2026](?doc=1079), [LACERS — Agenda — March 24, 2026](?doc=921), [PERA Colorado — Board Pack — March 19, 2026](?doc=406), [PERA Colorado — Board Pack — March 20, 2026](?doc=404)*

**Bottom line:** Governance modernization is no longer confined to investment policy — boards are formalizing AI governance, succession protocols, and performance evaluation frameworks in ways that will shape institutional decision-making for years, and plans without these structures in place face increasing operational and fiduciary risk.
