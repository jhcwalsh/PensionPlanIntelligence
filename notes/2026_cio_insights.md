# CIO Insights: 2026 Institutional Trends
*Synthesized from board and investment committee activity across 46 U.S. public pension plans (~$3.4 trillion AUM)*
*Generated: April 15, 2026*

---

## 1. Private Equity Deployment Accelerates — But Structure and Fee Discipline Are Defining the Strategy

The most consistent investment signal across this dataset is sustained, large-scale private equity commitment activity, with a clear and deliberate shift toward cost-efficient structures. **CalPERS ($502B)** committed **$13.8 billion** in new PE investments in the first half of fiscal year 2026, with **41% deployed through no- or low-fee vehicles** including co-investments and Customized Investment Accounts (CIAs) ([doc_id=1011](?doc=1011)). The CIAs generated a **25.1% one-year return** versus **-0.5%** for the Fund of Funds sleeve — a performance gap that makes the structural preference self-reinforcing ([doc_id=1011](?doc=1011)).

**Minnesota SBI ($161B)** approved eleven private markets commitments totaling up to **$1.975 billion** at its October 2025 meeting, spanning managers including **Blackstone Supplemental Account – M** ($300M), **Strategic Partners X** ($250M), **Permira IX** ($250M), and **TPG Partners X** ($150M), among others ([doc_id=684](?doc=684)). **LACERA ($75B)** approved a **$350 million** commitment to **AE Industrial Partners IV, L.P.** alongside two co-investments — **$41 million** alongside **Kingswood Capital Management** and **$25 million** alongside **Primary Venture Partners** — all CIO-approved under delegated authority ([doc_id=84](?doc=84)). **WSIB ($200B)** advanced commitments to **Charterhouse Capital Partners XII** (pan-European mid-market buyout, targeting €1.5–2.0bn) and **Spark Capital IX** and **Spark Capital Growth VI** (venture and venture growth, $8.7B committed AUM firm) ([doc_id=682](?doc=682), [doc_id=1084](?doc=1084)).

The CalPERS data also surfaces a structural market concern: global median PE buyout purchase price multiples rose to **14.5x EBITDA** in 2025 from **13.3x** in 2024, while equity contributions now represent **66–69%** of deal financing versus mid-50s% in 2017–2019 ([doc_id=1011](?doc=1011)). Funds closed globally fell **38% year-over-year** in Q3 2025 even as aggregate deal value rose, reflecting concentration among larger managers ([doc_id=1011](?doc=1011)). Plans are responding by concentrating commitments with established GPs and using co-investment to reduce fee drag.

*Sources: [CalPERS — Agenda — March 16, 2026](?doc=1011), [MN SBI — Board Pack — March 26, 2026](?doc=684), [LACERA — Agenda — April 08, 2026](?doc=84), [WSIB — Board Pack — April 02, 2026](?doc=682), [WSIB — Agenda — April 16, 2026](?doc=1084)*

**Bottom line:** The PE allocation build is real and broad-based, but the defining characteristic of 2026 is structural discipline — plans are concentrating capital in co-investments, CIAs, and direct-to-GP relationships to capture the illiquidity premium while compressing fee loads. Managers unable to offer co-investment access or institutional fee structures face a shrinking addressable market among large public plans.

## 2. Private Equity Secondaries as the Preferred Catch-Up Vehicle for Underallocated Plans

A distinct sub-theme within private markets is the use of secondaries as a tactical tool by plans that are materially underweight their PE targets. **Dallas Police & Fire Pension System ($3B)** approved a **$35 million** commitment to **Blackstone Strategic Partners X** and up to **$15 million** to **Lexington Continuation Vehicle Investors** — both secondaries vehicles — voted **9-1** at its March 2026 meeting ([doc_id=1017](?doc=1017), [doc_id=570](?doc=570)). The rationale is explicit in the data: DPFP's private equity allocation stood at **1.8% actual versus a 6% target**, with total private markets at **11.2% versus an 18% target**, a **-$148 million** shortfall ([doc_id=1017](?doc=1017)). The plan anticipates **4–6 additional private markets commitments** throughout 2026 per its pacing plan ([doc_id=1017](?doc=1017)).

**PSERS ($73B)** similarly approved a commitment to **LS Power Equity Partners VI, L.P.** alongside **DRA Growth and Income Fund XII** and **Warwick Partners V** at its March 2026 Investment Committee meeting ([doc_id=380](?doc=380)). While PSERS' allocation gap is not quantified in the available data, the pattern of multi-commitment tranches at a single meeting is consistent with pacing acceleration.

The secondaries preference reflects a well-understood dynamic: J-curve mitigation, near-term distributions, and faster deployment relative to primary commitments. For plans with significant allocation gaps and active pacing plans, secondaries and continuation vehicles are functioning as the primary catch-up mechanism in 2026.

*Sources: [DPFP — Agenda — April 02, 2026](?doc=1017), [DPFP — Agenda — April 02, 2026](?doc=570), [PSERS — Agenda — March 19, 2026](?doc=380)*

**Bottom line:** Plans with structural PE underweights are using secondaries and continuation vehicles as the fastest path to target allocation. Managers raising secondary funds with strong LP relationships — particularly those already approved by multiple public plans — are well-positioned to capture this demand.

## 3. Portfolio Architecture Is Evolving: Alpha-Beta Separation and Total Portfolio Approaches Gain Traction

Several plans are undertaking fundamental restructuring of how they think about portfolio construction — moving beyond static asset class buckets toward integrated risk and return frameworks. **SIB-ND ($8B)** is executing a multi-phase evolution toward "Advanced Funds Management," explicitly separating alpha from market (beta) exposures, building overlay and portable alpha capabilities, and integrating AI tools into investment monitoring ([doc_id=1078](?doc=1078), [doc_id=1079](?doc=1079)). The plan's Equity 2.0 initiative delivered **18.0% in 2025 versus a 17.1% benchmark**, and a Fixed Income 2.0 re-underwriting is now underway with new managers expected by year-end 2026 ([doc_id=1078](?doc=1078), [doc_id=1079](?doc=1079)).

**CalPERS ($502B)** is transitioning to a **Total Portfolio Approach (TPA)**, which the board pack describes as a fundamental shift in capital allocation framework across asset classes ([doc_id=1011](?doc=1011)). **IPERS ($41B)** adopted Portfolio C, eliminating its **5% Global Smart Beta** allocation and reallocating **2% to core fixed income** and **3% to public credit**, while simultaneously hiring three absolute return/portable alpha managers — **Florin Court Capital**, **Capital Fund Management SA**, and **AHL Partners LLP** — in a single board action ([doc_id=82](?doc=82)). IPERS' CEO flagged a structural concern: tracking error of only **23 basis points** against a target of **150 bps**, signaling the fund is taking far less active risk than its policy allows ([doc_id=82](?doc=82)).

These are not cosmetic changes. The convergence on alpha-beta separation, portable alpha, and total portfolio frameworks across plans of very different sizes suggests a maturing of institutional investment philosophy — one that prioritizes capital efficiency and risk budgeting over traditional asset class silos.

*Sources: [SIB-ND — Board Pack — April 09, 2026](?doc=1078), [SIB-ND — Board Pack — March 13, 2026](?doc=1079), [CalPERS — Agenda — March 16, 2026](?doc=1011), [IPERS — Board Pack — March 27, 2026](?doc=82)*

**Bottom line:** The shift toward TPA and alpha-beta separation is accelerating among large plans and beginning to appear at mid-sized funds. Investment managers should expect increasing demand for portable alpha, overlay, and uncorrelated return strategies — and declining tolerance for high-fee, benchmark-hugging active mandates.

## 4. Fee Scrutiny Intensifies: Validation, Benchmarking, and Incentive Restructuring

Fee governance is receiving board-level attention across multiple plans, with concrete structural actions rather than aspirational language. **KPPA ($20B)** ran a formal RFP for Investment Cost Efficiency Services, selecting **Albourne America LLC** at **$2,000 per fund** for since-inception fee validation — a service that has analyzed over **7,000 investments** and validated over **$200 billion** in client assets globally ([doc_id=1020](?doc=1020)). The RFP process surfaced a conflict-of-interest concern: finalist **XTP Implementation Services** is a sister company of **Wilshire**, KPPA's existing general consultant, both owned by Monica Holdings LP ([doc_id=1020](?doc=1020)).

**Colorado PERA ($60B)** is restructuring its private equity incentive fee formula, proposing to increase excess return targets from **50 bps to 70 bps** (1-year), **30 bps to 40 bps** (3-year), and **25 bps to 30 bps** (5-year), phased in over five years ([doc_id=407](?doc=407)). PERA's internal management cost analysis found internal management costs of approximately **6.6 basis points** versus an estimated **$111 million** cost to externally manage the same assets — currently managed internally for approximately **$27 million** ([doc_id=407](?doc=407)).

**LACERS ($25B)** disclosed fee rates for two watch-listed managers: **Segall Bryant & Hamill** at **57 bps** (11th percentile among peers) and **Informed Momentum Company** at **50 bps** (4th percentile), with total fees paid to SBH since inception of **$3.6 million** ([doc_id=1089](?doc=1089)). The disclosure of peer-relative fee positioning alongside performance watch status signals that fee benchmarking is becoming a standard component of manager retention decisions.

*Sources: [KPPA — Board Pack — March 25, 2026](?doc=1020), [PERA Colorado — Board Pack — March 19, 2026](?doc=407), [LACERS — Agenda — April 14, 2026](?doc=1089)*

**Bottom line:** Fee validation is moving from a back-office function to a board-level governance priority. Plans are building independent fee verification infrastructure, restructuring incentive formulas to demand higher excess returns, and using peer-relative fee data in manager retention decisions. Managers charging above-median fees without commensurate alpha face increasing scrutiny.

## 5. Manager Watch Lists and Retention Under Performance Pressure — With Nuance

The data reveals a more nuanced approach to manager retention than a simple performance-termination model. **LACERS ($25B)** renewed contracts for both **Segall Bryant & Hamill** (U.S. Small Cap Value, ~$172M, on watch since August 2024 due to since-inception underperformance of **-1.55% vs. Russell 2000 Value**) and **Informed Momentum Company** (U.S. Small Cap Growth, ~$132M, on watch since June 2024), granting one-year renewals in both cases despite watch status ([doc_id=1089](?doc=1089)). The rationale: IMC posted **+14.5% fiscal year-to-date outperformance** versus the Russell 2000 Growth as of February 28, 2026, and SBH showed strong recent 1-year (**29.44% vs. 24.95%**) and 3-year (**15.0% vs. 12.39%**) results ([doc_id=1089](?doc=1089)).

**SIB-ND ($8B)** took the opposite approach, terminating **William Blair International Leaders** in early March 2026 and replacing it with **Principal International**, funded with approximately **$407 million** ([doc_id=1078](?doc=1078)). **IPERS ($41B)** flagged material concern with real assets manager **Heitman CREDIT** and minor concerns with fixed-income managers **Man** and **Mesirow**, and LARS manager **Welton Global** ([doc_id=82](?doc=82)).

The pattern suggests boards are distinguishing between cyclical underperformance (where recent recovery justifies patience) and structural concerns (where termination is warranted). One-year renewals for watch-listed managers are functioning as a structured probationary mechanism rather than a permanent reprieve.

*Sources: [LACERS — Agenda — April 14, 2026](?doc=1089), [SIB-ND — Board Pack — April 09, 2026](?doc=1078), [IPERS — Board Pack — March 27, 2026](?doc=82)*

**Bottom line:** Watch list status is not a binary termination trigger — it is a structured governance tool. Managers on watch should expect intensified reporting requirements and shorter contract terms, but recent performance recovery can preserve mandates. The key variable is whether underperformance is attributed to identifiable, reversible factors or to structural strategy deterioration.

## 6. Risk Mitigation Allocations and Fixed Income Are Being Actively Managed, Not Passively Held

Contrary to the assumption that fixed income is a passive, set-and-forget allocation, multiple plans are actively restructuring their defensive sleeves. **LACERA ($75B)** increased its Risk Reduction and Mitigation (RRM) target allocation from **19% to 24%** in its 2024 Strategic Asset Allocation, with the portfolio generating a **6.8% one-year return versus a 6.0% benchmark** as of February 2026 ([doc_id=85](?doc=85)). The RRM portfolio's **$22.26 billion** market value provides **37 months of benefit payments** in liquidity (excluding hedge funds), and the Investment Grade Bonds sleeve is structured **70% passive / 30% active** with effective duration of **5.8 years** and yield-to-worst of **4.2%** ([doc_id=85](?doc=85)).

**SIB-ND ($8B)** launched a Fixed Income 2.0 initiative — a full re-underwriting of its investment-grade portfolio — following the success of its Equity 2.0 program, with new managers expected by year-end 2026 ([doc_id=1079](?doc=1079)). The plan also approved a new internally managed **Long Government/Credit Enhanced Index Strategy** of approximately **$42 million** for the Grand Forks Pension Plan and Grand Forks Park District ([doc_id=1078](?doc=1078)). **IPERS ($41B)** reallocated its eliminated Global Smart Beta sleeve into **2% core fixed income** and **3% public credit** under Portfolio C ([doc_id=82](?doc=82)).

The macro context is relevant: the Federal Reserve held the federal funds rate at **3.50%–3.75%** at its March 2026 meeting (second consecutive pause), with the 10-year yield at approximately **4.3%** and core PCE sticky at approximately **3.1%** ([doc_id=1078](?doc=1078), [doc_id=84](?doc=84)). Plans are treating the current rate environment as an opportunity to lock in duration and restructure fixed income programs that were built for a different rate regime.

*Sources: [LACERA — Agenda — April 08, 2026](?doc=85), [SIB-ND — Board Pack — March 13, 2026](?doc=1079), [SIB-ND — Board Pack — April 09, 2026](?doc=1078), [IPERS — Board Pack — March 27, 2026](?doc=82)*

**Bottom line:** Fixed income is not a passive residual — it is being actively restructured across multiple plans to improve duration management, reduce fee loads, and better match liability profiles. Managers offering differentiated active fixed income strategies with clear benchmark-relative value propositions are finding receptive audiences.

## 7. Governance Modernization: AI Adoption, Succession Planning, and Operational Infrastructure

A cross-cutting theme is the formalization of governance infrastructure that had previously been informal or absent. **LACERA ($75B)** approved an AI Policy in March 2026 and is deploying Microsoft Copilot organization-wide, with AI projects in development including a member-facing chatbot, AI-enabled disability retirement medical report organization, and AI-enhanced investment analysis tools ([doc_id=5](?doc=5)). **SIB-ND ($8B)** presented an AI integration roadmap progressing from current productivity tools toward portfolio monitoring and ultimately AI-driven investment recommendations ([doc_id=1079](?doc=1079)).

**LACERS ($25B)** adopted a formal Succession Policy establishing an order of succession — Executive Officer, Assistant General Manager, Chief Investment Officer — in the event of a General Manager vacancy, fulfilling a 2022 Management Audit recommendation ([doc_id=921](?doc=921)). **KPPA ($20B)** surfaced a governance concern during its fee validation RFP: the potential conflict of interest created when a general investment consultant (Wilshire) and a fee validation finalist (XTP) share common ownership ([doc_id=1020](?doc=1020)).

**Colorado PERA ($60B)** conducted a formal board performance evaluation through **Mosaic Governance Advisors**, with 14 of 16 trustees participating, identifying key improvement areas including meeting effectiveness and role clarity between board and staff ([doc_id=406](?doc=406)). Priority trustee education topics for 2026 include AI in investments, asset allocation, investment performance measurement, and cybersecurity risk oversight ([doc_id=406](?doc=406)).

*Sources: [LACERA — Agenda — April 01, 2026](?doc=5), [SIB-ND — Board Pack — March 13, 2026](?doc=1079), [LACERS — Agenda — March 24, 2026](?doc=921), [KPPA — Board Pack — March 25, 2026](?doc=1020), [PERA Colorado — Board Pack — March 19, 2026](?doc=406)*

**Bottom line:** Governance modernization — AI policy, succession planning, conflict-of-interest protocols, and board self-evaluation — is moving from best practice to baseline expectation. Plans that have not formalized these frameworks face increasing audit and reputational risk, and service providers operating in multiple roles for the same client face heightened scrutiny.
