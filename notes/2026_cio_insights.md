# CIO Insights: 2026 Institutional Trends
*Synthesized from board and investment committee activity across 46 U.S. public pension plans (~$3.4 trillion AUM)*
*Generated: April 15, 2026*

## 1. Private Equity Deployment Accelerates — But Fee Discipline Is the New Differentiator

The most consistent signal across large plans is not simply that private equity allocations are growing — it is *how* capital is being deployed. **CalPERS ($502B)** committed **$13.8 billion** in new PE investments in the first half of fiscal year 2026, with **41% deployed through no- or low-fee vehicles** including co-investments and Customized Investment Accounts (CIAs) (doc_id=1011). The CIAs generated a **25.1% one-year return** versus **-0.5%** for the Fund of Funds sleeve — a gap that makes the fee-efficiency argument self-reinforcing (doc_id=1011). **LACERA ($75B)** approved a **$350 million** commitment to **AE Industrial Partners IV, L.P.** alongside two co-investments — **$41 million** alongside **Kingswood Capital Management** and **$25 million** alongside **Primary Venture Partners** — demonstrating the same co-investment-alongside-approved-manager model (doc_id=84). **Minnesota SBI ($161B)** approved eleven private markets commitments totaling up to **$1.975 billion** in a single October 2025 board action, spanning buyout, secondaries, and real assets across managers including **Advent International GPE XI**, **Blackstone Supplemental Account – M**, **Strategic Partners X**, **Permira IX**, and **TPG Partners X** (doc_id=684).

The secondaries market is drawing particular attention from smaller plans seeking faster J-curve mitigation. **Dallas Police & Fire Pension System ($3B)** committed **$35 million** to **Blackstone Strategic Partners X** and up to **$15 million** to **Lexington Continuation Vehicle Investors** — both secondaries vehicles — approved **9-1** at the March board meeting, with private equity severely underweight at **1.8% actual versus a 6% target** (doc_id=1017). **Houston Police Officers' Pension System ($8B)** had **Ares Pathfinder III, L.P.**, **Kingswood Capital Opportunities Fund IV, L.P.**, and **LS Power Equity Partners VI, L.P.** before its Investment Committee simultaneously (doc_id=568).

*Sources: [CalPERS — Agenda — March 16, 2026](?doc=1011), [LACERA — Agenda — April 08, 2026](?doc=84), [MN SBI — Board Pack — March 26, 2026](?doc=684), [DPFP — Agenda — April 02, 2026](?doc=1017), [HPOPS — Agenda — April 07, 2026](?doc=568)*

**Bottom line:** Fee structure is becoming as important as manager selection in PE. Plans that cannot access co-investments or CIAs at scale are structurally disadvantaged; the CalPERS data quantifies the performance cost of fund-of-funds reliance at roughly 25 percentage points of one-year return.

## 2. Portfolio Architecture Is Being Rebuilt — Alpha-Beta Separation Gains Traction

Several plans are moving beyond traditional asset class buckets toward more sophisticated portfolio construction frameworks. **North Dakota SIB ($8B)** is executing a three-phase roadmap toward "Advanced Funds Management," explicitly separating alpha from market exposures, with Phase 3 targeting overlay and portable alpha frameworks (doc_id=1078). The board funded **Arrowstreet US All Cap** with approximately **$790 million** across the Legacy Fund, Pension Pool, and Insurance Trust as part of this evolution (doc_id=1079). The Equity 2.0 initiative — an earlier phase of this restructuring — delivered **18.0% in 2025 versus a 17.1% benchmark** (doc_id=1078). A Fixed Income 2.0 re-underwriting is now underway, with new managers expected by year-end 2026 (doc_id=1079).

**IPERS ($41B)** adopted Portfolio C, eliminating its **5% Global Smart Beta** allocation and reallocating **2% to core fixed income** and **3% to public credit**, while simultaneously hiring three absolute return/portable alpha managers — **Florin Court Capital**, **Capital Fund Management SA**, and **AHL Partners LLP** — approved **7-0** (doc_id=82). CEO Greg Samorajski flagged a structural concern: IPERS' tracking error of **23 basis points** against a target of **150 basis points** signals the fund is running far less active risk than its policy allows, prompting a multi-meeting review (doc_id=82).

**CalPERS** is transitioning to a Total Portfolio Approach (TPA) that will fundamentally shift capital allocation across asset classes, with private equity expected to retain a meaningful role within the new framework (doc_id=1011).

*Sources: [SIB-ND — Board Pack — April 09, 2026](?doc=1078), [SIB-ND — Board Pack — March 13, 2026](?doc=1079), [IPERS — Board Pack — March 27, 2026](?doc=82), [CalPERS — Agenda — March 16, 2026](?doc=1011)*

**Bottom line:** The shift from static asset class buckets to dynamic alpha-beta separation is no longer a theoretical aspiration — it is being operationalized at plans ranging from $8B to $502B. Plans still organized around traditional policy portfolios should treat this as a structural competitive disadvantage in active risk deployment.

## 3. Risk Mitigation Portfolios Are Earning Their Allocation — But Duration Remains a Scar

The 2022 rate shock left lasting marks on liability-hedging portfolios, and boards are now evaluating whether those structures have rehabilitated. **LACERA ($75B)** increased its Risk Reduction and Mitigation (RRM) target allocation from **19% to 24%** in its 2024 Strategic Asset Allocation, with the portfolio generating a **6.8% one-year return versus a 6.0% benchmark** and a **5.1% three-year return versus 5.0%** as of February 28, 2026 (doc_id=85). The RRM portfolio's **$22.26 billion** market value provides **37 months of benefit payments** in liquidity (excluding hedge funds) — a metric that reflects explicit liquidity stress-testing embedded in the design (doc_id=85). However, the Long-Term Government Bonds sleeve carries an inception-to-date return of **-6.4% annualized** since its November 2021 launch, a direct consequence of the 2022 rate spike (doc_id=85).

**TMRS ($40B)** reported total fund performance of **13.29% in 2025, exceeding its benchmark by 1.07%** and adding **$5.6 billion** to the Trust, with current asset allocation targets of Public Equity 35%, Private Equity 20%, Credit 21%, Real Assets 18%, and Tactical Opportunities 6% (doc_id=504). NEPC projects a **3.7% illiquidity premium for private equity versus U.S. large-cap equity** over a 10-year horizon, supporting TMRS's continued overweight to private markets relative to many peers (doc_id=504).

*Sources: [LACERA — Agenda — April 08, 2026](?doc=85), [TMRS — Agenda — March 26, 2026](?doc=504)*

**Bottom line:** Risk mitigation allocations are delivering across multiple time horizons for plans that sized them correctly, but the duration scar from 2022 is still visible in inception-to-date figures. Boards reviewing long-duration hedging programs should distinguish between structural design validity and the timing cost of the 2021 launch cohort.

## 4. Fee Transparency and Cost Validation Are Becoming Governance Priorities

A quiet but significant trend: plans are institutionalizing fee oversight as a formal governance function rather than treating it as an incidental audit item. **Kentucky KPPA / CERS ($20B)** ran a competitive RFP for Investment Cost Efficiency Services, selecting **Albourne America LLC** at **$2,000 per fund for since-inception validation** with optional ongoing monitoring at **$1,300 per fund quarterly** or **$800 per fund annually** — chosen over contingent-fee competitors **bFinance** (charging **35% of identified savings**) and **XTP Implementation Services** (success-based, no fixed fee) (doc_id=1020). The board noted that Albourne has analyzed over **7,000 investments** and validated over **$200 billion** in client assets since launching the service in 2014 (doc_id=1020).

**Colorado PERA ($60B)** quantified its internal management advantage: internal costs of approximately **6.6 basis points** versus an estimated **$111 million** cost to externally manage the same assets, currently managed internally for approximately **$27 million** (doc_id=407). PERA is also advancing a Private Equity Incentive Phase-in, proposing to raise excess return targets from **50 bps to 70 bps** (one-year), **30 bps to 40 bps** (three-year), and **25 bps to 30 bps** (five-year) phased over five years — a direct tightening of the performance bar for internal PE managers (doc_id=407).

**IPERS ($41B)** reported FY2025 preliminary investment management expenses of **$62.08 million (0.137% of average fund value)**, a decrease of **33.1% from FY2024**, and ranked as a low-cost fund in CEM Benchmarking with a five-year net value added of **0.8%** (doc_id=82).

*Sources: [KPPA — Board Pack — March 25, 2026](?doc=1020), [PERA Colorado — Board Pack — March 19, 2026](?doc=407), [IPERS — Board Pack — March 27, 2026](?doc=82)*

**Bottom line:** Fee validation is transitioning from audit function to strategic investment tool. The CERS RFP outcome signals that fixed-fee, since-inception validation models are preferred over contingent arrangements — a preference that reflects both conflict-of-interest concerns and the desire for comprehensive historical coverage rather than prospective savings only.

## 5. Technology Modernization and AI Adoption Are Moving from Strategy to Execution

Across multiple plans, AI and technology infrastructure have moved from board education topics to funded operational programs. **LACERA ($75B)** has **Microsoft Copilot** deployed organization-wide, with AI projects in active development including a lacera.gov chatbot, AI-enabled disability retirement medical report organization, AI-enhanced Excel productivity for investment analysis, and future applications for board meeting assistance and contract research (doc_id=5). The mainframe migration — a **$920,000 project** — is on track with AI and automated testing, targeting completion in June 2028 (doc_id=5).

**North Dakota SIB ($8B)** presented a formal AI integration roadmap progressing from current productivity tools through portfolio monitoring toward AI-driven investment recommendations, alongside an RFP for investment performance and benchmarking consulting services under a fixed-fee structure (doc_id=1079). Staff explicitly flagged AI tools as a mechanism to strengthen investment analysis and monitoring (doc_id=1078).

**Dallas Police & Fire Pension System ($3B)** is undertaking a pension administration software upgrade from a **23-year-old PensionGold v2 system**, with a pre-engagement phase with **LRS** estimated at **$300,000** and total pre-engagement budget of approximately **$425,000** (doc_id=1017). The technology gap at smaller plans — where legacy systems constrain analytical capacity — is becoming a governance risk in its own right.

*Sources: [LACERA — Agenda — April 01, 2026](?doc=5), [SIB-ND — Board Pack — March 13, 2026](?doc=1079), [SIB-ND — Board Pack — April 09, 2026](?doc=1078), [DPFP — Agenda — April 02, 2026](?doc=1017)*

**Bottom line:** The technology gap between large and small plans is widening. Plans with sub-$10B AUM running decade-old administration systems face compounding disadvantages: limited analytical capacity, higher operational risk, and reduced ability to implement sophisticated portfolio strategies. Boards should treat technology modernization as an investment in governance capacity, not merely an administrative cost.

## 6. Geopolitical and Policy Uncertainty Is Reshaping Manager Mandates — Quietly

*Emerging signal — limited data*

Several plans flagged geopolitical and U.S. policy developments as active investment considerations rather than background context. **WSIB ($200B)** dedicated agenda time to geopolitical outlook and U.S. policy developments alongside its investment manager recommendations, and held a dedicated discussion on energy transition investing and climate change considerations (doc_id=1084). **LACERA ($75B)** noted ongoing U.S. tariff and trade policy uncertainty pressuring supply chains and business sentiment, with the Federal Reserve holding the federal funds rate at **3.50%–3.75%** at its March 2026 meeting — the second consecutive pause — and February 2026 CPI at **2.4% year-over-year** (doc_id=84). LACERA staff also held discussions with emerging markets managers regarding Middle East strategy changes following geopolitical developments, and noted LACERA has no known exposure to Iran (doc_id=1090).

**Colorado PERA ($60B)** included divestment from companies with prohibitions against Israel as a consent agenda action item (doc_id=404), while **North Dakota SIB** noted that social investing and economically targeted investing are prohibited unless meeting the exclusive benefit rule (doc_id=1078).

*Sources: [WSIB — Agenda — April 16, 2026](?doc=1084), [LACERA — Agenda — April 08, 2026](?doc=84), [LACERA — Agenda — April 14, 2026](?doc=1090), [PERA Colorado — Board Pack — March 20, 2026](?doc=404)*

**Bottom line:** Geopolitical considerations are influencing manager mandates, emerging markets positioning, and restricted company lists simultaneously — but the policy responses vary widely by plan. CIOs should expect continued board-level pressure on geopolitical exposure disclosures, particularly in emerging markets and energy transition mandates.
