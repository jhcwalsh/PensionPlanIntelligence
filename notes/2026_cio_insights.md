# CIO Insights: 2026 Institutional Trends

## 1. Private Markets: Structural Acceleration, Not Just Cyclical Deployment

The pace and architecture of private markets commitments in 2026 signal a fundamental structural shift rather than opportunistic deployment. **Minnesota SBI ($161B)** approved eleven commitments totaling up to $1.975 billion in a single October 2025 meeting, spanning buyouts (**Advent International GPE XI at $150M**, **Permira IX at $250M**, **TPG Partners X at $150M**), secondaries (**Strategic Partners X at $250M**, **Blackstone Supplemental Account at $300M**), and real assets (**TA Realty Value-Add Fund XIV at $150M**). **LACERA ($75B)** committed $416 million in a single CIO-delegated tranche including a $350 million anchor to **AE Industrial Partners IV** and co-investments alongside **Kingswood Capital Management** and **Primary Venture Partners**. **WSIB ($200B)** is simultaneously building across European mid-market buyout (**Charterhouse Capital Partners XII**, targeting €1.5–2.0bn with CCP X generating 20% net IRR and 2.0x TVPI), venture (**Spark Capital IX** and **Spark Capital Growth VI** from an $8.7B AUM firm), and private credit via its Innovation Portfolio (**Monarch Capital Partners VII**).

The underweighted narrative: structural fund concentration is intensifying. Global PE fundraising showed the number of funds closed fell 38% year-over-year in Q3 2025 even as aggregate capital raised held steady — per **CalPERS ($502B)** data. Mega-fund allocators are crowding the same 20–30 GPs. **Dallas Police & Fire ($3B)** illustrates the other end: private markets are 11.2% actual versus an 18% target, a $148M shortfall driving an active pacing plan — two sequential commitments to secondaries (**Blackstone Strategic Partners X at $35M**, **Lexington Continuation Vehicle at $15M**) approved 9-1. Plans undershooting private market targets are being forced into secondaries and continuation vehicles rather than primaries, accepting vintage-year compression at elevated NAV entry points.

*Sources: [MN SBI — Board Pack — March 26, 2026](?doc=684), [LACERA — Agenda — April 08, 2026](?doc=84), [WSIB — Board Pack — April 02, 2026](?doc=682), [CalPERS — Agenda — March 16, 2026](?doc=1011), [DPFP — Agenda — April 02, 2026](?doc=1017)*

**Bottom line:** The bifurcation between mega-allocators with deliberate pacing infrastructure and smaller plans scrambling to deploy via secondaries is widening; managers should expect continued concentration of LP capital in existing relationships and continuation vehicles rather than new fund formation.

## 2. Fee Optimization and Cost Architecture as Alpha Source

Fee scrutiny has graduated from a governance checkbox to a strategic investment function. **Kentucky KPPA ($20B)** ran a competitive RFP with three finalists — **Albourne America LLC**, **bFinance**, and **XTP Implementation Services** — for dedicated fee validation services, ultimately recommending Albourne at $2,000 per fund for since-inception validation. Albourne has now validated over $200 billion in client assets across more than 7,000 investments since 2014. The conflict analysis embedded in the KPPA deliberations is itself instructive: **XTP and Wilshire are sister companies under Monica Holdings LP** (majority-owned by Motive Partners and CC Capital), and Wilshire already serves as general consultant — a structural conflict the committee had to explicitly address.

**CalPERS** quantified the cost advantage of internal management: approximately 6.6 basis points internally versus an estimated $111 million to externally manage the same assets, with the internal program running at ~$27 million. **IPERS ($41B)** reported FY2025 investment management expenses of $62.08 million (0.137% of average fund value), a 33.1% year-over-year decrease. **Colorado PERA ($60B)** is proposing a Private Equity Incentive Phase-in that would raise excess-return targets for incentive compensation — 1-year threshold moving from 50bps to 70bps, 3-year from 30bps to 40bps, 5-year from 25bps to 30bps — effectively raising the bar for fee-sharing with internal managers. CalPERS deployed 41% of $13.8 billion in H1 FY2026 PE commitments through no- or low-fee vehicles (co-investments and CIAs), with its CIA structure generating 25.1% versus the total PE portfolio's 17.8% one-year return.

*Sources: [KPPA — Board Pack — March 25, 2026](?doc=1020), [CalPERS — Agenda — March 16, 2026](?doc=1011), [IPERS — Board Pack — March 27, 2026](?doc=82), [PERA Colorado — Board Pack — March 19, 2026](?doc=407)*

**Practical implication:** Fee validation and co-investment infrastructure are now measurable alpha contributors — plans without a dedicated cost-efficiency function or access to co-investment sourcing are systematically underperforming peers on a net-of-fee basis.

## 3. Portfolio Architecture Modernization: Alpha-Beta Separation and Total Portfolio Approaches

A quiet but significant convergence is underway in how large plans think about portfolio construction. **North Dakota SIB ($27.7B AUM)** is explicitly executing a three-phase roadmap toward "Advanced Funds Management" — separating alpha from beta, adding uncorrelated alpha sources including alternative strategies and sector specialists, and building toward portable alpha overlays. The $790 million funding of **Arrowstreet US All Cap** across the Legacy Fund, Pension Pool, and Insurance Trust reflects this: a high-active-share, systematic manager deployed at scale as a distinct alpha vehicle, not a benchmark-hugging equity sleeve. SIB also hired **Principal International** at $407 million after terminating **William Blair International Leaders**, explicitly on benchmark and strategy fit.

**CalPERS** is executing a parallel transition to a Total Portfolio Approach (TPA) that will "fundamentally shift capital allocation framework across asset classes." **IPERS** CEO Greg Samorajski flagged the inverse problem: tracking error of only 23 basis points against a 150bps target, an active risk budget almost entirely unexploited. The board commissioned a multi-meeting review. **IPERS** simultaneously hired three absolute return / portable alpha managers — **Florin Court Capital** (Florin Court Capital Programme), **Capital Fund Management SA** (CFM ISTEC Program), and **AHL Partners LLP** (AHL Cat Bond Program) — to begin filling that risk budget.

The underweighted signal: these architectural shifts require new benchmarking frameworks that most plans lack. SIB issued an RFP for a performance and benchmarking consultant specifically to support Phase 3.0. **TMRS ($40B)** is conducting a trustee risk tolerance survey via **NEPC** to inform efficient frontier modeling — the precursor to an asset allocation update that will likely increase alternatives exposure from the current 6% Tactical Opportunities sleeve.

*Sources: [SIB-ND — Board Pack — March 13, 2026](?doc=1079), [SIB-ND — Board Pack — April 09, 2026](?doc=1078), [CalPERS — Agenda — March 16, 2026](?doc=1011), [IPERS — Board Pack — March 27, 2026](?doc=82), [TMRS — Agenda — March 26, 2026](?doc=504)*

**Practical implication:** Plans with underspent active risk budgets represent the most actionable near-term client opportunity for systematic, alternative, and portable alpha managers — this is not a fringe development but a deliberate strategic priority at multiple plans representing combined AUM exceeding $300 billion.

## 4. Macro Navigation: Rate Plateaus, Equity Drawdowns, and Duration Reassessment

The macro environment across Q1 2026 presented a consistent headwind: the Federal Reserve held at 3.50%–3.75% for consecutive meetings, the S&P 500 fell -4.3% YTD through March 31 with the Russell 3000 down -4.0%, and core PCE remained sticky at approximately 3.1%. Against this backdrop, plan-level responses diverged sharply.

**LACERA** increased its Risk Reduction and Mitigation allocation from 19% to 24% in its 2024 Strategic Asset Allocation. The $22.26 billion RRM portfolio — comprising Investment Grade Bonds ($11.7B, 70% passive/30% active at 5.8-year duration), Long-Term Government Bonds ($1.7B, 13.9-year duration), Hedge Funds ($7.5B), and Cash ($1.3B) — returned 6.8% versus a 6.0% benchmark over one year. The Long Government sleeve carries an inception-to-date return of -6.4% annualized (inception November 2021) — a reminder that duration positioning extracted during the 2022 rate spike has not yet recovered. **SCERS ($12B)** committed $450 million to **Man Numeric** for a Global Core ex-US Strategy, consistent with a rotation toward international equity that outperformed U.S. markets YTD. **LACERS** separately approved an RFP for Global Listed Infrastructure — an inflation-sensitive real asset increasingly in demand as a fixed-income substitute.

**TMRS** reported 2025 total fund performance of 13.29%, outperforming its benchmark by 107 basis points — driven by an asset allocation with 20% private equity, 18% real assets, and 21% credit that is better positioned for a higher-nominal-return environment than the traditional 60/40 framework. Its 10-year forward NEPC projection shows private equity at 8.2% versus 4.5% for U.S. large-cap equity, a 370bps illiquidity premium.

*Sources: [LACERA — Agenda — April 08, 2026](?doc=85), [SIB-ND — Board Pack — April 09, 2026](?doc=1078), [TMRS — Agenda — March 26, 2026](?doc=504), [SCERS — Agenda — March 18, 2026](?doc=256), [DPFP — Agenda — April 02, 2026](?doc=1017)*

**Bottom line:** Plans with high fixed-income overweights relative to policy targets — **Dallas Police & Fire** carries fixed income at 19.4% versus a 13% target — face a dual drag: underexposure to equity recovery and duration risk should rates re-accelerate; the imperative to rebalance is structural, not tactical.

## 5. Governance Professionalization: Internal Controls, Technology, and Succession Infrastructure

A consistent thread across board activity is the institutionalization of operational infrastructure that has historically been underfunded in public plans. **LACERA** is deploying Microsoft Copilot broadly, developing AI-assisted disability medical report organization, an AI-enabled lacera.gov chatbot, and AI-enhanced Excel tools for investment analysis — with a formal AI Policy approved 7-0 in March 2026. Its mainframe migration is a $920,000 project targeting June 2028. **Colorado PERA's** 2025 internal audit recovered approximately $770,000 in employer and member contributions through compliance audits, while its Cash Overlay audit and Wire Transfer Monitoring both received medium-risk ratings — signaling that operational risk in private markets settlement and overlay execution warrants board-level attention. **LACERS (City, $25B)** adopted a Succession Policy establishing an explicit order (Executive Officer → Assistant General Manager → CIO), directly addressing a 2022 management audit recommendation that had gone unimplemented for four years.

**PSERS ($73B)** addressed the Verus/Cerity merger at its Investment Committee — a reminder that consultant consolidation creates concentration risk in fiduciary advisory services. **OCERS ($25B)** approved its 2026 Audit Plan with three open management action items in Investments and Member Services, simultaneously recruiting for a vacant Director of Internal Audit following a resignation — an operational gap that investment committees should monitor.

*Sources: [LACERA — Agenda — April 01, 2026](?doc=5), [PERA Colorado — Board Pack — March 19, 2026](?doc=408), [LACERS — Agenda — March 24, 2026](?doc=921), [PSERS — Agenda — March 19, 2026](?doc=380), [OCERS — Agenda — March 26, 2026](?doc=935)*

**Practical implication:** Operational governance gaps — vacant audit leadership, underdeveloped succession policies, absent fee validation infrastructure — are now material investment risks, not administrative afterthoughts; boards that treat them as such will face compounding exposures as private market complexity increases.

## 6. The Geopolitical Portfolio: Divestment Mandates and ESG Recalibration

Political pressure on investment mandates is intensifying with measurable portfolio implications. **Colorado PERA** included divestment from companies with prohibitions against Israel as a consent agenda action item — a directional shift consistent with Anti-BDS legislation appearing as a recurring governance item (a June 2026 agenda item references an "Anti-BDS 50bps calculation"). **WSIB** held a formal discussion on Energy Transition investing and climate change alongside its Economically Targeted Investments Annual Report, while **Charterhouse Capital Partners** — seeking WSIB's commitment to CCP XII — highlighted its B Corp certification (May 2025) and net-zero-by-2030 targets as LP-facing credentials. **LACERA** submitted a comment letter to the UK Financial Conduct Authority on sustainability reporting requirements, signaling active engagement in global ESG regulatory developments even as domestic political pressure pushes in the opposite direction.

The signal that deserves more attention: geopolitical fragmentation is creating manager-level complexity. LACERA staff explicitly noted they were directing emerging markets managers to review Middle East strategy changes in real time following geopolitical developments, with no existing Iran exposure confirmed. **North Dakota SIB**'s IPS explicitly prohibits social investing and economically targeted investing "unless meeting the Exclusive Benefit Rule" — a legal anchor increasingly invoked by plans under political pressure.

*Sources: [PERA Colorado — Board Pack — March 20, 2026](?doc=404), [WSIB — Agenda — April 16, 2026](?doc=1084), [LACERA — Agenda — April 08, 2026](?doc=84), [SIB-ND — Board Pack — March 13, 2026](?doc=1079)*

**Bottom line:** The ESG polarization narrative obscures the operational reality: regardless of political direction, geopolitical fragmentation is forcing investment staff to actively manage portfolio exposures at the manager and country level in real time — a capability that requires infrastructure most plans are still building.
