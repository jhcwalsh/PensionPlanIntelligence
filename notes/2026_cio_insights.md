# CIO Insights: 2026 Institutional Trends
*Synthesized from board and investment committee activity across 46 U.S. public pension plans (~$3.4 trillion AUM)*
*Generated: April 15, 2026*

---

## 1. Private Markets Deployment Accelerates Despite Structural Underweights

The most consistent theme across the dataset is aggressive private markets commitment pacing against a backdrop of persistent underallocation. **Dallas Police & Fire Pension System ($3B, TX)** entered 2026 with private equity at 1.8% actual versus a 6% target and private credit at effectively 0% versus a 4% target, driving a board-approved pacing plan for 4–6 additional commitments throughout the year ([source](?doc=1017)). **Kentucky Public Pensions Authority / CERS ($20B, KY)** similarly reported private equity NAV of approximately $1.08 billion against a $1.6 billion target on a $20 billion plan, prompting adoption of a $600 million annual commitment pace for 2025–2026 stepping down to $400 million thereafter ([source](?doc=1022)). **IPERS ($41B, IA)** approved a CY2026 private markets commitment plan with estimated maximums of $750 million to private real assets, $700 million to private equity, and $575 million to private credit ([source](?doc=82)).

At the large-fund end, **CalPERS ($502B, CA)** committed $13.8 billion in new PE investments in the first half of fiscal year 2026, with 41% deployed through no- or low-fee vehicles including co-investments and Customized Investment Accounts ([source](?doc=1011)). **LACERA ($75B, CA)** approved over $1.8 billion in new commitments across real estate, credit, and private equity in January 2026 alone, including a $750 million multi-asset credit mandate to **Neuberger Berman**, a $400 million commitment to **Green Equity Investors X**, and a $400 million commitment to **PRISA** ([source](?doc=86)). **Minnesota SBI ($161B, MN)** approved eleven private markets commitments totaling up to $1.975 billion in a single October 2025 meeting, spanning **Advent International GPE XI**, **Blackstone Supplemental Account – M**, **Permira IX**, **TPG Partners X**, and others ([source](?doc=684)).

The signal here is not simply that plans are buying private markets — it is that the underweight is structural and multi-year, meaning deployment pressure will persist regardless of near-term public market volatility. Plans that are still building toward target will be price-insensitive buyers of vintage 2026 and 2027 funds.

*Sources: [DPFP — Agenda — April 02, 2026](?doc=1017), [KPPA — Board Pack — February 25, 2026](?doc=1022), [IPERS — Board Pack — March 27, 2026](?doc=82), [CalPERS — Agenda — March 16, 2026](?doc=1011), [LACERA — Agenda — February 11, 2026](?doc=86), [MN SBI — Board Pack — March 26, 2026](?doc=684)*

**Bottom line:** Structural underweights at plans ranging from $3 billion to $502 billion will sustain elevated private markets commitment volumes through at least 2027; GPs with established LP relationships are well-positioned to capture re-up capital at scale.

## 2. Fee Pressure and Cost Efficiency Are Becoming Governance Priorities

Fee scrutiny has moved from a background concern to an explicit board-level agenda item. **KPPA / CERS** issued a formal RFP for Investment Cost Efficiency Services, ultimately recommending **Albourne America LLC** for fee validation at $2,000 per fund for since-inception validation, citing Albourne's analysis of over 7,000 investments and $200 billion in validated client assets ([source](?doc=1020)). The competing finalist **bFinance** proposed a contingent fee model charging 35% of identified savings — a structure the board viewed as a potential conflict of interest ([source](?doc=1020)).

**Colorado PERA ($60B, CO)** quantified its internal management advantage at approximately $111 million annually versus estimated external management costs of the same assets, with internal costs running approximately 6.6 basis points against an estimated 27.3 basis points externally ([source](?doc=415)). **OPERS ($97B, OH)** reported total estimated investment program costs of $585.1 million or 45.2 basis points of AUM for 2026, with CEM Benchmarking placing actual 2024 costs at 33.4 basis points excluding performance fees, below the peer median of 37.4 basis points ([source](?doc=20)). **WSIB ($200B, WA)** highlighted **Public Pension Capital LLC**'s capped carry structure of 10% maximum versus the market standard 20%, with the fund's own analysis estimating this generates approximately 2.89% higher net returns per year on identical cash flows ([source](?doc=681)).

The LACERA Investment Committee data adds granularity: **Segall Bryant & Hamill** charges 57 basis points (~$929,772/year) ranking in the 11th percentile among peers, while **Informed Momentum Company** charges 50 basis points (~$609,167/year) ranking in the 4th percentile — both retained despite watch status partly because of competitive fee positioning ([source](?doc=1089)).

*Sources: [KPPA — Board Pack — March 25, 2026](?doc=1020), [PERA Colorado — Board Pack — November 20, 2025](?doc=415), [OPERS — Agenda — January 21, 2026](?doc=20), [WSIB — Board Pack — February 19, 2026](?doc=681), [LACERS — Agenda — April 14, 2026](?doc=1089)*

**Bottom line:** Fee validation is becoming a formal governance function, not just a negotiating tactic; managers with above-median fee structures and mediocre performance face compounding termination risk as plans institutionalize cost benchmarking.

## 3. Portfolio Architecture Is Shifting Toward Risk Diversification and Portable Alpha

Several plans are undertaking structural portfolio redesigns that go beyond asset allocation tweaks. **OPERS** approved a significant increase in Risk Mitigation Strategies from 3.5% to 10% of the Defined Benefit Fund, funded by reducing Core Fixed Income from 8.6% to 5% and trimming Private Equity and Real Estate targets, with the transition supported by temporary monthly benchmark resets through June 2026 ([source](?doc=20)). **IPERS** eliminated its 5% Global Smart Beta allocation entirely, reallocating 2% to core fixed income and 3% to public credit, while simultaneously hiring three portable alpha managers — **Florin Court Capital**, **Capital Fund Management SA**, and **AHL Partners LLP** — for absolute return strategies ([source](?doc=82)). The IPERS CEO flagged that the fund's tracking error of 23 basis points was far below the 150 basis point target, signaling a multi-meeting review of active risk policy ([source](?doc=82)).

**SIB-ND ($8B, ND)** is executing a three-phase evolution toward "Advanced Funds Management," explicitly targeting alpha-beta separation, new uncorrelated alpha sources, and a portable alpha overlay framework as a Phase 3 goal, while simultaneously transitioning private markets from a fund-of-funds to a direct GP model projected to save over $6 million annually in management fees ([source](?doc=1079)). **NCRS ($120B, NC)** proposed increasing real asset equity from 12.5% to 18% and credit from 7% to 9% while reducing corporate equity from 48% to 44% and investment grade debt from 29.5% to 25%, informed by an Asset Liability Management study ([source](?doc=742)).

These are not incremental rebalances. They represent a coherent institutional view that traditional 60/40-adjacent structures are insufficient for the current environment of sticky inflation, elevated equity valuations, and compressed fixed income returns.

*Sources: [OPERS — Agenda — January 21, 2026](?doc=20), [IPERS — Board Pack — March 27, 2026](?doc=82), [SIB-ND — Board Pack — March 13, 2026](?doc=1079), [NCRS — Board Pack — January 29, 2026](?doc=742)*

**Bottom line:** Plans are actively restructuring away from passive fixed income ballast toward diversifying strategies, portable alpha, and real assets — a structural shift that will increase demand for liquid alternatives, trend-following, and infrastructure managers over the next 18–24 months.

## 4. Co-Investment Programs Are Maturing Into Strategic Priorities

Co-investments have moved from opportunistic supplements to deliberate portfolio construction tools. **CalPERS** deployed 41% of its first-half FY2026 PE commitments through no- or low-fee vehicles including co-investments and Customized Investment Accounts, with its CIA structure generating a 25.1% one-year return versus 12.9% for buyouts and -0.5% for Fund of Funds ([source](?doc=1011)). **TMRS ($40B, TX)** reported co-investments reaching 9% of its private market portfolio as of September 2025, with co-investments outperforming other private market assets by approximately 11% on a three-year basis, managed through the **Trinity River Holdings** vehicle in partnership with **GCM Grosvenor** ([source](?doc=506)). **LACERA** approved co-investment commitments of $41 million alongside **Kingswood Capital Management** and $25 million alongside **Primary Venture Partners** in March 2026, and $50 million alongside **Partners Group** in January 2026 ([source](?doc=87), [source](?doc=86)). **OCERS ($25B, CA)** committed $163 million to co-investment opportunities in 2025 and expanded its co-investment program to private market asset classes beyond real estate ([source](?doc=941)).

The performance data from CalPERS is particularly instructive: the Fund of Funds structure returned -0.5% over one year while CIAs returned 25.1% ([source](?doc=1011)). This performance gap, combined with the fee advantage, is accelerating the structural shift away from fund-of-funds toward direct and co-investment vehicles across the institutional market.

*Sources: [CalPERS — Agenda — March 16, 2026](?doc=1011), [TMRS — Agenda — December 11, 2025](?doc=506), [LACERA — Board Pack — March 11, 2026](?doc=87), [LACERA — Agenda — February 11, 2026](?doc=86), [OCERS — Agenda — February 25, 2026](?doc=941)*

**Bottom line:** Co-investment programs are now a core return-enhancement and fee-reduction tool; GPs that cannot offer meaningful co-investment access alongside fund commitments face a structural disadvantage in LP capital raising.

## 5. Real Assets Outperformance Is Driving Allocation Increases

Real assets delivered some of the strongest relative performance in the dataset, and boards are responding with allocation increases. **LACERA**'s Real Assets ex-Real Estate portfolio returned 12.1% for the one-year period ending December 31, 2025, outperforming its custom benchmark of 7.6% by 450 basis points, with Natural Resources returning 17.8% versus a 4.1% benchmark and infrastructure co-investments returning 23.1% since inception versus a 9.9% benchmark ([source](?doc=88)). **NCRS** proposed increasing real asset equity from 12.5% to 18% of the total fund ([source](?doc=742)). **OCERS** approved a 2026 real assets pacing plan of $350 million and a private income strategies pacing plan of $400 million per year on a rolling three-year basis ([source](?doc=941)). **IPERS** allocated a maximum of $750 million to private real assets in its CY2026 commitment plan ([source](?doc=82)).

LACERA's committee noted that gold demand surged to record highs in 2025 amid geopolitical tension, and that structurally rising demand for metals tied to AI, electrification, and industrial automation supports long-term Natural Resources investment dynamics ([source](?doc=88)). **OCERS**' consultant **Townsend** increased long-term real estate return expectations, with core up 50 basis points to 6.5% and value-add up 200 basis points to 8.5% ([source](?doc=941)). **Meketa**'s 2026 capital market assumptions presented to OCERS showed infrastructure at 7.5% and real estate at 7.1% ten-year expected returns versus U.S. equity at 6.3% ([source](?doc=941)).

*Sources: [LACERA — Agenda — March 11, 2026](?doc=88), [NCRS — Board Pack — January 29, 2026](?doc=742), [OCERS — Agenda — February 25, 2026](?doc=941), [IPERS — Board Pack — March 27, 2026](?doc=82)*

**Bottom line:** Real assets are no longer a diversifier of last resort — they are delivering benchmark-beating returns and attracting incremental allocation from plans across the size spectrum; managers in infrastructure, natural resources, and data center-adjacent real estate are in a strong fundraising position.

## 6. Private Equity Benchmark Reform Is Gaining Traction

A quiet but significant governance shift is underway in how plans measure private equity performance. **KPPA / CERS** and **KPPA / KRS** both adopted a "neutralized" benchmarking approach for private equity, replacing the prior Russell 3000 + 300 basis points benchmark with a methodology that uses actual time-weighted PE returns as the benchmark contribution to total fund roll-up, while separately reporting dollar-weighted IRR versus Public Market Equivalent on a quarterly basis ([source](?doc=1022), [source](?doc=1026)). The prior benchmark had produced distortions: KRS reported PE performance of 8.33% versus an 18.3% benchmark in one quarterly period under the old methodology ([source](?doc=1026)). **VRS ($110B, VA)** hired **Mercer** to independently review benchmarks and hurdle rates for Private Equity, Credit Strategies, and Diversifying Strategies, with results due April 2026 ([source](?doc=633)). **Colorado PERA** is transitioning to a new private equity incentive fee structure with higher excess return targets phased in over five years, increasing the one-year target from 50 to 70 basis points and the five-year target from 25 to 30 basis points ([source](?doc=407)).

The common thread is that plans are recognizing that public equity plus a premium benchmark creates artificial volatility in PE performance measurement and can mask genuine manager skill. The shift toward neutralized or PME-based approaches will change how GPs are evaluated and retained.

*Sources: [KPPA — Board Pack — February 25, 2026](?doc=1022), [KPPA — Board Pack — January 12, 2026](?doc=1026), [VRS — Board Pack — January 30, 2026](?doc=633), [PERA Colorado — Board Pack — March 19, 2026](?doc=407)*

**Bottom line:** The move away from public equity plus premium benchmarks for private equity is accelerating; GPs should expect more rigorous PME-based evaluation and tighter hurdle structures in new fund negotiations with sophisticated LPs.

## 7. Technology Modernization and AI Adoption Are Becoming Investment Operations Imperatives

AI and technology modernization appeared on investment committee agendas at multiple plans, moving beyond IT committee discussions into the investment function itself. **SIB-ND** presented an AI integration roadmap progressing from current productivity tools toward portfolio monitoring and ultimately AI-driven investment recommendations, alongside a broader Advanced Funds Management strategy ([source](?doc=1079)). **LACERA** is developing an AI-enabled disability retirement medical report organization tool, an AI-enhanced Excel productivity tool for investment analysis, and a lacera.gov chatbot, with Microsoft Copilot already deployed organization-wide ([source](?doc=5)). **OCERS** noted AI tools being explored for fee verification and cash flow modeling in private markets ([source](?doc=941)). **VRS** made a $35 million co-investment in an AI-related company via its Exposure Management Portfolio through **JP Morgan** ([source](?doc=633)). **Colorado PERA** received a board education session on AI in investments delivered by Charlotte Wood, Global Head of AI and Innovation at **Schroders** ([source](?doc=411)).

On the operational side, **IPERS** implemented an AI-powered phone system (Amazon Connect) and a cloud-based pension administration platform (Vitech), funded by a $60 million legislative appropriation over ten years ([source](?doc=61)). **LACERA** is executing a $920,000 mainframe migration with AI and automated testing, targeting completion in June 2028 ([source](?doc=5)).

*Sources: [SIB-ND — Board Pack — March 13, 2026](?doc=1079), [LACERA — Agenda — April 01, 2026](?doc=5), [OCERS — Agenda — February 25, 2026](?doc=941), [VRS — Board Pack — January 30, 2026](?doc=633), [PERA Colorado — Board Pack — November 21, 2025](?doc=411), [IPERS — Board Pack — September 25, 2025](?doc=61)*

**Bottom line:** AI adoption in pension investment operations is moving from pilot to production; plans that invest in data infrastructure and AI tooling now will have a structural advantage in manager monitoring, fee validation, and portfolio analytics within three to five years.
