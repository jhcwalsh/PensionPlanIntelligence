# CIO Insights: 2026 Institutional Trends
*Synthesized from board and investment committee activity across 35 U.S. public pension plans (~$2.9 trillion AUM)*
*Generated: April 13, 2026*

---

## 1. The Structural Shift Away from Asset Class Buckets

The most consequential long-term signal from 2026 board rooms is the move — led by CalPERS and SIB-ND but visible as an aspiration across many mid-sized plans — away from fixed asset class target allocations toward total portfolio risk frameworks. CalPERS is calling it TPA (Total Portfolio Approach); SIB-ND maps it as a three-phase "Advanced Funds Management" roadmap. The implication is significant: as plans mature toward this model, manager mandates will increasingly be sized by marginal risk contribution rather than asset class buckets, and the concept of being "overweight" or "underweight" private equity will give way to questions about portfolio-level factor exposures.

**Practical implication:** Managers who can articulate their strategies in risk-factor terms — not just asset class terms — will be better positioned in consultant and CIO conversations over the next three to five years.

---

## 2. Private Markets Pacing Is Being Compressed

Across virtually every plan in the sample, private markets remain below target allocation, and boards are actively accelerating pacing. **DPFP ($3B, TX)** is running a $148 million gap to its PE target and has committed to four to six new investments in 2026. **IPERS ($41B, IA)** approved a $2.025 billion commitment plan for the year. **LACERA ($75B, CA)** executed $1.8 billion in a single month. This is not opportunistic — it is a structural catch-up driven by the denominator effect unwinding and boards facing pressure to deploy.

The critical observation is *how* plans are deploying: co-investments and secondaries now represent a systematic allocation preference, not a tactical play. **CalPERS ($502B, CA)** reports 41% of first-half FY2026 PE commitments went through no- or low-fee vehicles. The CIA (Customized Investment Account) structure returned **25.1%** on a one-year basis versus **12.9%** for traditional buyouts. These are not marginal differences — they are reshaping fee expectations across the LP base.

**Practical implication:** The window for traditional commingled fund economics at the largest plans is narrowing. Plans under $10B will follow in two to three years as co-investment access improves.

---

## 3. The Credit Cycle Is Driving Mandate Creation

Private credit is showing up across plans that never had a formal allocation — **IPERS** (still below target), **Colorado PERA ($60B, CO)** (new asset class created January 2025 at 6% target), **LACERS ($8B, CA)** evaluating multi-asset credit. The macro backdrop explains why: the Fed holding at 3.50–3.75% while PCE remains stuck around 3.1% means real rates are positive, and credit spreads remain attractive relative to historical norms. Plans are locking in yields now.

More telling is the number of simultaneous credit manager searches. DPFP has a second Multi-Asset Credit search planned for later in 2026. IPERS ran an absolute return RFP and hired three managers — **Florin Court Capital**, **CFM SA** (CFM ISTEC Program), and **AHL Partners** (AHL Cat Bond Program). The convergence of credit and alternatives mandates reflects genuine uncertainty about whether these allocations belong in fixed income, absolute return, or their own bucket — a definitional debate CIOs are actively navigating.

---

## 4. Consultant Fee Validation Is Becoming a Competitive Pressure Point

**KPPA's ($20B, KY)** Albourne hire for fee validation services — covering since-inception validation at $2,000 per fund across 7,000+ investments and $200 billion in validated client assets — signals an institutional shift toward systematic fee auditing. Combined with CalPERS' explicit preference for co-investments and CIAs on fee grounds, and LACERS' audit finding about passive acceptance of custodian data without independent validation, 2026 represents a genuine inflection point in how institutional investors think about total cost of ownership.

The Albourne win over **bFinance** (contingent 35% of savings fee) and **XTP Implementation Services** (success-based) on the basis of existing relationships and analytical depth is instructive: plans are choosing scale and expertise over incentive alignment in fee oversight, signaling that fee auditing is transitioning from a one-time exercise to an ongoing operational function.

**Practical implication:** GPs who have relied on fee complexity as a revenue source should anticipate a more scrutinized environment — particularly on management fee offsets, deal fees, and monitoring agreements.

---

## 5. Macro Stagflation Risk Is Entering Board Discussions

The Q1 2026 data — S&P 500 down **4.3%** YTD, Russell 3000 down **4.0%**, Fed pausing for a second consecutive meeting, PCE at **3.1%**, GDP slowing to approximately **0.7%** — is creating a genuine stagflationary undertone surfacing in multiple board rooms. **TMRS ($40B, TX)** is reviewing its **6.75%** assumed rate of return, already below peer Texas plans at 7.0–7.25%. **DPFP** reported a total fund return of **-2.41%** through March 31, 2026.

The international developed equity divergence is particularly notable: MSCI World ex-US declined only **0.9%** versus U.S. indices down 4%+. Plans with underweights to international developed equity — common given U.S. outperformance through 2024 — may face uncomfortable rebalancing conversations. **IPERS'** strong CY2025 international equity return (**33.21%**) and **LACERA's** natural resources performance (**17.8%** one-year, driven by gold and industrial metals) suggest that commodity-sensitive and non-U.S. exposures are providing genuine diversification precisely when it is needed.

---

## 6. AI Is Moving from Policy to Procurement

**LACERA** adopted a formal AI Policy (March 2026, 8-0 vote), deployed Microsoft Copilot organization-wide, and is building AI applications including investment analysis tools and a member-facing chatbot. **SIB-ND** has an explicit AI roadmap targeting eventual investment recommendations. **Colorado PERA** identified AI in investments as a priority trustee education topic for 2026.

This is no longer aspirational. Plans are making procurement decisions. The question for the next 12–18 months is whether AI tools for investment analysis will be built in-house, licensed from custodians and consultants, or sourced from specialist vendors. The first-mover advantage belongs to plans with the internal technical capacity to validate AI outputs — a capability gap that will take years to close at most public plans.

---

## 7. Governance Risk Is Underappreciated

Several audit findings in the period deserve CIO attention as early warning indicators: **LACERS** receiving findings on passive custodian data acceptance without independent validation; **Colorado PERA** identifying five real estate properties exceeding leverage limits (one at **76.16%**), two proxy voting errors, and approximately **750 proxy votes missed** over three years during a Northern Trust account transition; **PSERS ($73B, PA)** navigating simultaneous consultant and actuarial vendor transitions following the Verus/Cerity merger.

These are operational risk events, not investment risk events — but they occur in environments where CIOs are simultaneously deploying more capital into more complex structures with more manager relationships. The succession policy **LACERS** adopted (Executive Officer → AGM → CIO) is a governance best practice increasingly relevant as investment teams expand and delegated authority frameworks grow.

---

## Overall Read

2026 is a year of deliberate structural change, not reactive repositioning. Plans that are ahead of the curve — on total portfolio approaches, co-investments, private credit allocation, and AI tooling — are pulling further ahead. Plans still debating asset class targets and relying on traditional GP relationships face a widening governance and performance gap. The macro environment is providing a natural stress test, and the next 12 months will reveal which structural changes are genuinely embedded versus aspirational.
